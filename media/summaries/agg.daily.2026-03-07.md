Meesho disclosed a tax demand of ₹1,499.73 crore for assessment year 2023–24, issued by the Income Tax Department under sections 143(3) and 156. The company has contested the adjustments, asserting legal grounds for appeal, and anticipates no material impact on its financials or operations. A prior demand for FY22 remains stayed by the Karnataka High Court and is under active consideration. Despite a significant increase in Q3 losses—rising to ₹491 crore from ₹37.4 crore in the same period the previous year—revenue grew by 32%.

Digital ad impressions have increased more than fivefold since 2021 and by 2.5 times compared to 2024, with Q4 2025 showing a 15% rise from Q1. Services, education, and personal accessories accounted for the highest share of impressions, while online shopping led in category volume at 12%. The top five advertisers—Flipkart, Amazon, Adobe India, Hindustan Unilever, and Reliance Retail—accounted for 15% of total impressions, and over 120,000 advertisers operated exclusively online. Instagram dominated web publishers with 65% of impressions, and programmatic delivery accounted for 95.8% of ad views.

Mangalore Refinery and Petrochemicals Limited (MRPL) denied reports of operational disruptions, clarifying that no production halt had occurred. The company stated that its operations remained unaffected by recent market conditions, and all facilities were functioning within normal parameters.

Brent crude futures were observed at $92, with WTI near $91. Peter McGuire, a veteran investor, projected a potential rise to $100 within a week due to escalating tensions involving Iran, with a further climb to $120–$130 possible if conflict persists. The conflict was noted to pose risks to LPG and crude imports, particularly from Russia, potentially increasing premiums and restricting industrial gas availability. Short-term impacts were expected to be limited if hostilities remained under a month, though prolonged conflict could reduce private capital expenditure, strain production, and heighten inflationary pressures, despite continued positive growth momentum.

Captain Fresh, a Bengaluru-based packaged seafood startup, completed the acquisition of PrimeSAU (Frime), Spain’s leading sustainable tuna processor, with €180 million in annual revenue and operations across 33 countries. The acquisition was structured to expand Captain Fresh’s multi-species portfolio, enhance European processing capabilities, and enable cross-market distribution across the US, Europe, and emerging economies. PrimeSAU’s Barcelona plant, recently upgraded with a €50 million investment, was confirmed to be independent of the acquisition. Both leadership teams emphasized cultural alignment between family-run enterprises and the strategic intent to become a one-stop diversified seafood provider.

InvestorAI’s CEO described the firm’s proprietary AI models, developed using custom computer vision and data pipelines, as distinct from off-the-shelf large language models. The platform delivers buy-sell recommendations and timing signals, offering pre-constructed, diversified investment baskets for intraday, short-term, and multi-week horizons, with automated execution and stop-loss protection. With a lean research team of five, the firm claims superior efficiency and higher short-term returns compared to traditional asset management setups. The CEO maintains human oversight for long-term decisions. Since 2017, the platform has grown to approximately 30,000 users, with annual user growth rates of 100–200%, and a 65% retention rate among paying subscribers. The CEO views India’s demographic dividend as a 20-year bull market and anticipates consolidation in the wealth-tech sector, positioning InvestorAI for global scalability.

Amazon India’s Saheli program entered into a Memorandum of Understanding with the Army Women Welfare Association during the ElevateHER 2026 event in Bengaluru. The initiative aims to facilitate structured onboarding, capability building, and nationwide customer access for women from army families seeking to launch sustainable businesses on Amazon’s marketplace, extending Amazon’s broader inclusion strategy for veterans.

In a CNBC interview, Carolyn Dawson of Founders Forum noted a shift in Indian startups from scale-first models to deep-tech innovation, with AI recognized as a transformative force rather than a speculative bubble. Founders are now under greater pressure to demonstrate profitability and capital efficiency, particularly in biotech, robotics, and hardware-intensive sectors, while maintaining long-term resilience. Investors continue to support deep-tech ventures with patience, emphasizing sustained returns. AI is seen as a co-pilot to human talent, prompting firms to recruit younger, AI-fluent employees. India’s growing global recognition is attributed not only to its large market size but also to advancements in research, manufacturing, and innovation.

Stride Ventures, India’s first venture-debt platform backed by Saudi Arabia’s Public Investment Fund (PIF), was highlighted as a sign of increasing global confidence in Indian-origin investment funds and the maturation of the venture-debt asset class. Since its 2019 launch, Stride has deployed $1.6 billion across approximately 200 companies, including 20 unicorns, through seven multi-currency funds. The PIF partnership is expected to support a $500 million GCC-focused push and accelerate the firm’s target of $1 billion in new deployments over the next two years. Stride maintains a sector-agnostic approach with a leaning toward consumer, fintech, and climate-tech, providing non-dilutive capital for working capital, operating expenses, acquisitions, and pre-IPO needs. AI is viewed as an enabler rather than a standalone disruptor, with investments focused on companies integrating AI to enhance operational efficiency.
