Kotak Mahindra Q4 Earnings: Strong Profit Growth and Strategic Highlights

KOTAKBANK HDFCAMC

Kotak Mahindra Bank reported a 13% YoY rise in consolidated profit (₹5,238 cr) and a 17% QoQ increase in net profit (₹4,027 cr) for Q4 FY26, driven by higher income, cost discipline, and sharply lower credit costs (39 bps vs 63 bps in Q3). Net interest income grew 4% QoQ with a NIM of 4.67%, and fee income rose 9% QoQ. Deposits expanded 15% YoY, CASA reached 43.3%, and advances grew 16% YoY with stable unsecured mix. Asset quality improved, with NPA falling to 0.25% and provision coverage above 79%. The bank’s cost‑to‑asset ratio fell to 2.73% and ROE stood at 11.1% on a full‑year profit of ₹14,000 cr. Growth was underpinned by four focus segments: high‑net‑worth (private banking and Solitaire, managing ₹10.8  lakh cr), Core India (Kotak 811 adding 2.5–3  lakh customers monthly), SME lending, and Institutional services. Subsidiary performance was robust: Kotak Securities (PAT ₹400 cr, 13.5% market share), Kotak PIME (PAT ₹240 cr), Kotak AMC (PAT ₹184 cr, AUM up 22%), and Kotak Life (PAT ₹90 cr, VNB up 31%). Group AUM reached ₹7.47  lakh cr and net worth ₹1.81  lakh cr. The bank remains resilient to geopolitical risks and continues to invest ~13% of OPEX in technology

Sector Rotation & Portfolio Balancing: Navigating Thematic Calls in a Range‑Bound Market

DHUNINV

In this episode of “Let’s Talk Money,” host Surabhi Upadhyay and guests Rohit Matu, Sachin Reliker (Access Mutual Fund) and Nitin Balera (Zeus Investments) dissect the emerging practice of sector rotation and its relevance for retail investors. They explain that while macro‑driven narratives (geopolitics, policy reforms, energy security) continually shift, the core investment framework remains: target sectors with large, expanding opportunity sets and pick companies with competitive edges. 

Key takeaways:

  • Sector selection hinges on macro tailwinds, policy support and industry size; current top bets include industrial/capital goods, consumer internet, consumer discretionary, financial services and industrial metals.
  • Portfolio allocation for a moderate‑to‑aggressive investor should be modest—roughly 10‑15 % of total assets in sectoral bets, monitored closely to avoid over‑concentration.
  • Sector‑rotation funds aim to capture momentum across 6‑7 leading micro‑economic sectors out of 12, with medium churn and a diversified stock base (≈60‑70 stocks). They differ from pure thematic funds by allowing dynamic re‑weighting and professional rebalancing, reducing tax and transaction costs for investors.
  • Risk management: sector funds are higher‑risk, higher‑return products; investors must align them with risk tolerance, maintain a long‑term horizon (≥3 years), and accept that under‑performing sectors may be trimmed quickly.
  • Investor guidance: most retail investors should rely on professional fund managers for tactical sector moves, rather than chasing recent outperformance (“euphoria buying”). Proper risk profiling and disciplined exposure limits are essential to harness sectoral alpha while mitigating downside

Anthropic’s “Mythos” AI Model Triggers Nationwide Cybersecurity Alarm

HDFCBANK

The launch of Anthropic’s advanced AI model “Mythos,” capable of rapidly analysing and fixing complex codebases, has raised alarms across India’s financial, power, telecom, and critical infrastructure sectors. Experts on a panel—including former IPS officer Nandkumar Sarawade, NPCI executive Nitin Mishra, HDFC Bank’s Ramesh Naqshminarayanan, and Spark founder Kapil Vaswani—described the threat as a “day‑zero” vulnerability surge that compresses the gap between discovery and exploitation from years to hours. While existing risks have long existed, Mythos accelerates exploitation, demanding a shift from traditional patch‑and‑react models to AI‑driven defence, resilient system design, and continuous red‑team testing. Key recommendations include: rapid patching, deploying defensive AI tools, adopting micro‑segmentation and white‑box cryptography, building domestic security talent, establishing sector‑specific governance committees, and fostering public‑private collaboration for shared threat intelligence. Open‑source approaches are favoured for transparency, but require skilled staff. The panel urges immediate budget allocation, board‑level oversight, asset inventory, and coordinated industry platforms to mitigate the looming cyber‑risk landscape
