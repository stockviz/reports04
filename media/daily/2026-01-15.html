MOSL’s Decision To Stay Invested In AU SFB | In Conversation With Raamdeo Agrwal & Sanjay Agarwal 2026-01-15 12:35

HDFCBANK AUBANK KOTAKBANK

The interview centers on MOSL’s continued commitment to AU Small Finance Bank, with Raamdeo Agrwal and Sanjay Agarwal discussing how technology, succession planning, and long‑term vision shape the bank’s future.

Key take‑aways

  1. AI as an enabler – AI‑driven customer interaction removes language barriers and lets CEOs focus on scaling the business rather than operational minutiae.

  2. Leadership succession – As a first‑generation entrepreneur, Sanjay stresses that a founder must eventually step aside. Regulators encourage a dual‑CEO/MD structure to ensure institutional continuity beyond any single individual.

  3. Growth beyond lending – The real opportunity now lies in non‑lending financial services—wealth management, asset‑management, insurance, and capital‑market platforms. India’s domestic savings are projected to exceed $50 trillion in the next two decades, creating a massive demand for competent asset managers.

  4. Building scalable institutions – Success should be measured in centuries, not just years. A robust team, strong governance, and transparent processes are essential for creating an enduring financial institution.

  5. Entrepreneurial mindset – Entrepreneurs must adopt a long‑term, patient outlook (20 + years), focus on capital preservation, and understand that success is cumulative—each win builds the next.

  6. Education gap – Current Indian business education does not nurture true entrepreneurship; practical, experience‑based learning is needed rather than placement‑focused curricula.

Overall, the conversation underscores that AI, thoughtful succession, and a shift toward asset‑management services will drive AU SFB’s next phase of growth, while a culture of long‑term stewardship and better entrepreneurial education will support the broader Indian financial ecosystem.

The Story Behind AU Small Finance Bank| In Conversation With Raamdeo Agrawal & Sanjay Agarwal 2026-01-15 11:30

AUBANK

The conversation recounts the origins and growth of AU Small Finance Bank, focusing on the roles of private‑equity investor Motilal Oswal (represented by Ramdeo Agrawal) and founder‑entrepreneur Sanjay Agarwal.

Key points

  • Early investment: Motilal Oswal’s private‑equity arm first invested in AU Small Finance around 2008‑2009 after a brief due‑diligence check that hinged on the company’s existing line of credit from HDFC Bank (≈ ₹200 cr). The firm was already profitable (≈ ₹2 cr net profit) and had a disciplined, tight‑operating model.

  • Capital commitment and exit: The PE fund initially put in about ₹30‑40 cr, later adding more capital in 2010. Over the next decade they stayed invested, gradually selling the stake. The final exit valued AU at a market‑cap of roughly ₹5,000 cr, delivering a ~33‑times return on Motilal’s capital (not the 100‑times return sometimes claimed).

  • Founder’s background: Sanjay Agarwal, a chartered accountant from the 1990s, tried several ventures before focusing on financial services. He built AU Small Finance as a niche, profit‑driven lender, initially as a non‑bank financial company (NBFC) with a strong partnership model with HDFC.

  • Ramdeo’s vision: Agrawal saw AU as a “next HDFC” – a private‑sector lender that could capture the migration of credit from public to private hands. He praised the company’s risk discipline (no unsecured loans), tight operations, and strong leadership.

  • Growth narrative: AU’s business model emphasized high‑quality lending, robust risk management, and the ability to deploy large volumes of capital in a market projected to sustain 14‑15 % credit growth for decades. This gave the firm a massive runway despite its modest start (₹70 cr market cap at entry).

  • Lessons for investors: The discussion highlighted the importance of backing profitable, well‑run founders; verifying credibility through trusted partners (e.g., HDFC’s endorsement); focusing on disciplined capital deployment; and seeking sectors where structural tailwinds (private‑sector credit expansion) can drive long‑term value.

Overall, the dialogue underscores how early, patient capital combined with strong governance and a clear growth thesis turned a small NBFC into a high‑valued small‑finance bank, rewarding its early investors with substantial (though not 100‑X) multiples.

Why Nominal GDP, Not Real Growth, Is The Real Budget 2026 Challenge: Top Economists Share View 2026-01-15 10:32

KRBL BEL

The discussion centers on India’s fiscal and growth outlook. Speakers note recent budget measures—labor‑intensive export focus, high customs duties, a textile PLI, removal of QCO on VSF, and FTAs—that signal progress, but stress the need for further tariff reductions and a clear roadmap for fiscal consolidation and capex expansion over the next three‑to‑four years. They reference existing targets (FI31, debt‑to‑GDP) and call for explicit tax‑to‑GDP goals. Regarding the US‑India trade deal, participants argue it is not pivotal to India’s growth forecasts; recent US tariff disputes appear to be easing, and ongoing US tech investments (Intel, Tata Electronics, Apple, Google, Microsoft, Amazon) suggest India’s integration into the global hardware ecosystem regardless of the deal. Concerns about tariffs on Iranian and Russian oil affect specific firms, but given India’s large workforce and $4 trillion economy, the overall impact on macro growth is limited. The speakers conclude by expressing confidence in the policy direction and inviting continued monitoring.
