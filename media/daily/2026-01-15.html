Wadhwani AI: Solving India’s Biggest Challenges in Health, Agriculture & Education | N18M

Summary

  • Founding & Mission – Badwani AI, launched in 2018 by brothers Romesh and Sunil Badwani, is a non‑profit institute that builds population‑scale AI solutions for India’s public health, agriculture and education systems. Its primary goal is social impact; AI is the enabling tool.

  • Approach – Solutions are problem‑first, not technology‑first. The institute co‑creates with governments, donors and frontline workers, embedding AI directly into national digital platforms (e.g., TB Mukt Bharat, NIKSHAY, Isanjivni, CDAC’s Sanjivni). Iterative data collection and trust‑building are central to deployment.

  • Culture – Guided by four pillars: Serve, Care, Learn, Communicate. Staff are recruited for empathy, humility and a service mindset, ensuring human‑in‑the‑loop oversight of AI systems.

  • Building Trust – Early challenges included explaining AI to farmers and avoiding a “high‑brow” approach. Trust is earned by showing genuine care, taking responsibility for user errors, and delivering reliable, culturally appropriate tools.

  • Cultural & Linguistic Adaptation – All solutions are built for India’s multilingual reality (≈14‑15 languages). Field teams live in villages, conduct user research, and design “prescriptive” interfaces that accommodate low literacy and first‑time smartphone users.

  • Key Deployments

    • Health: TB treatment adherence prediction, vulnerability mapping, infant‑measurement video analytics, diabetic retinopathy screening, integration with NIKSHAY and Sanjivni.
    • Agriculture: National pest‑surveillance system (initially cotton, now multi‑crop), Kisan Mitra grievance management, AI‑assisted farmer call‑center, grain‑quality analyzer.
    • Education: Oral‑read‑fluency tool rolled out in multiple states, vision for AI‑guided foundational learning from age 3 onward.

  • Human‑in‑Command Philosophy – AI is always overseen by trained humans because residual error rates, however low, can be catastrophic for individuals.

  • Strategic Outlook – Badwani AI will host a pre‑summit to showcase its work ahead of the India AI Impact Summit 2026, positioning India as a global leader in multilingual, large‑scale AI for social good.

  • Future Vision & Legacy – The institute aims to embed trustworthy AI into India’s public fabric over the next 20 years, ultimately making AI a universally trusted tool that prevents problems (e.g., early childhood learning gaps) rather than merely solving them.

Supreme Court To Hear ED’s Case For Probe Into ‘Obstruction’ Of I-PAC Raids By Mamata Banerjee 2026-01-15 13:35

EFCIL

The Supreme Court is hearing the Enforcement Directorate’s (ED) case that the West Bengal government, led by Chief Minister Mamata Banerjee, obstructed raids on the political consultancy I‑PAC.

ED’s allegations
* The raids, part of a ₹2,742‑crore multi‑state money‑laundering probe, were carried out under Section 17 of the PMLA without prior notice to the state police.
* Banerjee, the Kolkata Commissioner of Police and other senior officials entered the premises, seized officers’ phones and documents, and allegedly tried to hide or destroy evidence.
* The ED claims this amounts to theft and intimidation, warning that such interference could discourage central agencies from doing their job.

TMC’s defence
* The party says the raids targeted confidential election‑related data belonging to I‑PAC, which was being used by the Trinamool Congress for upcoming West Bengal elections.
* It argues the ED’s action was a political ploy to obtain party strategy documents and that the state was not properly informed of the operation.
* Senior counsel Kapil Sibal contends that the ED’s timing—just before elections—suggests ulterior motives, and that the seized material was election‑related, not evidence of money‑laundering.

Legal context
* Under the PMLA, the ED can conduct searches without notifying the accused or local police, but it may request police assistance for security.
* The ED maintains it did not seize any material; it only took possession of items it believed were being hidden.
* The Supreme Court noted the seriousness of the alleged obstruction, the disruption of court proceedings, and the need to examine whether central agencies can operate freely in the state.

Key questions pending
1. Was there genuine evidence of money‑laundering linked to I‑PAC, or was the raid a data‑theft attempt?
2. Did Banerjee’s intervention constitute illegal obstruction or a lawful exercise of state authority?
3. How will the court balance the ED’s investigative powers against the state’s claim of protecting confidential electoral information?

Overall, the conversation underscores that AI, thoughtful succession, and a shift toward asset‑management services will drive AU SFB’s next phase of growth, while a culture of long‑term stewardship and better entrepreneurial education will support the broader Indian financial ecosystem.

The Story Behind AU Small Finance Bank| In Conversation With Raamdeo Agrawal & Sanjay Agarwal 2026-01-15 11:30

AUBANK

The conversation recounts the origins and growth of AU Small Finance Bank, focusing on the roles of private‑equity investor Motilal Oswal (represented by Ramdeo Agrawal) and founder‑entrepreneur Sanjay Agarwal.

Key points

  • Early investment: Motilal Oswal’s private‑equity arm first invested in AU Small Finance around 2008‑2009 after a brief due‑diligence check that hinged on the company’s existing line of credit from HDFC Bank (≈ ₹200 cr). The firm was already profitable (≈ ₹2 cr net profit) and had a disciplined, tight‑operating model.

  • Capital commitment and exit: The PE fund initially put in about ₹30‑40 cr, later adding more capital in 2010. Over the next decade they stayed invested, gradually selling the stake. The final exit valued AU at a market‑cap of roughly ₹5,000 cr, delivering a ~33‑times return on Motilal’s capital (not the 100‑times return sometimes claimed).

  • Founder’s background: Sanjay Agarwal, a chartered accountant from the 1990s, tried several ventures before focusing on financial services. He built AU Small Finance as a niche, profit‑driven lender, initially as a non‑bank financial company (NBFC) with a strong partnership model with HDFC.

  • Ramdeo’s vision: Agrawal saw AU as a “next HDFC” – a private‑sector lender that could capture the migration of credit from public to private hands. He praised the company’s risk discipline (no unsecured loans), tight operations, and strong leadership.

  • Growth narrative: AU’s business model emphasized high‑quality lending, robust risk management, and the ability to deploy large volumes of capital in a market projected to sustain 14‑15 % credit growth for decades. This gave the firm a massive runway despite its modest start (₹70 cr market cap at entry).

  • Lessons for investors: The discussion highlighted the importance of backing profitable, well‑run founders; verifying credibility through trusted partners (e.g., HDFC’s endorsement); focusing on disciplined capital deployment; and seeking sectors where structural tailwinds (private‑sector credit expansion) can drive long‑term value.

Overall, the dialogue underscores how early, patient capital combined with strong governance and a clear growth thesis turned a small NBFC into a high‑valued small‑finance bank, rewarding its early investors with substantial (though not 100‑X) multiples.
