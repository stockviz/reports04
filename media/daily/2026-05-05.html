The Great Global Talent Divide: Shifts in Migration, Skill Demand, and AI’s Role

IEX TATACAP CGPOWER

In a Future of Work Summit 2026 panel, leaders debated how talent flows are changing from the classic “go where the opportunity is” model to a more complex, geographically‑anchored reality. Panelists highlighted India’s persistent net outflow of skilled workers (≈30 k annually) despite growing tier‑2 hubs, and the emerging “Ghar‑vapasi” trend where talent returns or is attracted back by government incentives and expanding local ecosystems. They noted that countries worldwide are now competing for scarce skills, positioning talent as a new geopolitical currency, with the UK‑India Free Trade Agreement introducing chapters on business mobility, youth visas, and joint university campuses to foster bilateral talent circulation. In the semiconductor arena, India boasts strong design talent but still lacks manufacturing depth; recent government‑backed fabs and university labs aim to close this gap. Regarding AI, speakers agreed it won’t eliminate jobs outright but will displace specific skill sets, making continuous up‑skilling essential. The rise of Global Capability Centers (GCCs) further underscores the need for adaptable talent pipelines as de‑globalisation pressures reshape where and how skilled workers are sourced. Overall, the discussion underscored that talent attraction, retention, and reskilling are now central to national economic strategy
