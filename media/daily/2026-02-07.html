Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal On India-US Interim Trade Deal | India & US Trade Deal | CNBC TV18

Summary

  • Context: Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal addressed a press conference on the India‑U.S. interim trade agreement, fielding questions from journalists and industry representatives.

  • Key Elements of the Interim Deal

    • Tariff Reductions: Zero‑duty or reduced‑tariff treatment for a wide range of U.S. products, including spices, coffee, coconut oil, various nuts (cashew, Brazil peanut, chestnut, etc.), fruits (avocado, banana, mango, pineapple, etc.), mushrooms, processed foods (jams, pastes), seeds (canary, sesame, poppy), generic drugs, pharmaceuticals, automotive parts, aircraft components, machinery, chemicals, minerals, precious metals, essential oils, and luxury items such as watches and chandeliers.
    • Quota‑Based Measures: Some sectors will operate on quota‑based access rather than universal duty elimination; the extent depends on Indian domestic production capacity and market needs.
    • Critical Minerals & Rare Earths: The agreement emphasizes cooperation on critical minerals, with the U.S. and India working through the “Pak Silica” initiative to develop alternative sources and resilient supply chains, especially after China’s export restrictions.
    • Digital Trade: No discriminatory barriers were identified; any emerging non‑tariff issues will be jointly addressed.
    • Health & Safety Standards: Exports to the U.S. must meet FDA standards, while imports must comply with Indian regulations (FSSAI, etc.); quality assurance is emphasized.

  • Specific Questions & Answers

    • Apple & DDGS Limits: Import limits will be quota‑based where necessary.
    • Aircraft Parts: Boeing and Airbus already source significant components from India; the interim deal is expected to boost this trade.
    • Auto Parts: Anticipated zero‑duty value for auto‑parts exports is around $40–45 billion.
    • Precious Metals: The government does not dictate purchases but will support imports if they are competitively priced.
    • Strawberries & Pesticides: Exporters must meet both Indian and U.S. safety standards; quality compliance is mandatory.
    • Monitoring of Russian Oil Purchases: The Indian Foreign Ministry will respond to U.S. statements on this issue.

  • Overall Outlook: The interim agreement is framed as a calibrated opening of Indian markets to U.S. goods, aiming to deepen bilateral trade, diversify supply chains, and strengthen strategic partnership across multiple sectors.

Fixing India’s Hospitals: How SuperHealth Is Bringing Transparency, Trust & Profits To Healthcare

APOLLO M&M OLAELEC NH

SuperHealth’s founders—Varun Dubey (ex‑Practo, Ola, Nvidia), Dr. Alex (radiologist, former Apollo/Narayana exec), and Manoj (tech/operations veteran)—set out to fix India’s chronic hospital problems: opaque pricing, unreliable appointments, profit‑driven doctor incentives, and exorbitant construction costs.

They argue that the “original sin” of healthcare is its high delivery cost (land, construction, devices, medicines, staff). Traditional chains absorb massive upfront capital (e.g., costly land purchases) and then recover it by over‑charging patients, using doctors as salespeople, and varying prices by bed class or test type.

SuperHealth’s model tackles this by:

  • Leasing land and using rapid, low‑cost construction to cut per‑bed cost from ~₹2 cr to ~₹0.7 cr.
  • Offering fixed‑salary, equity‑based doctor compensation with no sales quotas, eliminating the doctor‑as‑salesperson dynamic.
  • Providing transparent, flat‑rate pricing that doesn’t change after admission, with no hidden fees for room class, extra days, or test variations.
  • Designing hospitals for efficiency (e.g., integrated CSSD, private rooms) to improve safety and eliminate wait times—99.5 % of appointments start on time.
  • Delivering high‑quality, multi‑specialty care in a 50‑bed “hyper‑local” Bengaluru hospital that has been operational for four months, receiving strong patient satisfaction.

Funding comes mainly from angel investors and local ecosystem backers (including Mahindra‑Singh Dhoni’s office), attracted by the universal mistrust even wealthy patients have in existing hospitals. The team plans to replicate the model across India, open‑source their operational processes, and prove that a profitable, patient‑centric hospital chain is scalable.

Creating More, Remembering Less: Decoding the Present & Future of Creativity | Storyboard 18

3MINDIA BOMDYEING PRESSMN BAJAJ-AUTO

Storyboard 18 – “Creating More, Remembering Less” – Key Takeaways

  • Context & Jury – The episode launches the inaugural Storyboard 18 Awards, introducing a grand jury of India’s top creative, cultural and brand leaders (e.g., Amitabh Kant, Prasoon Joshi, Suhail Seth, etc.) who will judge lasting creativity.

  • Memory vs. Volume – Hosts highlight how ads from childhood stick because they were seen collectively on linear TV, with repeated exposure and a shared cultural moment. Modern advertising—instant, algorithm‑driven, short‑lived—creates far more content but leaves fewer memorable impressions.

  • Nostalgic Examples

    • Denaifa Doolani cites “Dubara Mat Puttna” for its pure stickiness.
    • Ashish Kazanchi recalls the Kelvinator “dentures‑chattering” ad, Nirma, Happy Dent, and other jingle‑driven classics that stopped people in their tracks.

  • Current Landscape – Panelists agree that great work still exists (e.g., recent Adidas film, “Sasa Lele”), but its lifespan in the cultural zeitgeist is dramatically shorter. Turn‑around times have collapsed from weeks to days/hours.

  • AI’s Role

    • Neville Shah (Kynet) sees AI as a speed‑up for repetitive, execution‑heavy tasks (image search, mood‑board generation) but not a replacement for idea generation. The creative spark—empathy, nuance, business insight—remains uniquely human.
    • Ashish Kazanchi likens AI to cheaper, more accessible production tools (e.g., affordable Arri cameras). The tool doesn’t remove the need for strategists or creators; it only expands the means of execution.
    • Sachin Kamle (Leo India) treats AI as a utility that frees time for deeper craft, while also exploring larger societal solutions (smart‑farm, AI‑driven animation pipelines). He stresses that AI should augment, not dominate, creative thinking.

  • Why Good Ads Fade

    1. Lack of Longevity Strategy – Brands no longer commit to multi‑year campaigns; without sustained exposure, even successful ads lose cultural resonance.
    2. Attention Fragmentation – Audiences are split across screens and platforms, diluting any single message’s impact.
    3. Generational Context – An ad that resonates with one cohort may be invisible to another; lasting recall now often depends on cross‑generational storytelling.

  • Desire to Revive Classics

    • Neville would resurrect Kelvinator’s “cold‑est‑thing” concept with fresh renditions.
    • Sachin prefers not to tamper with gold‑standard ads but notes successful modern reinterpretations (e.g., Cadbury’s gender‑role‑reversed cricket ad, Bajaj’s refreshed “Hamara Bajaj”).
    • Ashish would love to remake the simple, punchy “fishermen” ad, believing its premise still holds power.

  • Overall Insight – Creativity is not vanishing; it is evolving. The challenge for today’s creators is to blend rapid, AI‑enabled production with human‑centric storytelling that can cut through the noise and achieve lasting recall, even if that recall now spans shorter cycles or specific audience segments.

Impact of U.S. Tariff Changes on India’s Russian Oil Imports: Insights from MK Surana

MK Surana, former CMD of HPCL, explains that India cannot instantly halt Russian crude purchases; contracts are typically signed months in advance, so any reduction will be gradual. While overall Russian oil imports have declined, they are not yet zero, and one Indian refinery still relies entirely on Russian crude. The U.S. executive order reducing tariffs to 25 % and warning of reinstating duties if India resumes buying Russian oil separates the tariff on Indian goods from the broader trade deal, making them distinct issues. Surana notes that the order targets “directly or indirectly” Russian-origin oil, and the practical effect will depend on how India restructures its supply chain and navigates existing contracts. The situation is viewed as a temporary, contextual adjustment rather than a permanent shift.

India–US Trade Deal Pact | Market Experts Decode Key Sectors Set To Benefit

GOKEX FORCEMOT

India‑US Trade Deal – Key Take‑aways

  • Deal Overview
    • The agreement lifts many US tariffs on Indian goods, expanding bilateral trade from the current $45‑46 bn to a target of $120 bn by year 5.
    • It covers farm products, chemicals, textiles, aircraft parts, auto ancillaries, organic chemicals, and a few niche items (e.g., wine & spirits).
    • The pact is seen as a “done deal” and aligns with the recently signed India‑EU agreement, giving India parity with neighboring exporters.
  • Market Sentiment
    • Analysts (Nishal Maheshwari, Peter Maguire, Dr. A. Shaktival) view the announcement as expected, with no major surprises.
    • Emerging‑market funds, previously underweight India, are likely to re‑enter quickly.
    • Overall sentiment: bullish for Indian equities, especially in the sectors directly affected.
  • Sector‑Specific Impact
    • Chemicals – Biggest beneficiary; US tariffs of 50 % are removed, unlocking a stagnant market.
    • Textiles & Apparel – Immediate upside; US exports currently $5.25 bn, projected 30 % rise in 4 months and 40 % in a year. Bangladesh’s supply‑chain issues are shifting orders to India.
    • Aircraft & Aircraft Parts – Export push expected under the new framework.
    • Auto Ancillaries – Companies like Bharat Force stand to gain from eased access.
    • Energy – Anticipated lower crude prices as US‑Venezuela flows increase and Russian oil discounts vanish; cheaper energy should curb Indian inflation.
    • Food & Agri‑products – Still pending detailed rules, but inclusion signals longer‑term upside.
  • Potential Risks
    • Trade Deficit – Opening markets could widen the US‑India deficit if export growth lags import growth; however, experts stress that building sales and market share outweighs short‑term balance‑sheet concerns.
    • Make‑in‑India – No significant threat; the deal is expected to complement domestic manufacturing by expanding export markets.
    • Human‑resource Constraints – Particularly in textile scaling, labour mobilisation may be a bottleneck.
  • Stock‑level Outlook (short‑term, Monday’s open)
    • Apparel exporters – e.g., Gokuldas Exports (high US exposure).
    • Chemical producers – firms with sizeable US sales pipelines.
    • Auto ancillary firms – Bharat Force and similar exporters.
    • Other beneficiaries – Companies in aircraft parts, organic chemicals, and niche GPU‑related tech (e.g., NetWeb/A2E).
  • Medium‑term (6–12 months)
    • Continued strength in textiles, chemicals, auto ancillaries, and aerospace parts as the US market fully integrates the tariff concessions.
    • Gains will be reinforced by the EU‑India deal slated for next year, creating a broader “dual‑market” advantage.
    • Earnings growth, not just sentiment, will drive stock performance once the initial rally settles.
  • Strategic Context
    • The pact serves both economic and geopolitical aims: deepening US‑India ties, diversifying supply chains away from China (“China‑plus‑one”), and securing energy supplies at lower cost.
    • It positions India as a preferred alternative for US brands seeking stable, cost‑effective sourcing.

Bottom line: The India‑US trade agreement is largely as anticipated and is set to boost exports in chemicals, textiles, aircraft parts, and auto ancillaries while keeping inflation in check via cheaper energy.
