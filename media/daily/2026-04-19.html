RBI’s New Asset‑Size‑Based Framework for Upper‑Layer NBFCs and Its Implications

RECLTD BAJAJFINSV IRFC PFC NSIL

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is revamping the classification of “upper‑layer” non‑bank financial companies (NBFCs) by replacing the earlier composite scoring system with a simple asset‑size threshold of INR 1 lakh crore. All NBFCs—including public‑sector and government‑owned entities such as REC, PFC, IRFC, and other PSUs—will be deemed upper‑layer if they cross this limit, triggering mandatory listing within three years and stricter supervision (higher capital, liquidity buffers, and transparency).

Key discussion points:
- Inclusion of PSUs: Previously excluded, PSUs now face the same rules, raising concerns about sector‑specific exposure limits for entities like REC and PFC that predominantly finance power assets.
- Tata Sons / CICs: Tata Sons, classified as a core investment company (CIC), would fall under the new rule if its assets exceed the threshold; however, its status as an NBFC remains contested. If deemed an NBFC, it must list; otherwise, it may seek de‑classification.
- Exposure & Capital Norms: Upper‑layer NBFCs will see tightened group exposure limits (e.g., 30 % of Total Off‑Balance‑Sheet Exposure vs. 50 % previously) and tighter capital adequacy requirements, especially for non‑government‑guaranteed exposures.
- Liquidity Support: RBI is expected to provide dedicated liquidity windows for systemic‑risk‑bearing upper‑layer NBFCs.
- Five‑Year Review Cycle: The asset‑size threshold will be revisited every five years, giving entities a transition window but also a permanent “lock‑in” period once classified.
- Strategic Responses: Smaller NBFCs may attempt short‑term balance‑sheet trimming to stay below the limit, but long‑term compliance and potential consolidation or listing are likely. Public‑sector NBFCs may need to diversify portfolios or seek RBI exemptions to meet exposure norms.
- Market Impact: The simplification aims for regulatory neutrality, but it may create a level playing field disparity between public and private NBFCs and could pressure specialized NBFCs to broaden activities or restructure.

Overall, the RBI’s asset‑size‑based rule seeks clearer, objective supervision of large NBFCs, while raising significant compliance, listing, and risk‑management challenges for both public‑sector entities and large private groups
