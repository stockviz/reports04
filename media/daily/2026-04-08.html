Title: Why Pausing SIPs in Mid‑Cap and Small‑Cap Funds Can Hurt Your Portfolio

Summary:
In a discussion on “Your Money Matters,” Shithij Mahajan, Managing Partner of Complete Circle Wealth Solutions, warns investors against stopping or pausing systematic investment plans (SIPs) during market downturns, especially in mid‑cap and small‑cap segments. He explains that SIPs work best when markets are correcting, allowing investors to accumulate more units at lower valuations. Mahajan recommends maintaining a balanced asset allocation—approximately 50 % large‑cap, 30 % mid‑cap, and 20 % small‑cap—and using systematic transfer plans (STPs) to deploy lump‑sum cash over 12‑14 weeks rather than a single entry. He stresses a long‑term horizon: at least seven years for mid‑caps and ten years for small‑caps, linking investments to specific financial goals. When selecting funds, he advises focusing on mid‑size mutual funds (₹5‑20 crore AUM) with strong managers, citing examples such as Bandhan and ICC small‑cap funds, and Mid‑cap funds from Mirae, Motilal, and Invesco. Despite concerns that the mid‑cap universe is limited, Mahajan notes that regulations require 65 % of assets to be in mid‑cap stocks, leaving ample scope for active management and potential returns of 18‑23 % annually. The key takeaway: keep SIPs alive, use STPs for lump‑sum deployment, stay disciplined, and maintain a diversified, goal‑aligned portfolio.

Key Changes in India’s Income Tax Act 2025 and FY27 Filing

The 2025 Income Tax Act replaces the 1961 Act but retains the same tax principles; it simplifies language, removes redundancies and re‑numbers sections, leading to a 35‑40 % reduction in text. For FY 26 (ended 31 Mar 2026) filings still use the 1961 Act forms, while FY 27 (starting 1 Apr 2026) follows the new Act, creating a brief dual‑act transition. The “assessment year” concept is eliminated – the financial year now doubles as the tax year. Income‑tax slabs and surcharge rates remain unchanged, with the old and new regimes continuing side‑by‑side (new regime remains default). Major reliefs for salaried employees include: • HRA exemption raised to 50 % of base salary and extended to non‑metro cities (Bengaluru, Pune, Hyderabad, Ahmedabad). • Higher limits for children’s education, hostel allowance, meal vouchers (₹200 per meal) and gift vouchers (₹15,000). • Perquisite valuation updates, especially for employer‑provided cars and electric vehicles. TCS on overseas education, medical expenses and tour packages is reduced to a uniform 2 % (down from 5 %/20 %). Filing timelines stay largely the same (original returns by 31 July, revised returns now allowed until 31 Mar of the next tax year). New/renumbered forms include Form 130 (salary certificate), Form 124 (HRA details), Form 168 (formerly 26AS), and updated non‑resident remittance forms (replacing 15CB and 10F). For corporates, buy‑backs will be taxed as normal capital gains, and several compliance requirements (tax audit, transfer‑pricing, foreign tax credit certificates) have been tightened, increasing the workload for tax advisors
