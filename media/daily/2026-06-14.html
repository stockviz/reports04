India’s Data‑Centre Bet: Costs, Opportunities and Risks

HFCL VOLTAS CLEANMAX GEPIL RELIANCE

The panel discussed how data has become the backbone of the digital economy and why India is positioning itself as a global data‑centre hub. India generates about 20 % of global data but holds only 3 % of data‑centre capacity (≈1.5 GW), prompting massive private‑sector investment backed by government incentives such as a 20‑year tax holiday and relaxed data‑localisation rules. Standard data‑centres cost ~₹60 crore per MW, AI‑focused sites up to ₹200 crore, with projected capex of ₹60,000 crore to add 5 GW by 2030, taking total capacity to ~7 GW. The discussion covered the value‑chain split (land ≈ 26 %, electrical ≈ 42 %, cooling ≈ 16 %, other ≈ 16 %), highlighting opportunities for Indian firms in construction, power, cooling, networking, and equipment. Benefits include cost‑advantage (India is second‑cheapest after China), rapid power‑addition, and potential job and multiplier effects, though data‑centres are not major direct job creators. Environmental concerns—high water and power consumption, reliance on renewable versus coal energy—were flagged, with calls for monitoring, water‑reuse tech, and stricter guardrails. The panel debated the role of tax breaks, local sourcing, and offset requirements to ensure Indian companies capture value. Overall, India aims to capture a share of the $300 bn AI‑driven data‑centre boom, but must balance investment, sustainability, and inclusive growth

SEBI Appeals to Supreme Court Over Relief Granted to Sahara Managers

ZSARACOM

The Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) has filed a petition in the Supreme Court challenging a partial ruling by the Securities Appellate Tribunal (SAT) that gave relief to four managers and the company secretary of Sahara India Commercial Corporation Ltd (SICCL). While the SAT upheld SEBI’s action against SICCL—finding that its optionally fully convertible debentures (OFCDs) issued from 1998‑2008 amounted to a public offer that raised about ₹14,106 crore from nearly 2 crore investors—the tribunal exempted the four managers and the secretary from liability, treating them as employees. SEBI argues they cannot escape responsibility and seeks to overturn that relief. The Supreme Court’s vacation bench, headed by Chief Justice Surya Kant and Justice V Mohana, will hear the case on June 18. The dispute stems from a 2018 SEBI order directing SICCL to refund investors, disclose its inventory, and bar certain officials from market participation
