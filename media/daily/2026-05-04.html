Tata Trusts Vote on Reappointing Vice‑Chairmen Amid Growing Internal Rift

3MINDIA TITAN NSIL

Tata Trusts trustees are set to vote by 10 May on the reappointment of vice‑chairmen Venu Srinivasan and Vijay Singh to the Tata Education and Development Trust (TEDT), its wealthiest affiliate with a ₹6,000 crore corpus. All five TEDT trustees – Noel Tata (chair), Singh, Srinivasan, Jehangir Nariman Mistry and Mehli Mistry – must unanimously approve the move, making Mehli Mistry’s vote pivotal after he recently challenged their continuation at another trust. A loss would strip Singh and Srinivasan of their Tata Trusts vice‑chair titles, though they could remain trustees. The vote coincides with a broader Tata Trusts meeting on 8 May, where dissent is expected to surface. Recent tensions include Singh’s removal from the Tata Sons board, Mehli Mistry’s ouster from two principal trusts, and disagreements over Tata Sons’ governance, including support for CEO Natarajan Chandrasekaran and whether the conglomerate should stay private. The outcome will signal the direction of the Trusts’ leadership unity and influence over Tata Sons

India’s Equity Outlook: Strong Fundamentals but Valuation Headwinds

ICICIAMC IVC

Ramneek Kundra of DSP Pension Fund Managers argues that India’s structural strengths merit a higher foreign investor allocation, yet current market valuations (Nifty ~21x earnings) are stretched compared to global peers. He highlights premium to emerging markets, oil import dependence, currency risk, and inflation as deterrents for FIIs, while stressing diversification over home‑bias. Kundra notes that retail inflows have surged but buying dips at lofty valuations remains risky. DSP maintains a high active‑share strategy, focusing on quality stocks to capture alpha despite potential performance volatility. Regarding the National Pension System, he points to low fees limiting marketing, modest adoption, and distribution incentives, though tax benefits make it a valuable long‑term tool. Overall, India offers compelling business opportunities, but valuation, macro, and structural factors temper allocation enthusiasm

Infosys Accelerates GCC Play with AI‑First Offerings and In‑House Incubation

HEXT INFY WAKEFIT MPHASIS HCLTECH TECHM

Infosys is responding to the rise of global capability centres (GCCs) that are insourcing work traditionally outsourced to IT services firms. It has created a dedicated GCC practice, embedding its AI suite Topaz into all GCC contracts to deliver AI‑specialised talent, automated workflows, and pre‑trained models. The company is establishing incubation hubs across its 22 delivery locations, providing ready‑to‑use facilities and cybersecurity controls, and scaling them to 500 seats in tier‑I and 250 in tier‑II cities. Most engagements follow a build‑operate‑transfer (BOT) model, with plans to hand over AI tools and software after operation. Infosys’ strategy mirrors peers like HCL and Tech Mahindra, but it aims to capture market share by cannibalising its own outsourcing revenue, leveraging deep client relationships and a repeatable, AI‑driven GCC delivery framework
