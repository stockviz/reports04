SBI Q3 preview: Net profit seen rising 5%, loan growth to stay strong

MOTILALOFS SBIN HDFC 5PAISA KOTAKBANK

State Bank of India (SBI) is expected to post a 5% YoY rise in Q3FY26 net profit to ₹17,810 cr, up from ₹16,891 cr a year earlier, with results due on 7 Feb. Analysts forecast loan growth of 13‑14% YoY, driven by retail, agriculture, small‑business and personal loans, while corporate capex remains muted. Corporate loans now make up 33% of the domestic loan book. The bank projects net interest margins to stay above 3% in Q4 FY26, supported by low‑cost CASA deposits and a diversified loan mix. Q2FY26 credit cost was 39 bps; analysts expect it to stay around 40‑50 bps through FY26‑28. Asset quality is expected to remain stable, with the gross NPA ratio at 1.73% as of September and slippages projected near 0.8% of loans. RBI rate cuts (total 125 bps since Feb 2025) and policy easing are likely to aid credit growth.

Aye Finance to test IPO market even as non-bank peers prefer to wait

BAJFINANCE 5PAISA AFSL HEROMOTOCO JMFINANCIL

Aye Finance, a VC‑backed microlender, is set to launch an IPO at ₹122‑₹129 per share, valuing the firm at about ₹3,200 crore—slightly below its 2025 Series G round. The fresh‑issue component has been cut to ₹710 crore (from ₹885 crore) and the offer‑for‑sale (OFS) reduced to ₹265 crore, with several anchor investors trimming or dropping their sales. Proceeds will bolster Tier‑1 capital to fund growth for the next 2‑3 years, targeting a loan book of ₹15‑₹16 trillion.

The IPO proceeds amid a broader slowdown in NBFC listings, as peers such as Veritas Finance, Hero FinCorp and others have postponed IPOs due to rising bad loans and asset‑quality concerns. NBFCs are expected to see modest profitability improvement only by FY 2026‑27. Aye Finance’s current NPA ratio is 4.85% (overall stress pool ~9%), but the company reports improving credit costs and a “phygital” model that drives growth without proportional branch expansion. Elevation Capital, the largest shareholder, is not selling shares. The subscription window runs 9‑11 February for retail investors.

AI startups positioned to transform India’s IT services, despite SaaS setbacks

AVL WIPRO HCLTECH BMWVENTLTD INFY HALDER TCS TVSELECT 5PAISA

  • India’s 2010s SaaS boom fell short of expectations, producing few $5 bn+ firms; most successes (e.g., Freshworks, Zoho) were founded by Indians who later moved HQs abroad.
  • The “fast‑follower” model—offering cheaper copies of Western products—worked in pre‑AI times but is ineffective now; AI demands original, rapid innovation.
  • AI startups are poised to disrupt India’s $283 bn IT services sector, focusing on three segments: building new applications, software implementation, and application‑maintenance automation.
  • Lightspeed Venture Partners backs AI‑first founders (e.g., Emergent, Ressl, Realfast) and expects a future roll‑up: entrepreneurs will acquire legacy IT firms with ~20 % margins, automate them, and lift margins to ~40 %.
  • Recent breakthroughs from Palantir’s Hivemind and Anthropic’s Claude Cowork have heightened anxiety among Indian IT incumbents, triggering noticeable drops in the stocks of TCS, Infosys, HCLTech, Wipro and Tech Mahindra.
  • Overall, VCs see AI as the next growth engine for Indian tech, shifting focus from SaaS volume to AI‑driven transformation of traditional IT services.

It’s all about hours clocked—Urban Company data punctures work value talk

ZOMATO URBANCO

Urban Company’s data (April‑Dec 2025) shows its top 5% of professionals work ~167 hours/month—about twice the average – and earn roughly double the monthly income (₹51,673 vs ₹28,322), but their higher pay comes from longer hours and more orders, not higher hourly rates (₹310‑₹313 for all). Workers report falling earnings as service prices drop, commissions rise, and costs increase; one partner’s income fell from ₹55‑60 k to ₹13 k. Unions argue gig work lacks upward mobility, skill‑based pay, and basic rights (e.g., paid menstrual leave, restroom access). New Indian labour codes (late 2025) now recognize gig workers, mandating 1‑2 % of platform turnover to a Social Security Fund (capped at 5 % of worker payments) and Aadhaar‑linked benefit portability. CEOs of aggregators claim gig jobs match entry‑level IT salaries and promote upskilling, but critics say the model forces longer hours for shrinking take‑home pay without real career progression.
