How AI Is Supporting Learning At Scale | The Wadhwani AI Story | N18M

The video describes how Wadhwani AI, founded by brothers Ramesh and Sunil Vadhwani, uses artificial intelligence to improve reading fluency for millions of primary‑school children in India. Their ORF (Oral Reading Fluency) app listens to a child read in Hindi, English or a local language, identifies correct, incorrect, omitted and added words, and assigns a transparent fluency score. By collecting teacher‑verified voice data and student recordings, the system trains custom speech‑recognition models that tolerate dialectal variations instead of auto‑correcting errors. The app lets teachers assess every student quickly, group pupils by reading level, and deliver personalized remediation, while also providing parents with real‑time progress reports. Piloted in Gujarat and Rajasthan, the platform has already scaled to millions of users and is being integrated into state competency‑based assessments. The team envisions expanding the technology nationwide, adding comprehension analysis, and ultimately creating a lasting, AI‑driven learning infrastructure for India’s early‑grade education.

India–US Trade Deal: Textile Exports Get 15–18% Profit Boost, Pearl Global’s Palab Banerjee Explains

PGIL

Summary

  • Trade Deal Impact: The new India‑U.S. trade agreement eliminates the 25 % reciprocal tariff that Indian textile exporters faced, bringing India’s effective tariff to roughly 2 % lower than competitors (Bangladesh, Cambodia, Vietnam, Indonesia). This translates into a 15‑18 % improvement in bottom‑line profitability for Indian exporters serving the U.S. market.

  • Immediate Financial Effect:

    • Indian firms previously had to offer ~15‑18 % discounts to offset the tariff gap.
    • With the tariff removed, those discounts disappear, instantly boosting profitability for U.S.‑focused operations (evident in Q4 2024, February‑March).
    • Top‑line growth may materialize in subsequent quarters as the cost advantage rekindles demand.

  • Company‑Specific Outlook (Pearl Global):

    • Global footprint: manufacturing in India, Indonesia, Vietnam, Bangladesh, Guatemala.
    • About 25 % of Pearl’s revenue comes from India; the rest from other low‑tariff sites.
    • The tariff removal mainly benefits the Indian segment; overall company impact is modest because the bulk of production is already diversified.
    • No major shift in capacity allocation is expected; the firm will continue to leverage each country’s comparative advantages.

  • Industry Response:

    • Many pure‑play Indian exporters had been cutting prices or shifting orders abroad to mitigate the tariff; they can now resume normal pricing and regain lost U.S. business.
    • Companies that had moved production to other Asian hubs may not fully revert, but the cost‑advantage restores India’s competitiveness.

  • Broader Context:

    • Recent EU‑India trade concessions add further market access, enhancing India’s export outlook.
    • Government reforms—easier capital access, labor law flexibility, raw‑material availability, and improved “ease of doing business”—complement the trade deals, creating a more favorable environment for textile manufacturing.

  • Key Takeaway: The removal of the 25 % U.S. tariff gives Indian textile exporters a direct 15‑18 % profit uplift on U.S. sales, restores price parity with regional rivals, and signals a turning point for the sector as broader policy reforms and additional trade agreements bolster India’s export competitiveness.

India, US Seal Trade Deal: Historic Deal To Serve As A Major Catalyst For Indian Markets | CNBC TV18 2026-02-03 10:50

HDFCAMC ABSLAMC

The interview centers on the newly announced India‑U.S. trade deal and its market implications.

  • Catalyst Effect – Both guests agree the deal is a major catalyst that revives market sentiment after a prolonged lull dominated by gold and low equity interest. It should lift corporate profit growth, improve the rupee, and encourage foreign‑institutional (FI) participation.

  • Sector Focus

    • Large‑cap, liquid Nifty names are the first beneficiaries; money is expected to flow into big, liquid stocks rather than small‑cap or niche picks.
    • Export‑oriented sectors such as textiles, engineering, and certain financial services could gain from reduced U.S. tariffs (the new 18 % duty is lower than that faced by Bangladesh, Vietnam, China).
    • IT services are seen as neutral – they gain from a weaker rupee but are not directly affected by tariffs.
    • Financials may see modest upside due to credit‑growth spillovers from export‑linked firms.
    • Mid‑ and small‑caps could benefit from the budget’s buy‑back rule change; promoters will be incentivised to repurchase shares, creating a near‑term supply‑drain and potential upside for smaller stocks.

  • Buy‑back Rule Change – Promoter buybacks will now be taxed at the same rate as capital gains, making buybacks a more attractive alternative to dividends. Companies are expected to announce buybacks quickly, sucking cash out of the market and supporting share prices.

  • Foreign Investor Outlook – After a period of outflows driven by valuation concerns and rupee weakness, foreign investors are likely to return, especially to large‑cap stocks, as valuations improve and the currency stabilises. However, continued heavy issuance (IPOs, PE exits) could temper the rally.

  • Long‑Term Perspective – India’s share of global GDP (≈ 4 %) and market cap (≈ 3.4 %) could rise to 7‑8 % over the next decade, offering a sizable wealth‑creation opportunity. Domestic investors have remained counter‑cyclical, and the deal, together with the rating upgrade and GST‑driven consumption, sets the stage for a sustained equity upside.

A Lot More Bilateral Negotiations Still Need To Happen: Neuberger Berman | US-India Trade Deal

HAL NETWEB DATAPATTNS BEL PGIL

The interview with Conrad (Neuberger Berman) focuses on the new US‑India trade deal that grants India an 18 % tariff relief on many imports. He notes that while the relief will boost heavily impacted export sectors such as gems, jewelry, textiles, and apparel, broader benefits for India Inc will depend on further bilateral negotiations to address remaining tariff and non‑tariff barriers, especially those covered by Section 232 (autos, aluminum, timber, copper, trucks, shipping, steel, etc.).

Key points:

  • The 18 % cut is competitive versus other ASEAN exporters and should trigger a relief rally for export‑driven firms that saw 30‑40 % top‑line declines.
  • Sustainable recovery requires deeper FDI, private‑sector CAPEX, and a wider set of sectors beyond the initial beneficiaries.
  • Section 232 restrictions remain; they affect a modest share of trade but still matter for autos, steel, and related industries.
  • Energy implications: India will shift from discounted Russian crude to U.S. and Venezuelan supplies. Refiners, accustomed to heavy crude, are expected to adapt, though the previous arbitrage advantage is gone.
  • Investment outlook: Neuberger Berman has limited exposure to chemicals and textiles, preferring domestic growth themes—defense (e.g., Bharat Electronics), software/engineering, and data‑center infrastructure (e.g., NetWeb).
  • Overall, the deal is a positive step, but additional bilateral talks and sector‑specific resolutions are needed to translate the tariff cut into lasting, economy‑wide gains.

Textile Sector Cheers India-US Trade Deal, Pact To Aid Textile Cos’ Margin & Boost Export Demand

PGIL

The textile industry welcomed the new US‑India trade deal that cut tariffs from 50% to 18%, easing a major cost burden. Palab Banerjee of Pearl Global said the lower tariff eliminates the need to shift production to Bangladesh or offer steep discounts, allowing the company to grow both top‑line and bottom‑line sales in India. He estimates India now contributes roughly 25‑30% of Pearl Global’s revenue, with US exposure just under 50%, and expects a more balanced market mix across the EU, US, UK, Japan and Australia. Banerjee indicated the tariff reduction will improve margins rather than be passed on as discounts.

Archaevage, President Emeritus of the Textile Association, echoed the optimism, crediting the decision to Prime Minister Modi and President Trump. He highlighted the US apparel market’s size (≈ $130 billion of a $288 billion total) and noted that the 18% tariff will revive idle capacity in Indian textile hubs (Tirpur, Panipat, etc.) and boost exports across categories—apparel, home textiles, technical textiles, and more. Both speakers view the tariff cut as a “very good day” for Indian textiles, expecting renewed production and stronger export demand.
