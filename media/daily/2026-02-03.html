Textile Sector Cheers India-US Trade Deal, Pact To Aid Textile Cos’ Margin & Boost Export Demand

PGIL

The textile industry welcomed the new US‑India trade deal that cut tariffs from 50% to 18%, easing a major cost burden. Palab Banerjee of Pearl Global said the lower tariff eliminates the need to shift production to Bangladesh or offer steep discounts, allowing the company to grow both top‑line and bottom‑line sales in India. He estimates India now contributes roughly 25‑30% of Pearl Global’s revenue, with US exposure just under 50%, and expects a more balanced market mix across the EU, US, UK, Japan and Australia. Banerjee indicated the tariff reduction will improve margins rather than be passed on as discounts.

Archaevage, President Emeritus of the Textile Association, echoed the optimism, crediting the decision to Prime Minister Modi and President Trump. He highlighted the US apparel market’s size (≈ $130 billion of a $288 billion total) and noted that the 18% tariff will revive idle capacity in Indian textile hubs (Tirpur, Panipat, etc.) and boost exports across categories—apparel, home textiles, technical textiles, and more. Both speakers view the tariff cut as a “very good day” for Indian textiles, expecting renewed production and stronger export demand.

Chemical Companies With US Exposure To Gain From US-India Trade Deal; Oil Stocks To Benefit

ACUTAAS RELIANCE NAVINFLUOR SRF AARTIIND NEOGEN VINATIORGA ONGC

  • Chemicals sector: Companies such as SRF, Naveen Fluorine, Acutas Chemicals, Neogen Chemicals, Aarti Industries and Vinati Organics rely heavily on U.S. exports. Recent U.S. tariffs created uncertainty and hurt their sales, especially over the past three months. The upcoming U.S.–India trade deal is expected to lift many of these tariffs, providing relief. Neogen noted that battery‑chemical items are excluded from the tariff list, but the deal’s fine print still leaves about 18% of products subject to duties.

  • Oil & gas sector: The same tariffs also targeted oil, prompting political pressure for India to shift crude purchases from Russia to the U.S. (and possibly Venezuela). Since November, Russian oil from sanctioned entities has essentially stopped. Oil marketers report they are no longer buying from sanctioned Russian firms and are increasing U.S. crude imports, adjusting their product mix accordingly. Reliance has historically sourced ~20% of its crude from Venezuela; a renewed tie‑up could give it a $5‑$8 per barrel discount, boosting its gross refining margin. ONGC is awaiting dividend payouts, and any resurgence of Venezuelan imports would be broadly positive for the Indian oil‑gas industry.

US Tariff Reduction Lowers The Export Cost Burden & Improves The Export Viability Of Indian Auto Cos

ROHLTD TATAMOTORS BHARATFORG SONACOMS BALPHARMA PENINLAND EICHERMOT

The US tariff cut reduces export costs, boosting the export viability of Indian auto makers and suppliers. Key beneficiaries are:

  • Tata Motors – JLR derives 33% of its volumes from the US and contributes 86% of Tata’s passenger‑vehicle revenue.
  • Bharat Forge – about 38% of its sales come from North America, supplying forgings and parts.
  • SONA and BLW – US sales exceed 40% of total revenue.
  • Samwaradhan/Madison – North‑American exposure around 18‑20%.
  • Bal Krishna – 17% of revenue from the US.
  • Isha Motors/Royal Enfield – minimal exposure, only ~2% of volumes in the US.

Overall, the tariff reduction mainly benefits Tata Motors (through JLR) and the high‑exposure auto‑ancillary firms Bharat Forge, SONA and BLW.

U.S. & India Reach Trade Deal; Trump Slashes India Tariffs To 18%: Indian Market & Economy To Gain?

M&M HDFC SELMC HCLTECH BCG

The United States and India have finalized a trade agreement that reduces Indian tariffs on U.S. goods from 50% to 18%, positioning India as a competitive manufacturing hub alongside Vietnam, Indonesia and the Philippines and encouraging supply‑chain shifts away from China.

Key points from the discussion: - Economic impact: Both Ed Yardeni and Matt Orton view the deal as a major win for the U.S. and India and a strategic loss for Russia (and indirectly for China). They expect it to boost India’s economy, corporate earnings, and stock market performance. - Investor sentiment: The reduction in tariffs should improve foreign‑investor (FII) sentiment, especially after a weak period for India relative to other emerging markets. Positive reactions were already seen in ADRs for HDFC and Enfora. - Sector winners: Companies likely to benefit include Maruti Suzuki (M&M), HCL Technologies, and firms in agriculture, machinery, manufacturing, and AI‑related supply chains that align with India’s recent union budget priorities. - Policy backdrop: Further support could come from an RBI rate cut and clear guidance, which would stabilize the rupee and facilitate dollar inflows. - Broader context: The deal complements India’s new 21‑year tax holiday for foreign data‑center investors, helping to dispel the “anti‑AI” perception and positioning India for AI and cloud growth. - Market outlook: With improved trade terms and supportive fiscal/monetary policies, the panel expects stronger domestic and foreign demand, a potential rebound in Indian market outperformance, and continued bullishness for the global economy.
