Gold & Silver Outlook: Central Bank Support, Market Volatility, and Growth Prospects

STANLEY 5PAISA

In a CNBC TV18 Prime interview, DHF Capital CEO Bas Kushman discussed the current state and future expectations for gold and silver. He noted that gold has pulled back from its recent all‑time highs of $5,500‑$5,600 to around $4,100, presenting a “healthy correction” while still delivering year‑on‑year gains. Central banks remain the main source of demand, buying several hundred tons regularly, which he believes will keep gold above the $4,000 level despite short‑term pressure. Analyst consensus ranges from $4,900 (Goldman Sachs) to above $5,000 (Morgan Stanley). Retail and ETF buying have been muted after profit‑taking and a brief dip linked to the SpaceX IPO.

The dollar’s strength, driven by a hawkish Fed outlook (possible 25‑bp hike in December) and easing geopolitical tensions, is supporting gold, though a further break below $4,000 is deemed unlikely. Kushman expects gold to trade in a $4,000‑$5,500 band this year, with the potential to reach high‑$5,000 levels by year‑end if central‑bank buying persists and geopolitical risks re‑emerge.

On silver, Kushman highlighted its higher volatility but stronger upside due to industrial demand—particularly for electric vehicles, AI chips, and data‑center equipment. Silver is trading near $81 and could climb to $100 per ounce, benefitting from its dual role as a hedge and industrial metal.

He also linked the massive global debt load (≈$350 trillion) and U.S. debt (~$38‑$39 trillion) to a growing appetite for precious‑metal reserves, reinforcing the de‑dollarization trend and supporting continued central‑bank accumulation of gold and silver. Overall, Kushman remains bullish on both metals, recommending a 5‑10 % portfolio allocation to gold for hedge purposes and a positive long‑term view on silver’s growth potential

Balanced Advantage Funds: Dynamic Allocation for Smoother Returns

ABSLAMC NDTV

Balanced Advantage Funds (BAFs) are hybrid mutual funds with no fixed equity‑debt split, allowing managers to shift allocations based on market conditions. This flexibility aims to curb the “behavior gap” where investors’ timing errors erode returns. During market peaks, managers may reduce equity exposure; during corrections, they can increase it, cushioning downside while still participating in upside. Historical data shows BAFs tend to experience lower drawdowns than pure equity funds, though they may lag in prolonged bull markets. To evaluate a BAF, investors should examine the fund’s minimum‑maximum equity range, past allocation shifts, and the manager’s proprietary model. Because intra‑fund switches are tax‑efficient, BAFs suit first‑time or risk‑averse investors seeking smoother journeys, as well as aggressive investors who want professionally managed, dynamically allocated exposure. The category is broadly suitable for core portfolio allocations, with no specific investor group to avoid

ICICIGI TVVISION MOTILALOFS
