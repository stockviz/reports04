BBC Plans Major Job Cuts to Save £500 Million

The BBC will cut up to 2,000 jobs, aiming to reduce its annual budget by about 10% (£500 million) over the next two years. Interim Director‑General Rhodri Talfan Davies cites inflation, license‑fee pressure, commercial income challenges, and a turbulent economy as drivers. The cuts, the largest in a decade, will mainly occur in the fiscal year starting April 1 2027. Matt Brittin, former Google executive, will become director‑general next month, succeeding Tim Davie after news chief Deborah Turness resigned over a controversial Trump documentary edit. The broadcaster faces criticism over the license fee, which recently rose to £180, and ongoing debates about its funding model

India’s hotels go beyond rooms, loading up on dining, banquets and spas

3MINDIA ADVANIHOTR

A Savills‑Hotelivate report finds Indian budget and mid‑scale hotels are overbuilt by global standards, routinely offering multiple restaurants, large banquet halls and leisure facilities to tap strong demand for weddings, family gatherings and group travel. Non‑room revenue can account for up to 60% of business, especially in tier‑II/III cities, prompting developers to build larger, amenity‑rich properties despite higher construction costs (average ₹1.36 cr per room). Occupancy remains steady at 67‑68%, while average room rates have risen 8‑9% to about ₹9,000‑9,100. The pipeline now includes over 120,000 new rooms (≈50% of current inventory), suggesting gradual easing of price pressure, though some markets like Goa and Lucknow show early softening

Conglomerates dominate India’s IBC asset acquisitions, driving turnarounds and value capture

JINDALSTEL MSPL ELECTCAST ESSARSHPNG RELIANCE UTTAMSTL JSWSTEEL IOC VEDL ADANIPOWER TATASTEEL BCG JPASSOCIAT

Over the ten‑year span of India’s Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, four major conglomerates—Adani, JSW, Reliance, and Tata—have accounted for nearly a quarter of the total admitted claims (≈13 trillion rupees) despite representing only 28 of 1,376 completed resolutions. Adani led with 13 deals (including six power firms), JSW followed with seven (notably Bhushan Power & Steel), Reliance secured five assets, and Tata bought three. Together they captured a disproportionate share of claim value—JSW ~8%, Adani ~6% (rising to ~10% with Jaiprakash Associates), Reliance ~6%, Tata ~5% of total admitted claims. Their acquisitions often involve large‑ticket claims (>1,000 crore), which represent 86% of total claim value. The groups leverage cross‑sector synergies, deep capital, and operational expertise to turn around distressed assets, achieving cost‑effective capacity expansions (e.g., Adani Power’s 5.5 GW at ~3.2 crore/MW vs. 10‑12 crore/MW for greenfield plants) and profitable exits (e.g., JSW’s sale of half BPSL stake for 37,250 crore). While creditors recovered an average of 31.6% of claims (171.5% of liquidation values), the dominance of these conglomerates underscores the IBC’s role in reallocating stuck assets, enhancing utilization, and preserving jobs

Slice Shifts Focus to Secured Loans in Fintech‑to‑Bank Pivot

Since its 2024 merger‑led transition into a bank, Slice is moving from an almost entirely unsecured consumer‑credit portfolio to a largely secured‑loan book, now 22% of assets and expected to dominate within a decade. The shift leverages its banking licence, lower cost of funds, and branch network expansion to grow asset‑backed lending such as loans against property and to small merchants. Deposits have more than doubled to ₹5,111 cr, and AUM rose 78% YoY to ₹4,554 cr in FY26. While secured lending demands physical underwriting and collateral verification, Slice aims to digitise the process over time. The company targets profitability, posted a net profit of ₹27.97 cr in the first nine months of FY26 after a FY25 loss, and is exploring a fresh $50‑100 m funding round. CEO Rajan Bajaj envisions Slice becoming India’s “NuBank,” though experts caution about the challenges for digital‑only banks in the market

Roblox Settles with Nevada Over Child Safety, Pays $12 Million

Roblox agreed to pay more than $12 million to Nevada and adopt stricter child‑protection measures after the state’s attorney general sued. The deal includes $10 million over three years for youth programs, funding a law‑enforcement liaison, and an online‑safety campaign. New safeguards require age verification for all users, limit nighttime notifications, use facial age‑estimation to restrict chats to same‑age peers, and allow chats only with “trusted friends” verified via QR code or contacts. Kids’ accounts under 16 will block adult content, provide vetted games, and expand parental oversight. The settlement, described as a “first‑of‑its‑kind” agreement, aims to set a national standard for digital safety as Roblox faces similar lawsuits in other states
