India Energy Week 2026 | India Is A Bright Spot For The Energy Market: Jio-BP | CNBC TV18

JIOFIN

Key Takeaways from Akshay Vadwahi, CEO, GOP (Jio‑BP) at India Energy Week 2026

  • India’s Energy Outlook
    • Per‑capita fuel consumption is only ~1/3 of the global average, making India a high‑growth market.
    • Current domestic fuel demand is rising: petrol volumes up ~6 % YoY, diesel ~2 %.
  • GOBP’s Differentiation Strategy
    • Active‑Technology Fuel: Additive‑enhanced petrol and diesel that deliver measurable mileage gains (≈4.5 % extra mileage on diesel, ~100 km added range on petrol).
    • Zero‑Premium Pricing: The enhanced fuels are sold at the same price as regular fuel to “democratise” efficiency.
    • Full‑Outlet Automation & AI: 100 % of outlets are AI‑enabled—real‑time monitoring of staff compliance, safety alerts (e.g., fire detection), and personalized customer experiences (e.g., targeted greetings, in‑store café services).
    • Multi‑Fuel “Mobility Stations”: Each outlet is being equipped to offer liquid fuels, gaseous fuels, and EV fast‑charging (≈1,000 chargers with >95 % uptime), turning traditional petrol pumps into integrated energy hubs.
  • Performance vs. Market
    • GOP’s overall fuel sales are growing ~30 % against a 6 % market growth; diesel (HSD) sales are up 28 % versus a 3 % market rise.
    • The growth is driven by the limited network size—expansion will expose more consumers to the differentiated product.
  • Energy Transition in India
    • Recent vehicle sales: 2.5 % EVs, 6.5 % gas‑engine vehicles; the remaining 90 % are petrol/diesel (≈80 % petrol, 10 % diesel).
    • GOP’s vision: supply the appropriate energy for any power‑train, emphasizing a seamless, experience‑focused transition.
  • Future Investment Focus
    • Current CAPEX allocation to “future energy” (low‑carbon fuels, EV infrastructure) is ~10 % of total spend.
    • Plans to double this to ~20 % within the next year, scaling proportionally with market adoption of new fuels.
    • Target: become the largest low‑carbon energy player in India by 2035.
  • Overall Message
    • India remains a “bright spot” for energy demand growth.
    • GOP is leveraging technology, AI, and a multi‑fuel outlet model to differentiate its offering, improve engine efficiency at no extra cost, and position itself at the forefront of India’s low‑carbon mobility transition.

CNBC-TV18 CII Town Hall | Budget Barometer | Over 70 CEOs, One Destination | Budget 2026 2026-01-31 13:06

LTF THERMAX

Over 70 CEOs gathered at the CNBC-TV18 CII Town Hall “Budget Barometer” ahead of India’s 2026 Union Budget, sharing expectations from Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman. Key themes included boosting manufacturing competitiveness, export promotion, infrastructure capex (expected at ₹14 lakh crore), and deregulation. Business leaders emphasized the need for customs duty rationalization, faster tax dispute resolution, and tax certainty over tax breaks. Specific asks included group taxation for infrastructure SPVs, pension fund investments in infrastructure, revival of PPPs, and customs amnesty. Enhancing ease of doing business, logistics efficiency, power costs, and transmission infrastructure were highlighted, especially for data centers and electronic manufacturing clusters. Support for R&D, innovation, and private sector PhD hiring was urged to move up global value chains. Education reform, attracting global talent, and expanding higher education were seen as crucial for long-term growth. The strategic use of FTAs, addressing India’s 2% global trade share, and focusing on mid-sized companies and MSMEs received attention. Real estate called for tax incentives for GCCs, REITs, and InvITs to attract FDI. Agriculture and food processing were identified as opportunities for global manufacturing. Trust between government and industry, ease of paying taxes, and dispute resolution mechanisms were deemed critical. The consensus centered on competitiveness, sustained capex, structural reforms, and shifting from “Ruler Raj” to “Citizen Raj” for India to achieve its full potential. CNBC TV18 will broadcast live budget coverage from 7 a.m. on February 1st.

Key take‑aways from the round‑table on “What Bharat Wants from Budget 2026”

  • Start‑ups & tech founders (Bengaluru) – demand clear, globally‑aligned reforms for ESOPs (single‑tax treatment, longer vesting periods), tax incentives for early‑stage funding, and legal safeguards that keep founders’ equity intact during IPOs and hostile take‑overs.

  • Renewable‑energy sector (Gujarat) – seeks a two‑year implementation roadmap for the nation’s gigawatt‑target, strict enforcement of green‑power purchase obligations for large manufacturers, and assurance that climate‑policy goals won’t be softened.

  • Agriculture & agri‑processing (Madhya Pradesh) – wants stronger export incentives, resolution of tariff barriers, and protection of domestic processors from GM‑import pressures; also highlights labour‑scheme (Jee‑Ram‑Ji) impacts on farm‑labour availability.

  • Textiles & apparel (Coimbatore) – welcomes the recent GST cut on non‑cotton inputs (18 % → 5 %) that lifts margins, but calls for additional export‑support measures and clarity on EU regulatory requirements.

  • Real‑estate & tier‑2 growth (Indore, Chennai, Ahmedabad) – notes rapid price appreciation and rising interest from GCC/IT firms, urging consistent land‑use policies and infrastructure backing.

  • Automobile & auto‑components (Pune, other hubs) – stresses the need for higher R&D incentives (current 2‑3 % of sales far below global peers), a stable PLI framework beyond current cycles, and smoother import‑approval processes for specialised equipment amid supply‑chain disruptions.

  • MSMEs & exporters – request short‑term working‑capital relief, simplified tax‑compliance assistance (no need for large in‑house teams), and faster resolution of tax disputes (target ≤ 12 months).

  • Foreign investors & capital markets – call for restoration of long‑term‑capital‑gains relief, parity between equity and fixed‑income taxation, and a decisive, transparent mechanism for settling disputes such as the Tiger‑Global case.

  • Defence & indigenisation – push for additional PLI schemes to accelerate local defence manufacturing and broader “Make‑in‑India” drives.

  • Overall sentiment – across regions, the consensus is for stable, predictable policy, accelerated dispute resolution, targeted PLI incentives, and continued fiscal discipline that sustains the consumption boost sparked by recent income‑tax and GST cuts.

Higher Difference Between LTCG On Equity, Debt In India Vs Many Large Developed Markets: Experts

PRUDMOULI LICHSGFIN AXISBANK LGBBROSLTD HYUNDAI WHIRLPOOL CUB

Summary

  • Context: Indian equity markets are down ~5% from all‑time highs and ~4% in early 2026, prompting calls to lower long‑term capital‑gains tax (LTCG) on equities. The current tax structure creates a large disparity between the 12.5 % LTCG rate on equities and the 30‑+ % marginal tax on interest income from debt, deposits, and bonds.

  • Key Points from the Experts:

    1. G Mahalingam (ex‑RBI/SEBI):
      • The high equity LTCG benefit encourages risk‑seeking behavior, pushing retail and corporate savers into equities despite higher volatility.
      • The differential (equity LTCG ≈ 12.5 % vs. interest tax ≈ 33‑42 %) is effectively a “wealth tax” on fixed‑income returns, discouraging investment in debt.
      • Over‑taxation of debt may distort asset allocation and raise financing costs for the economy.
    2. Anantranarayan (former banker/SEBI):
      • Tax considerations dominate asset allocation; retirees are forced to take more equity risk than their appetite permits.
      • Interest income taxed at marginal rates without indexation acts like a wealth tax, pushing investors toward equities.
      • A uniform, low LTCG rate across asset classes would let investors allocate based on risk, not tax.
    3. Srinivasan Varadarajan (ex‑Axis/Union Bank):
      • From a policy angle, the government seeks more equity capital to fuel growth; hence the preferential equity tax.
      • However, 80 % of debt capital is institutional; retail‑driven equity incentives have limited macro impact.
      • The current incentive has led to a surge in equity inflows (≈ 8.8 lakh crore) but also inflated valuations and misallocation of resources.
      • High equity tax advantage has inadvertently facilitated foreign direct investment (FDI) outflows, as multinational firms find Indian share prices cheap and repatriate capital.
      • The tilt risks depriving the debt market of needed funding, especially for lower‑rated borrowers and MSMEs that rely on banks.

  • Implications:

    • For Debt Markets: Reduced attractiveness of debt could tighten funding for corporates, especially MSMEs, leading to higher financing costs and potential slowdown in productive investment.
    • For Banks: Deposit growth is slowing; banks may face funding gaps while still bearing the burden of financing weaker borrowers.
    • For Capital Allocation: Tax‑driven skew toward equities may cause asset‑class misallocation, inflated equity valuations, and volatility.
    • For Macro Stability: Persistent current‑account deficits (~2.5 %) combined with weakened debt capital flows could strain external financing and amplify macro‑economic risks.

  • Suggested Policy Direction:

    • Re‑evaluate the LTCG rate to reduce the gap between equity and debt taxation.
    • Consider a uniform, low tax on long‑term gains across asset classes, possibly indexed for inflation.
    • Align tax policy with the goal of balanced financial deepening—encouraging both equity risk capital and robust debt markets.
    • Use the upcoming budget(s) to address the tax distortion, support bond‑market development, and safeguard funding for the MSME sector.
