How Are 100-Baggers Picked In The Indian Stock Market? Ft Pankaj Tibrewal 2026-01-14 17:15

Key Take‑aways from Pankaj Tibrewal’s interview

  1. Traits of 100‑baggers
    • Start as tiny players in large, growing markets (e.g., Bajaj Finance’s 0.2‑0.3 % market share in 1999 → 2.3 % today).
    • Benefit from a sizable, long‑run sector tailwind that gives them room to capture market share without intense competition.
    • Compounding at modest rates (≈20 % YoY) over 25 years can generate 100‑X returns; the exact winners can’t be identified a priori.
  2. Where new multi‑baggers may emerge in India
    • Non‑lending financial infrastructure (exchanges, depositories, AMCs, etc.).
    • Consumer discretionary as per‑capita income rises and aspirations shift.
    • Chemicals and downstream value‑addition as India climbs the global value chain.
    • Auto ancillaries, precision engineering, defense, aerospace, semiconductors – high‑growth, capital‑intensive niches.
    • Mid‑tier IT firms that will drive AI implementation across industries.
    • The “sweet spot” for discovery lies in companies with market caps of ₹5,000‑₹25,000 cr, where analyst coverage thins out.
  3. Capex landscape
    • Private‑sector capex has rebounded to ~₹11.5 L cr (vs. ₹5‑6 L cr a few years ago); total (private + government) sits above ₹31 L cr.
    • The surge is concentrated in a few large groups; broader participation is needed for sustained growth.
  4. Power & energy‑transition sector
    • Recent valuation busts (100‑150 P/E) were overstretched; market is correcting.
    • Fundamentals remain positive: power demand is picking up (6 % YoY growth in Dec), utilities and transmission assets could benefit post‑correction.
    • Caution on cash‑flow‑negative solar/module firms; growth without cash generation can be destructive.
  5. Budget expectations
    • Favorable tax reforms are welcome, but the key missing lever is the buy‑back tax – its removal would free up cash for capex or shareholder returns.
    • Additional fiscal headroom could come from:
      • Accelerated disinvestment,
      • Higher RBI dividend payouts,
      • Modest excise‑duty tweaks on oil‑products.
    • Longer‑term growth hinges on real GDP expansion, which will lift corporate earnings and tax receipts.
  6. Outlook for 2026
    • Fundamentals (earnings growth, domestic flow improvements) are better than a year ago.
    • Sentiment is extremely negative—potentially a contrarian buying signal.
    • If geopolitical or trade developments improve and foreign inflows resume, India could outperform other emerging markets.

Averaging Down Vs. Averaging Up | Market Expert Explains What Actually Makes Money

Averaging Down vs. Averaging Up
- Averaging down (buying more of a quality stock after a price decline) can be beneficial if you have a strong conviction that the stock’s long‑term trend remains positive and the dip is just market volatility. It lowers your average cost and positions you to profit from the expected rebound.
- Averaging up / pyramiding (adding to a position as the price rises) works when the stock shows consistent momentum (e.g., silver). Buying higher adds to a winning trade, but it requires confidence that the up‑trend will continue.
- In both cases the key is a positive outlook on the underlying asset over the short‑, medium‑, or long‑term, not merely chasing sunk‑cost thinking.
