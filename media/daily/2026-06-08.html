U.S. Lawmakers Push Aggressive Reforms to H‑1B Visa Program

TCS KISSHT

Since early 2024, a wave of Republican-sponsored bills aims to severely curtail, pause, or end the H‑1B visa program, which allows foreign specialists to work in the U.S. Proposals include cutting the maximum stay from six to two years, requiring proof of intent to return home, limiting visas to high‑salary roles, raising wage requirements, restricting use by large IT outsourcers, and even suspending the program entirely. Notable bills are Chip Roy’s “American White‑Collar Worker Jobs Act” (2026), Marjorie Taylor Greene’s “End H‑1B Now Act,” Greg Steube’s EXILE Act, and Eli Crane’s “End H‑1B Visa Abuse Act.” The measures would also give U.S. workers a tort cause of action against employers who displace them with non‑immigrants. Indian IT firms, which rely heavily on H‑1B talent, claim they are increasing domestic hiring and reducing visa dependence. Simultaneously, the Biden administration has tightened green‑card rules, further heightening uncertainty for H‑1B holders, most of whom are Indian nationals. Critics warn the proposals could harm the U.S. economy and face resistance from industry and bipartisan lawmakers
