Morgan Stanley to Cut 2,500 Jobs Amid Cost‑Cutting and Market Pressures

STANLEY

Morgan Stanley announced it will eliminate roughly 2,500 positions, representing about 3% of its global workforce. The cuts will primarily target investment‑banking and trading divisions where deal flow has slowed, as well as technology and operations teams tied to legacy platforms. The bank cites weaker capital‑markets activity, lower revenue from advisory fees, and a strategic shift toward digital services and automation as drivers for the reductions. The layoffs are part of a broader cost‑restructuring plan aimed at improving profitability and meeting earnings targets after recent earnings missed expectations. Employees affected will receive severance packages and outplacement support, with the reductions slated to be implemented over the next few months

Amazon Cuts Robotics Workforce

Amazon has begun laying off staff in its robotics division, according to a recent report. The layoffs are part of a broader effort to trim costs and streamline operations amid slowing growth. While the exact number of affected employees was not disclosed, the move signals the company’s shift in focus away from certain automation initiatives and could impact ongoing projects that rely on internal robotic technology. Industry analysts suggest the cuts reflect Amazon’s reassessment of its investment priorities and a response to competitive pressures in the e‑commerce and cloud sectors

Big Banks Poised to Profit from Credit Market Stress

BlackRock’s senior strategist, Kapnick, warned that ongoing turbulence in the credit markets will create significant opportunities for large financial firms. He noted that tighter lending conditions, rising defaults, and heightened risk aversion could push investors toward more stable, high‑quality assets managed by big players. As smaller lenders face funding pressures, the market share and pricing power of major banks and asset managers are expected to expand, potentially boosting their earnings despite broader economic uncertainty
