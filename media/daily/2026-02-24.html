Arbitrage vs Income‑Arbitrage Funds: Key Differences, Taxation, and Allocation Strategies

GANGESSECU

The discussion explains that traditional arbitrage funds buy stocks in the cash market and sell futures, offering equity‑like returns with low volatility, suitable for 6‑month to 2‑year horizons and taxed like equity (20% STCG, 12.5% LTCG after 1 year). Income‑arbitrage (balanced) funds combine up to 65% equity arbitrage with ~35% debt, giving lower volatility and tax benefits only after a 2‑year holding (12.5% LTCG, otherwise taxed at the investor’s slab). Arbitrage thrives in volatile markets; income‑arbitrage benefits when volatility is moderate and interest rates fall (debt component gains). Risk factors include lack of equity volatility (reducing arbitrage opportunities) and interest‑rate/credit risk on the debt side. Allocation guidance: use pure arbitrage for funds needed in <2 years (minimum 6 months), and income‑arbitrage for 2‑3 year needs where stability is preferred. Liquid funds remain preferable for very short‑term cash needs (<6 months
