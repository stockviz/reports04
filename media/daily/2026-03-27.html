Buyback Tax Reform Boosts Wipro Prospects, Infosys Expands via Acquisitions

TCS WIPRO INFY

The April 1 tax change shifts buyback taxation from up to 35% dividend tax to a flat 12.5% capital‑gain tax plus a 12% surcharge, lowering the effective rate and making buybacks more attractive. In the IT sector, buyback activity fell sharply in 2025 after a peak in 2024, with no IT buybacks that year. Over the past decade, TCS, Wipro and Infosys have each executed about five buybacks; TCS returned ~₹57,000 crore and holds the largest cash reserve (~₹62,000 crore), followed by Wipro (~₹57,000 crore) and Infosys (~₹35,000 crore). Analysts view Wipro as the most likely candidate for a post‑fiscal‑year‑end buyback due to its sizable cash pile and high payout ratio. Meanwhile, Infosys is pursuing inorganic growth, acquiring Optimum Healthcare (≈₹4,500 crore, 1.3‑1.7% FY‑27 earnings boost) and Status Global (≈₹2,200 crore, ~0.2% boost), with further deals slated for Q1 FY‑27, positioning the firm for expanded services in healthcare and insurance technology

upGrad to acquire Unacademy at steep discount amid ed‑tech slowdown

upGrad is finalising an all‑stock acquisition of Unacademy at a valuation of roughly ₹2,055 crore ($245 million), a more than 90% markdown from Unacademy’s 2021 $3.4 billion valuation and below the $300‑$400 million range it previously offered. The deal translates to about 0.12 upGrad shares per Unacademy share and is expected to close in 2‑3 months pending regulatory approvals. UpGrad, valued at around ₹17,000 crore, sees Unacademy’s ₹100 million cash reserves and improved profitability as strategic assets. Unacademy, which has shifted back to an online‑first model and cut its cash burn dramatically, will retain its AI‑driven language platform Airlearn under the combined entity. The acquisition follows broader consolidation in a cooling Indian ed‑tech market, with upGrad also eyeing assets from Byju’s and planning an IPO by 2027

Federal Judge Grants Preliminary Injunction to Anthropic in Pentagon Dispute

A San Francisco judge temporarily halted the Pentagon’s ban on Anthropic’s AI models, issuing a preliminary injunction that blocks the government’s designation of the firm as a national‑security risk. The ruling questions the legality of the Trump administration’s directive and the Department of Defense’s supply‑chain risk label, which barred federal agencies from using Anthropic’s Claude models and forced contractors to certify non‑use. The case, involving statutes 10 U.S.C. § 3252 and 41 U.S.C. § 4713, centers on whether the government overstepped legal bounds rather than its right to choose vendors, and could set a precedent for AI regulation and executive authority

Asia Braces for War‑Driven Energy and Plastic Crisis

ADFFOODS

The Iran‑Israel conflict is disrupting the Strait of Hormuz, cutting supply of oil, gas and naphtha that Asian manufacturers rely on for plastics and petrochemicals. Raw‑material prices have jumped up to 50%, forcing factories in South Korea, China and Japan to cut output, stock‑pile, or halt production of items ranging from film and containers to toys, noodles, cosmetics and household goods. Companies such as Samyang Foods, Nongshim, Yonwoo and Michelin are scrambling for resin and rubber, while retailers report panic buying of plastic‑dependent products like garbage bags and ramen. The region’s heavy dependence on Middle‑East energy makes it especially vulnerable, and the shortages are expected to drive price hikes and further supply concerns through at least mid‑2024

Millennium Ends Multi‑Billion Partnership with Engineers Gate

AHLADA NUVAMA

Millennium Management is withdrawing its capital from Engineers Gate after a three‑year partnership, with the external manager ceasing to manage Millennium’s money by month‑end. Disagreements over liquidity terms—engineered to match Engineers Gate’s own fund—prevented a restructuring. While Millennium valued the mandate at $3.6 billion (including leverage), Engineers Gate’s figure was about $1.5 billion. Engineers Gate will replace Millennium’s capital, targeting roughly $4 billion in assets by April. This marks the end of one of Millennium’s largest external manager relationships
