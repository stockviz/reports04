Need To Think Beyond PLIs, Should Look At Employment-linked Incentives Instead: Envision Capital

5PAISA

The conversation centered on reforming India’s equity‑tax regime and aligning incentives with employment to boost long‑term capital inflows and manufacturing growth.

Equity‑tax reforms - Current long‑term capital gains (LTCG) tax (10 % after one year) and other taxes (STT, dividend tax, buy‑back tax) discourage patient investment. - Proposal: introduce three holding‑period brackets— ≤ 1 yr at 20 %, 1‑3 yr at 12.5 %, > 3 yr at 0 %—redefining “long‑term” to three years and eliminating tax beyond that. - Expected effects: * Domestic investors would be more willing to stay invested, potentially increasing allocations. * Foreign investors, who have been net sellers for five years, would face a lower tax drag, making Indian assets more attractive despite existing withholding tax on rupee gains. * Reducing the tax burden could help reverse the under‑weight stance of sovereign wealth funds and other foreign capital.

Policy and budget context - The budget is presented to Parliament by the President and then read by the Finance Minister; key officials include the Minister of State for Finance, the Revenue Secretary, and the Expenditure Secretary. - Survey data points to manufacturing as the priority sector for the upcoming budget.

Incentive shift - Current Production‑Linked Incentives (PLIs) should evolve into Employment‑Linked Incentives (ELIs), tying subsidies to formal job creation rather than output alone. - This would support the “entrepreneurial state” vision, expanding the total addressable market (TAM) from a domestic base to a global one.

Target sectors - High‑growth, “new‑age” manufacturing: defense, electronics, semiconductors. - Traditional export‑oriented industries: textiles, jewellery, etc., especially under new free‑trade agreements. - Overall emphasis on “ease of doing manufacturing” to complement broader ease‑of‑doing‑business reforms.

The speakers conclude that re‑taxing equity gains, shifting PLIs to employment‑linked models, and focusing on manufacturing will drive capital inflows, job creation, and India’s integration into global value chains.

Former Policymakers Outline Key Focus Areas & Expectations For Budget 2026 | Union Budget 2026

IDBI

Budget 2026 – Key Take‑aways from the former‑policymaker panel

  • Tax stability: No change expected in long‑term or short‑term capital gains taxes; the government is likely to maintain the “stability” theme after last year’s reforms.

  • FDI/FBI flows: Current outflows are driven by a strong dollar and modest net FDI. Short‑term tweaks won’t reverse the trend; a deeper mindset shift is needed.

  • Gold & silver duties: Raising import duties is possible but would be modest, aimed at revenue rather than deterrence, to avoid smuggling.

  • Disinvestment: Revival will be slow and limited; only small‑scale asset sales are expected, with no major bank privatizations this year.

  • Defence spending: Capital‑expenditure likely to rise 15‑20 % (or more) thanks to pending orders and emergency procurements; non‑salary revenue outlays will also increase.

  • Part B focus: Greater emphasis on medium‑term tax vision, customs rationalisation, and ease‑of‑doing‑business measures. Expect a push to cut tariff lines from 8 to about 4 and to address dispute resolution and investigation backlogs.

  • Fiscal space: Constrained this year after personal‑income‑tax and GST reliefs; overall budget growth likely to stay around 7‑9 % with limited headroom for new spending.

  • GST collections: Near ₹2 lakh crore for Jan 2026, signalling robust revenue despite the tighter fiscal environment.

Capex Reforms, Capital Gains Tax Rejig: What Are Big Market Voices Expecting From Union Budget 2026?

KOTAKBANK

Key take‑aways from the NSE‑led market round‑table on Union Budget 2026

  • Capex & infrastructure – Market participants want the Finance Minister to keep the 20 %‑ish capex‑growth target, sustain private‑investment‑friendly infrastructure spending, and avoid any surprise cuts.
  • Fiscal discipline – A lower fiscal deficit and a credible path to bring the debt‑to‑GDP ratio down to 50 % by 2031 are top priorities; the preferred mix is higher non‑tax revenue (asset monetisation, divestments) rather than new taxes.
  • NSE listing – The exchange’s valuation (≈ ₹5 trn) is not a concern for regulators; the focus is on enabling market growth and investor confidence.
  • Tax reforms & FPI incentives – Calls for:
    • Reducing the burden on foreign portfolio investors (tax exemptions for sovereign/ pension funds that are tax‑exempt abroad).
    • Simplifying the STT/Tobin tax regime, possibly lowering or rationalising STT to improve market liquidity.
  • Defence spending – An expected 15‑20 % rise would be priced‑in already; it is not viewed as a surprise catalyst but reinforces the sector’s growth narrative.
  • Policy stability – Investors seek a clear, medium‑term (3‑5 year) roadmap that backs growth aspirations with concrete reforms, especially in ease‑of‑doing‑business and entrepreneurship (e.g., “DG‑Yatra” for investment).
  • Market outlook – Over the next 12‑18 months, mid‑ and large‑cap stocks are expected to outperform small‑caps; valuations remain near historical averages for large caps, while small caps still carry a premium despite price declines.
  • Macro environment – Despite a weak rupee driven more by narrative than fundamentals, the macro framework (banking stability, fiscal prudence) is considered strong; no major negative surprises in the budget are essential for market confidence.
  • Overall wish‑list – 20 % capex growth, disciplined fiscal path, non‑tax revenue boost, targeted tax relief for FPIs, and a stable, long‑term reform agenda to revive “animal spirits” and sustain growth amid global uncertainty.

Key Takeaways from the “Charting India’s Macro Roadmap” preview of Union Budget 2026

  • Historical Milestone: Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman will present her ninth consecutive budget, the first FM to do so.

  • Economic backdrop:

    • Weak equity sentiment and a depreciating rupee, but consumption is improving due to recent GST cuts and income‑tax tweaks.
    • The government aims to keep fiscal deficit within its target while boosting growth.

  • Structural changes in the presentation:

    • Part B of the budget speech will receive far more detail than before, covering both short‑ and long‑term goals and India’s global positioning.

  • Spending outlook:

    • Overall capital expenditure (CapEx) expected to rise 5‑6 % YoY.
    • Defense budget (K‑PEX) projected to grow 15‑20 %, though overall K‑PEX may stay in single‑digit growth.
    • Continued emphasis on infrastructure, health, and education.

  • Tax‑policy signals:

    • Possible abolition of the Securities Transaction Tax (STT).
    • Rationalisation of customs duties, cutting tariff slabs from eight to four.
    • Anticipated modest tweaks to long‑term capital‑gains tax (potential 10‑15 bp reduction) and possible adjustments to LTCG for foreign institutional investors.
    • No major consumption‑tax cuts expected; focus remains on channeling more disposable income to the middle class via direct transfers.

  • Manufacturing & Make‑in‑India:

    • Likely expansion of the Production‑Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme to deepen the manufacturing push.

  • Market expectations & risks:

    • Investors hope for measures that will curb FII outflows and stabilize the rupee.
    • Concerns over fiscal space given rising interest outlays and upcoming Finance Commission recommendations.
    • Analysts watch key stocks (ITC, defense names, railways, pharma, auto, financials) for budget‑driven moves.
    • Technical outlook for Nifty: support around 25,200–25,300; upside targets near 26,370 if the market breaks recent resistance.

  • Strategic focus:

    • “Reform, Perform, Transform” – balancing growth stimulation, consumption support, and fiscal discipline.
    • Emphasis on predictability, continuity, and stability of policy to attract long‑term capital.

Overall, the budget is expected to maintain a prudent fiscal stance while delivering a modest CapEx boost, a strong defense allocation, incremental tax rationalisation, and continued support for the Make‑in‑India agenda. The market’s reaction will hinge on how convincingly the government addresses rupee weakness, FII outflows, and the balance between growth‑driving spend and fiscal consolidation.
