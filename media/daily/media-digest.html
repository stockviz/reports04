Morgan Stanley Downgrades India Amid Oil‑Gas Risks and Uncertain Outlook

ICICIBANK AIRTELPP RELIANCE

Morgan Stanley’s chief Asia emerging equity strategist Jonathan Garner explained that India was moved to an equal‑weight rating in early March due to its high sensitivity to oil and gas imports (third‑highest among its peers) and the current uncertainty around Gulf supply disruptions. While India showed improving PMIs and policy easing, rising Brent crude—currently $90‑100 per barrel, with adverse scenarios of $120‑130—could drive a further 15‑20% equity downside across Asia. The firm foresees very wide outcome ranges, refraining from firm return forecasts, and emphasizes scenario analysis over base‑case assumptions. AI‑driven semiconductor growth in Korea and Taiwan is outpacing India’s IT services, and hard‑asset themes (copper, other metals, energy) remain bullish. Morgan Stanley maintains small overweights in Brazil, Japan, and Singapore, and keeps three core India stocks—Reliance Industries, ICICI Bank, and Bharti Airtel—on its emerging‑market focus list, while noting that India’s valuation premium and underperformance do not provide a cushion against the projected risks. Gold is viewed as a “solid hold” amid broader macro uncertainty

Why India’s Largest Caps Are Underperforming

KOTAKBANK ASIANPAINT IDEA INFY DABUR TCS WIPRO HDFCLIFE

A recent analysis of India’s 333 large‑cap stocks (market cap > ₹30,000 cr) shows that 32 have delivered negative returns and about 80 have lagged the benchmark over the past five years. The ten biggest losers—TCS, Infosys, Kotak Mahindra Bank, Asian Paints, Wipro, HDFC Life, Vodafone Idea, SRF, Shriram Cement and Dauber—have all underperformed despite being Nifty‑50 constituents. Key drivers include: IT firms suffering from a global tech‑spending slowdown and AI‑related concerns; consumer brands facing weak demand, fierce competition and inflated 2021 valuations; banks grappling with slower growth, higher valuations, leadership changes and RBI regulatory actions; paints and cement firms hit by margin pressure, new entrants and excess capacity; and insurers dealing with regulatory challenges and thin VNB margins. The findings highlight that simply holding blue‑chip large‑caps over the long term does not guarantee superior returns, underscoring the need for active investment approaches
