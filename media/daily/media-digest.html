The Supreme Court struck down President Trump’s broad “national emergency” tariff authority, forcing the administration to rely on narrower tools such as the rarely used Section 122 of the 1974 Trade Act (10‑15 % global tariffs for up to 150 days) and sector‑specific investigations under Sections 232, 301 and 201. Panelists debated the implications: Michael Kugelman and Ravinder Sajdev see the move as an escalation—Trump will continue to pursue high tariffs despite the court’s decision, using a patchwork of legal mechanisms to reach an 18‑20 % average levy on India. Ambassador Mahesh Sajdev stressed that the duties follow WTO‑sanctioned anti‑dumping/anti‑subsidy procedures and are not arbitrary. Jan Krishna highlighted the myriad statutory avenues (122, 232, 301, 201, 238) the administration can exploit, suggesting limited legal push‑back. The impact on Indian exporters could be severe: solar export values could drop from a projected $1 bn to under $800 m, while domestic capacity exceeds local demand, forcing firms to seek alternative markets such as the EU. Other sectors—steel, aluminium, copper, auto parts—face 25‑50 % tariffs under Section 232, compounding pressure. The panel agreed that the next 150 days will determine whether these measures are a temporary tremor or a lasting reset in U.S.–India trade, with broader consequences for U.S. allies and for China’s role as a potential alternative trading partner
