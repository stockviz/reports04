5PAISA IDEA

Title: Dinshaw Irani on India’s Post‑War Earnings Surge, Consumption‑Driven Opportunities, and Market Outlook

Summary: Dinshaw Irani says India’s Q1 earnings have rebounded sharply – mid‑caps up ~27%, small‑caps ~17% and large‑caps ~11% – thanks to resilient demand and firms passing higher raw‑material costs to consumers. He highlights discretionary and “new‑age” consumption as the most attractive themes, with a shift from banks to mid‑ and small‑caps. Government fiscal space remains ample, with modest borrowing and stable inflation, allowing private‑sector capex, especially in capacity expansion (KPX). Compared with Taiwan’s semiconductor‑driven market rise, Irani believes India’s growth will depend on corporate performance, crude price moderation, and resolution of the Gulf crisis. He expects oil prices to settle around $80‑85/barrel, limiting cost pressures, and foresees modest but steady market returns (12‑13% annual) rather than dramatic upswings.

SUZLON

Suzlon reported a robust FY26 with revenue up 54% to ₹16,700 crore and EBITDA rising 63% to ₹3,022 crore, but margins fell about 200 bps to roughly 17% versus the 19% consensus. The CFO attributed the dip to commodity‑led inflation, especially steel, noting most contracts are indexed and costs can be passed on. The order book stands at ~6 GW, 66% from PSUs and commercial‑industrial customers, and is expected to grow further. Cash flow is improving, with a target to tighten the cash conversion cycle by FY27‑end, while receivables will rise with growth. Export opportunities, highlighted by the new Blue Sky platform for Europe’s repowering market, are expected to materialise mainly in FY28. No specific guidance on margins, order inflow or revenue targets was provided, but the company remains confident of sustaining growth and enhancing profitability

Fuel Price Hikes Not Over Yet? Former HPCL CMD MK Surana Warns Of More Pain Ahead

Former HPCL chairman M.K. Surana explained that while recent price hikes have reduced daily losses for oil marketing companies from about ₹1,000 crore to a little over ₹600 crore, several factors keep future hikes likely. He highlighted timing gaps between crude purchases and sales, accumulated under‑recoveries that persist despite price cuts, and the uncertainty of crude and dollar price movements. Surana noted that government hikes aim to protect consumers but are constrained by geopolitical risks—especially the ongoing US‑Iran conflict—and volatile global oil prices. Unless crude prices stay benign and under‑recoveries are gradually cleared, OMCs have limited “elbow room,” and further price adjustments may be inevitable
