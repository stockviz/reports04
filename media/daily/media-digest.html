India-EU FTA | European Brands Like Skoda, Volkswagen, BMW & Audi Stand To Gain: Goldman Sachs

BMWVENTLTD TATAMOTORS M&M HYUNDAI

The EU‑India FTA will sharply cut import duties on European cars priced above €15,000. Current tariffs of 60‑110 % will fall to 40 % immediately, with a glide path to 10 % for up to 200,000 units annually. This reduces the ex‑showroom price of a €15,000 car to about ₹23.2 lakh. European premium brands (Skoda, VW, Renault, Mercedes, BMW, Audi, etc.) currently hold ~3.7 % of the Indian market; sales of cars above ₹23 lakh accounted for 8.2 % of domestic volumes in FY 26’s first nine months. The tariff cut mainly affects high‑price premium models, benefitting European makers. Domestic rivals with premium SUVs—Hyundai (≈3.3 % impact) and Mahindra & Mahindra (≈2 % impact)—will feel the most pressure, while Tata’s exposure is minimal. Electric vehicles are excluded from tariff reductions for the first five years to protect the Indian EV sector.

Expert Analysis On India EU’s 80 Bn Services Trade | Why It Matters For India

PGIL

The panel highlighted the strategic importance of the pending EU‑India free‑trade agreement, which would unlock about $80 bn in services trade and cement Europe as India’s largest single market bloc. Ambassador Mohan Kumar stressed that the deal—India’s most comprehensive FTA to date, covering government procurement, labor, environment and digital trade—has been two decades in the making and is crucial as multilateral trade falters and reciprocal, strategic agreements become the norm. It will help India de‑risk its supply chain away from China, complement the still‑under‑negotiated US deal, and diversify exports beyond the limited partners that already exceed $100 bn in bilateral trade.

Pallab Banerjee of Pall Global Industries noted that tariff disadvantages have pushed European buyers toward Bangladesh and Vietnam, but the EU‑India FTA would level the playing field, prompting firms to shift sourcing to India. He said his company is already preparing—investing in capacity and aligning with EU customers—anticipating a one‑year timeline before the agreement is signed.

Both speakers agreed that market sentiment will drive investment even before the ink dries, and that greater competition from Europe will push Indian manufacturing toward the 25 %‑of‑GDP target needed for a $10 trillion economy. The EU deal is seen as a “whole‑different ballgame” compared with other FTAs (e.g., Oman, New Zealand), and is essential alongside the US, ASEAN, China and GCC partnerships for achieving India’s growth ambitions.
