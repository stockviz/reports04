DGCA Introduces Faster Refunds and Free Name Corrections for Direct Bookings

INDIGO

The DGCA has mandated that airlines must issue full ticket refunds within 14 days, with cash refunds processed within 24 hours for cancellations made within 24 hours of purchase. For tickets booked directly on airline websites, airlines must allow free name corrections within a 48‑hour “look‑in” window and cannot charge for these changes. These provisions do not apply to domestic flights departing within seven days or international flights within 15 days. For bookings via agents or third‑party sites, the 14‑day refund timeline remains. The rules, aimed at addressing past refund complaints, take effect on 26 March 2026

India’s D2C luggage boom draws VC money as travel spikes

COMSYN VIPIND SAFARI

A surge in domestic and overseas travel has turned India’s direct‑to‑consumer luggage market into a hot spot for venture capital. The tourism data shows a 17.5% rise in domestic trips and a 10.8% rise in overseas trips in 2024. Startups such as Uppercase (Bengaluru) are seeking $20 million in Series C to expand offline stores and enter Europe, aiming for EBITDA positivity by FY28. Other D2C brands—Escape Plan, EUME, Nasher Miles, ICON Bags, Mokobara—have raised between $1.2 million and $25 million in recent rounds. Analysts estimate the domestic market at ₹20‑25 trillion, growing 12‑14% CAGR, driven by frequent short trips that shorten luggage replacement cycles. Yet per‑capita consumption remains low, leaving room for growth as first‑time buyers shift to organized, design‑focused brands. Legacy players like VIP Industries and Safari Industries face declining sales and margin pressure due to outdated designs, cost spikes, and supply‑chain disruptions. Experts warn that scaling luggage brands is capital‑intensive and requires strong retail presence, not just digital marketing. Uppercase favors a manufacturing‑led model with recycled Indian material to control costs and margins, while legacy firms struggle to adapt. The sector’s investor appeal hinges on the gap between low‑priced functional luggage and premium design‑led offerings

Soaring Whey Prices Prompt Indian Supplement Makers to Shift Toward Plant‑Based Proteins and New Channels

HEADSUP MARICO ITC SWIGGY BCG ZYDUSWELL

Rising whey costs—up 70% to ~₹3,000/kg—are squeezing margins in India’s fast‑growing protein supplement market, forcing manufacturers to rethink growth strategies. While premium whey retains pricing power, mass‑market players face elasticity issues, especially in tier‑2/3 regions, and cannot fully pass on price hikes due to price‑sensitivity and forex volatility. Protein demand is becoming mainstream, expanding beyond athletes to health‑conscious consumers, driving a market projected to grow from ₹7,461 crore in 2024 to ₹13,186 crore by 2033. Companies are diversifying: large FMCG firms launch protein‑fortified foods; investors back plant‑based supplement startups; and brands pursue institutional (B2B) partnerships and quick‑commerce channels to secure steadier revenue. The shift to plant‑based proteins (peas, rice, soy) aims to reduce reliance on volatile dairy whey, improve taste/texture, and broaden consumer appeal, while premium and private‑label whey products continue to serve different market segments
