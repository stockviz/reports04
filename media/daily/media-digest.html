Anthropic Blow, AI Disruption & The Road Ahead For Indian IT Companies | Srikanth Velamakanni

LATENTVIEW INFY

The discussion centers on how recent AI releases from Anthropic and Palantir could disrupt Indian IT services. Srikanth Velamakanni of Fractal Analytics explains that AI will compress labor‑intensive work, threatening the traditional time‑and‑material and staff‑augmentation revenue models of large Indian IT firms. However, this threat is partly overstated: most Indian IT projects involve complex, structured‑data transformations (e.g., SAP and cloud migrations) that AI tools are not yet able to replace fully.

Key points:

  • Short‑term impact – Limited; enterprises with complex, multi‑country operations still need extensive consulting and integration services.
  • Medium‑to‑long‑term impact – Significant, as AI can automate many tasks, reducing headcount requirements and eroding margin‑heavy, low‑value‑add models.
  • Jevons paradox – Lower AI‑driven costs may boost overall demand for tech services, creating new opportunities if firms shift to outcome‑based, subscription or licensing models.
  • Transformation imperative – Indian IT players must upskill talent, especially mid‑senior staff, adopt AI‑centric delivery, and secure “street permission” to invest in a multi‑year reinvention (e.g., Infosys‑Cursor partnership).
  • Who will win? – Larger firms have talent depth but face legacy inertia; mid‑size, more agile firms are likely to adapt faster, while younger hires already comfortable with AI tools reduce the entry‑level training burden.

Overall, Indian IT services are not in immediate danger of extinction, but they must rapidly evolve their business models and skill sets to stay relevant in an AI‑augmented future.

Valuations Of Indian IT Are Cheaper Compared To Global SaaS Players: Allspring Global Investments

DHUNINV

Indian IT stocks are currently undervalued, with sentiment overly bearish due to AI hype that is unlikely to materialize immediately. Growth remains muted, and the sector’s main risk is weak top‑line expansion rather than valuation pressure. Compared with U.S. SaaS firms, Indian IT services trade at much lower multiples, pay dividends, and offer a more attractive entry point for foreign investors seeking a structural growth story outside the high‑CAPEX, semiconductor‑driven rally in Korea and Taiwan. The market’s direction will hinge on whether AI adoption accelerates in the second half of the year and on broader macro risks—rising global CAPEX, potential inflation from overheating, and geopolitical volatility—which could affect commodity prices and corporate margins.
