The Macro Signal | Do Wars Actually Move Oil Prices? What’s The Impact On US Strikes On Venezuela?

The text argues that headlines alone rarely move oil markets; only genuine supply disruptions do. U.S. sanctions on Venezuela (2015‑2019) coincided with a long‑term production decline, so oil prices didn’t spike. Historical wars have sometimes driven prices up (1973 embargo, 1979 Iranian revolution, 1990 Gulf War, 2022 Russia‑Ukraine war), but many conflicts (e.g., 2003 Iraq, Israel‑Hamas flare‑ups) caused only brief spikes. Venezuela, despite holding the world’s largest proven reserves, now produces under 1 % of global supply, so its further decline cannot trigger a major oil shock. Larger producers such as Saudi Arabia, Russia, and Iran are the real risk factors. Any U.S. action that increases regional supply could modestly suppress prices, limiting inflation risk, while bolstering the dollar and affecting safe‑haven assets (gold, silver) and equities. Overall, U.S. pressure on Venezuela signals geopolitical strength but is unlikely to cause a sustained oil‑price upheaval.
