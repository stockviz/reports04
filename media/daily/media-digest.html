Government‑Backed NBFCs Seek RBI Exemption from Upper‑Layer Classification

INDIASHLTR PFC BIRLACORPN

Large government‑owned NBFCs such as Power Finance Corporation, Rural Electrification Corp and IRFC, each with assets over ₹1 lakh crore, are requesting exemption from the RBI’s new “upper‑layer” NBFC category. Under the draft norms, these entities would face a 25% cap on exposure to any single borrower—a constraint that conflicts with their business model of large‑ticket infra financing (e.g., IRFC’s sole borrower is Indian Railways). While the RBI permits hiving off loans to separate NBFCs to stay below the asset threshold, doing so would undermine the purpose of the classification. Consequently, these government‑backed NBFCs are lobbying the RBI to be excluded from the upper‑layer rules

India bans sugar exports till September 2026 amid low crop forecasts and tight stocks

India announced an immediate ban on exports of raw, white and refined sugar until September 2026, allowing only shipments already in the pipeline. Crop estimates have been revised down to 27.5 million tonnes from earlier 32‑33 million tonnes due to erratic monsoon, poor yields in Karnataka and Maharashtra, and concerns over the upcoming rabi crop. Closing stocks are far below the expected 5‑8 lakh tonnes, with only about 7‑8 lakh tonnes of the 15 lakh tonne export quota contracted, leaving 6.5‑7 lakh tonnes at risk. The move lifted international raw sugar prices by ~2% and London white sugar by ~3%. India’s recent export volumes have been around 8 lakh tonnes (2023) and 7.7 lakh tonnes (2022) despite higher quotas. Meanwhile, the Fair Remunerative Price (FRP) for cane has risen to ₹355 per quintal, while the sugar MSP has been unchanged since 2018, squeezing mill margins. A global sugar deficit of 3.7 million tonnes (DataGrowth) and higher crude oil prices boosting ethanol demand are also supporting price gains

India’s Sugar Outlook: Lower Production, Limited Export Impact, and Ethanol Pricing Challenges

MAWANASUG

Director Atul Chaturvedi of Sriyankar Sugars says the sharp drop in India’s sugar output to about 27.75 million tons—well below earlier forecasts of over 31 million—won’t materially affect earnings because domestic consumption roughly matches production and export volumes remain modest (≈ 8–9 lakh tons of the 1.5 million‑ton quota). Closing stocks are expected to fall to around 3.5 million tons from the anticipated 6 million, but this doesn’t threaten the industry’s health and may even support prices. On ethanol, the sector sees no progress in price parity with maize‑based ethanol (currently ₹72 per litre versus ₹65 for sugarcane juice ethanol). The current sugar‑to‑ethanol diversion season is over, with the next cycle starting Oct‑Nov, and the government faces a tight balancing act between fuel costs and food price stability
