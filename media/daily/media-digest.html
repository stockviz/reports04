Macros, Markets & The Budget Playbook | 2026 Market Outlook | CNBC TV18 Market Forum 2026-01-18 12:02

KRBL BEL

Key Take‑aways from the CNBC TV18 Market Forum (18 Jan 2026)

Macroeconomic backdrop
- China’s excess capacity remains entrenched, feeding global disinflation, especially in Asia.
- Oil market fundamentals point to further price declines, not upside.
- Inflation outlook: core inflation expected to stay low (2‑3 %).
- Growth projections: real GDP ≈ 6.5 % YoY; nominal GDP 9‑10 % (≈ 10.5 % if a 7.5 % real rate meets a 3.5 % price rise).
- Fiscal‑debt dynamics: Government will likely keep the nominal‑GDP‑growth‑linked budget target at ~10 % of GDP. Debt‑to‑GDP consolidation aim is 50 %; lower nominal growth forces tighter primary consolidation.

Metals outlook
- Precious metals (gold, silver) are a long‑run hedge against currency debasement and should outperform over a 3‑5 year horizon, though current sentiment is muted.
- Base metals:
- Copper, zinc, lead: Expect continued price gains as investors hedge against competitive currency debasement.
- Aluminium: Vulnerable to falling energy costs; price sustainability in doubt.
- Ferrous (iron‑ore): Currently over‑priced (~$100+); bullwhip‑effect inventory build‑up will likely reverse, leading to a price decline.

Credit and earnings
- Credit growth surged in late‑December (Fortnight‑over‑Fortnight jump from 0.8 % to 3 %, ~₹6.2 trn disbursed). This is turning a former “vicious cycle” into a “virtuous cycle”.
- Earnings growth is expected to rebound to double‑digit levels, with market pricing around 12‑15 % YoY, driven by stronger credit and consumption.

Growth drivers shift
- Early post‑pandemic momentum from government capex and real‑estate is waning.
- New drivers: consumer durables (autos, rural demand), private capex (data‑centres, renewables), and broader consumption.
- The sustainability of this consumption‑led lift over the next three quarters will be crucial for a private‑investment‑led capex expansion.

Budget & reform expectations
- Fiscal stance: Continued discipline; faster consolidation before the FY27 pay‑commission settlement.
- Reforms focus:
- Deregulation (internal & external) and customs simplification to unlock FTAs.
- Streamlining import duties to complement export‑led, labour‑intensive growth.
- Infrastructure: Calls for stronger road‑capex and urban‑infrastructure spending.

US‑India trade deal
- Panelists deem the pending U.S.‑India trade agreement largely irrelevant to India’s growth outlook; macro fundamentals and domestic reforms dominate the forecast.

Overall sentiment
- Despite geopolitical and supply‑chain headwinds, the consensus is that India can sustain double‑digit nominal growth, improve earnings momentum, and advance a reform‑driven growth model over the next fiscal year.

How India Can Embrace AI | Former Infosys CEO Vishal Sikka Exclusive | Global Lens 2026-01-18 10:03

INFY

Summary of Vishal Sikka’s interview on India’s AI future

  • Strategic Imperative: India must be present at every layer of the AI stack—building its own foundation models, tooling, and applications—and scale usage to hundreds of millions of people to stay globally relevant.

  • Skill Gap:

    • Globally only ~1.5 % of the population can build AI applications; <0.001 % can develop foundation models.
    • India needs to rapidly expand AI literacy from a “few‑million” level to a mass‑adoption level, training both developers and end‑users to understand AI’s capabilities and limits.

  • Education & Upskilling: Partnerships with institutions like Stanford’s AI Center, large‑scale training programs, and curriculum changes are essential to democratize foundation‑model knowledge.

  • Disruption Timeline: AI is already deployed (LLMs, code generators). The window to adapt is now; waiting years is impossible. Companies must re‑engineer processes today.

  • Impact on IT Services:

    • Traditional software development and maintenance can be done with far fewer humans using AI.
    • Indian services firms must transition to product‑centric models—owning the AI platform that powers the work and monetizing it as software, outcome‑based services, or SaaS, rather than pure labor.

  • Valuation Reality Check: Current AI company valuations far exceed present revenue streams (e.g., OpenAI’s $15 B revenue vs. $800 B infrastructure spend). A market correction is likely; sustainable growth depends on real, monetizable applications.

  • Lessons from Past Bets:

    • Small, high‑risk bets on emerging tech (OpenAI, LinkedIn) can yield massive returns.
    • Maintain a “1‑2 %” allocation for speculative, high‑impact experiments.

  • India’s Competitive Advantages:

    • Demographic dividend: a large, educated, tech‑savvy youth population.
    • Cultural emphasis on knowledge and a rich multilingual heritage that can fuel specialized foundation models (e.g., low‑resource Indian languages, domain‑specific models for healthcare, finance, “world models” of Indian geography and infrastructure).

  • Future Opportunities:

    • Vertical foundation models tailored to industry domains and Indian contexts.
    • World models that encode local physical and social environments.
    • Building a home‑grown large‑scale foundation model is still feasible; India is in the “first over” of a T20 match with many overs left.

  • Call to Action: Accelerate AI education, create indigenous foundation models, shift IT services toward AI‑enabled product offerings, and leverage India’s talent and scale to become a global AI leader.

Sensex & Nifty End The Week Flat While Broader Markets Outperform | Editors’ Roundtable 2026-01-18 09:04

INFY SMARTWORKS HCL-INSYS HINDALCO HINDCOPPER VEDL TCS AXISBANK

  • Market backdrop: Sensex and Nifty ended the week flat; broader Indian markets outperformed the benchmark.

  • IT services:

    • AI revenues are rising (HCL +20% QoQ, TCS +20% QoQ) but still a small share (HCL ~4%, TCS ~6%).
    • Growth for large‑cap IT firms remains single‑digit (3‑3.5% FY‑24), with street forecasts of 5‑8% for FY‑25.
    • Valuations are modest (Infosys ≈22× earnings, slightly above TCS).
    • Panelists favor mid‑cap, higher‑growth IT names over large caps, citing the need for organic growth and strategic acquisitions rather than buybacks.

  • Metals & commodities:

    • Precious metals (silver, platinum) up 18‑25% in ~20 days; industrial metals also rally on supply shortages and strong demand.
    • China’s gold purchases and export restrictions on silver support prices.
    • Hindustan Copper trading ~50× earnings; Hindustan Silver at ~12×, both pricey but attracting interest.
    • Aluminium sees upside from energy costs and AI‑driven power demand; related stocks (Nalco, Hindalco, Vidanta) are being held tactically.
    • Copper, zinc and aluminium exposure is justified by AI, EV and renewable‑energy demand.

  • Gold‑financing & exchanges:

    • Higher gold prices expand loan‑to‑value, letting financiers lend more; valuation is premium but tailwinds are strong.
    • Exchanges benefit from surging precious‑metal trading volumes.
    • Preference for large, well‑capitalised gold‑financing firms and major exchanges, not regional challengers.

  • Infrastructure:

    • Road‑heavy players have already absorbed most downside; future focus likely on power transmission & distribution (T&D), ports and defence.
    • Expect a re‑allocation of capex away from roads toward these priority segments.

  • Small‑ and mid‑cap performance:

    • Small‑cap indices lagged broader markets, but selective stock‑picking delivered strong results.
    • Portfolio targets >30‑35% EPS growth with ~30× P/E, yielding attractive PEG ratios.
    • Successful picks include niche private banks (MSME & gold‑financing exposure) and high‑growth mid‑caps (e.g., SmartWorks).

  • Financials:

    • Under‑performance creates valuation headroom; fresh capital should be steered into both large‑cap and mid‑cap financials (banks, brokerages, AMCs) as the sector recovers.

  • Real estate:

    • Listed developers, largely in high‑ticket segments, are still lagging the market; a stock‑market rally would likely revive their performance.

  • Key themes & outlook:

    • AI is a catalyst for metals and power‑related demand.
    • Mid‑cap/small‑cap growth stories with solid fundamentals and reasonable valuations remain the preferred play.
    • Structural shifts (government capex priority, FDI into finance, commodity‑price dynamics) will shape sector rotation over the next 3‑5 years.
