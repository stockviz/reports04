Bitcoin’s Unique Role in a Tokenized World

SWANCORP

Corey Clipston, CEO of Swan Bitcoin, explains that Bitcoin stands apart from tokenized assets like gold or oil because it is a fully decentralized digital money not tied to any physical commodity or centralized custodian, making it uncensorable and non‑reversible. He argues that inflation is fundamentally monetary—driven by excessive money printing—and that Bitcoin serves as both an inflation hedge and a potential global store of value, outpacing the dollar and gold over time. Clipston sees a three‑horse race among the dollar, gold, and Bitcoin, with Bitcoin likely to become the dominant global store of value in the long run. He dismisses most altcoins as lacking monetary purpose, likening them to company‑specific tokens with limited utility. Regarding institutional involvement, he notes that while Bitcoin ETFs attract inflows, true value lies in self‑custody of on‑chain Bitcoin; Swan Bitcoin recently launched Swan RBX to help families and businesses move from ETFs to direct Bitcoin ownership without triggering capital gains, emphasizing education on self‑custody and the risks of custodial solutions

India widens solar cell localisation mandate, ending arbitrage for cell‑selling manufacturers

TATAPOWER EMMVEE JWL WAAREEENER PREMIERENE SURANASOL

Indian solar cell makers have been profiting by selling domestically produced cells to module manufacturers that rely on imports, earning ~14‑15% higher EBITDA than assembling DCR‑compliant modules. A shortage of local cell capacity has driven up DCR module prices, creating a lucrative arbitrage. However, from 1 June the government extended the domestic‑content requirement to all government‑backed projects, including utility‑scale tenders after August 2025, and to net‑metering/open‑access projects from 1 January 2027. This will eliminate the market for non‑DCR modules and end the arbitrage, leaving only integrated firms with captive cell capacity (e.g., Premier Energies, Tata Power, Adani Solar, ReNew, Emmvee) to serve the expanding DCR‑driven market. The shift underscores a move toward well‑capitalised, vertically integrated manufacturers as India’s solar cell ecosystem matures
