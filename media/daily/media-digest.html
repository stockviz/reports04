Beyond the SpaceX Hype: Key Investor Questions on Valuation, Governance, and Growth Risks

HEALTHX

The interview with Columbia Business School professor Shivarram Rajkopal dissects SpaceX’s IPO, highlighting the massive $1.77 trillion valuation that rests largely on a $25 trillion total addressable market—only $2 trillion of which is space, the rest tied to AI, data centers, and other ventures. Rajkopal stresses that investors must scrutinize the conglomerate’s disparate businesses—Starlink’s modest but profitable $4‑5 billion operating income, the loss‑making X (formerly Twitter), XAI, and other speculative projects—rather than ride hype. He warns of governance risks: Musk controls ~40 % of equity but 80 % of voting power, with limited independent oversight and high “key‑man” risk. Transparency will improve post‑IPO, but investors need to demand rigorous disclosure to avoid blindly buying a “Musk token.” The discussion also touches on aggressive capital spending versus current revenue, comparing the scenario to Amazon’s early AWS investments, and questions whether SpaceX can deliver long‑term returns or becomes a speculative lottery ticket given Musk’s age and the uncertain timeline for orbital data centers, AI integration, and interplanetary ambitions

Edelweiss CIO Warns Against Chasing High‑Yield Bonds

EDELWEISS

In a NDTV Profit interview, CIO Dhaval Dalal explains bond fundamentals—coupon, maturity, yield‑to‑maturity—and the two main risks investors face: interest‑rate risk (price falls when rates rise) and credit risk (default). He stresses the importance of understanding macro‑economics, sector health, company fundamentals, and credit‑rating nuances, while noting that ratings are only opinions and require independent due diligence. Dalal outlines the bond‑holder’s priority in insolvency, the concept of “haircuts,” and the need for continuous monitoring of issuers. With global and domestic rate‑cut cycles ending and inflation pressures rising, he advises investors to keep portfolios short‑dated (2‑year + bonds) to limit price volatility, shifting to longer durations only when a falling‑rate environment is expected
