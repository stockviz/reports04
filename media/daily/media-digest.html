Social Media Ban: Protecting Children Or Policing Screens?

The panel discussed whether a legal ban on social‑media use for children under 16 is justified.

Key arguments for a ban
 Dr Hari Shetty (psychiatrist) cited data linking >3 hours of daily use to doubled anxiety/depression risk, sleep loss, and long‑term health harms, arguing that strong action is needed despite imperfect enforcement.
 He pointed to Australia’s and France’s blanket bans and heavy fines as necessary deterrents for platforms that ignore child‑safety obligations.

Key arguments against a ban
 Anshul Tiwari (Youth Ki Avaaz) warned that bans are blunt, easy to circumvent with VPNs/false IDs, and may push teens into unregulated corners of the internet, while also burdening platforms with disproportionate fines.
 Will Broad George (school founder) stressed that social media is integral to adolescent identity, agency, and community building; a total ban would increase social isolation, ignore the need for digital literacy, and place schools in a “digital police” role.
* Benjamin Ram (journalist) noted that bans rarely work; they drive users to other platforms and fail to address the underlying problem. He cited China’s age‑based time limits and content guards as a more nuanced approach.

Common ground
 All agreed that children need protection from harms such as cyber‑bullying, grooming, and addictive design.
 There is consensus that regulation—age‑verified parental consent, safe‑by‑design features, algorithmic limits, and digital‑literacy education—offers a more effective, proportionate solution than an outright prohibition.
* A pilot‑style, time‑bound ban with built‑in review (as being considered in Goa and Andhra Pradesh) was suggested as a possible compromise to test impact while allowing adjustments.

Conclusion
The panel leaned toward targeted, enforceable safeguards and education over a blanket ban, arguing that nuanced policy, platform accountability, and parental involvement are more likely to protect children’s mental health without stifling their agency or driving them to unsafe online spaces.
