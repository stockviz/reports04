AI Upskilling Extends to India’s Blue‑Collar Workforce

INFY LTIM HARRMALAYA RPGLIFE TATAMOTORS TATASTEEL TCS CEATLTD PERSISTENT WIPRO

Indian companies are expanding AI training beyond white‑collar staff to blue‑collar shop‑floor workers in sectors such as steel, automotive, tea, rubber, and tyres. Initiatives include teaching Python and data‑analytics basics to improve predictive maintenance, safety monitoring, quality control, and energy efficiency, aiming to cut cycle times, scrap, and human error. Major firms like Tata Steel, Tata Motors, and the RPG Group are piloting AI‑driven safety alerts and productivity tools, while IT giants are creating dedicated AI specialist roles. Despite AI’s impact on white‑collar job structures, blue‑collar adoption remains focused on augmenting tasks rather than replacing workers, with training seen as less steep and increasingly urgent

Sahi Secures $33 Million, Valuation Triples to $200 Million

FISCHER 5PAISA 3MINDIA SPARC GLOBAL BIOCON SWIGGY KOTAKBANK LUPIN HALDER

Sahi, a new‑age Indian broking platform founded in Dec 2024 by ex‑Swiggy CTO Dale Vaz and former Kotak Securities executive Manish Jain, raised $33 million—$20 million led by Accel Global and the remainder from existing backers Elevation Capital and Accel Partners India—pushing its valuation to about $200 million, up from $60 million a year earlier. In 18 months, the firm has onboarded ~400,000 demat accounts, placing it among India’s top‑30 brokers. The funding will fuel user‑base growth, entry into commodities, margin funding, mutual funds, and the development of AI‑driven, personalized trading features. Sahi’s edge stems from proprietary charting (vs. third‑party charts) and a 50 % lower brokerage fee (₹10 per order vs. market ₹20). Though still loss‑making, the company aims to capture market share, leveraging its AI‑native, lean‑team model. Investors see potential but caution that AI disruption in broking will be gradual, building atop existing trust, distribution, and regulatory rails

Elon Musk Testifies He Founded OpenAI as a Nonprofit to Counter Google

Elon Musk took the stand in Oakland, claiming he created OpenAI in 2015 as a nonprofit to provide a safety‑focused, open‑source alternative to Google’s AI efforts. He said he coined the idea, name, recruited key staff, and provided initial funding, insisting the charter barred private gain. Musk portrayed his motive as safeguarding humanity, warning that looting a charity would damage philanthropy nationwide. OpenAI’s lawyers countered that Musk’s real goal was control and profit, not safety. The trial, which could reshape AI governance and nonprofit law, continues with more witnesses expected

Purdue Pharma Set to Dissolve After Criminal Sentence Clears Path for $10 Billion Settlement

AJANTPHARM

Purdue Pharma’s criminal plea, pending a judge’s sentence, will enable a sweeping settlement that forces the company’s dissolution and the creation of a new public‑benefit entity, Knoa Pharma. The deal includes $8.3 billion in federal forfeitures, a $225 million government payment, and up to $7 billion from Sackler family members over 15 years, with $8,000‑$16,000 payouts to individual victims. While many survivors hope the settlement brings closure, some argue it falls short of true justice, citing limited restitution and the protection of the Sackler family from further lawsuits. The settlement also requires the release of internal documents and the removal of the Sackler name from institutions

Elon Musk Sues OpenAI Over Control and Mission Shift

Elon Musk has filed a $150 billion lawsuit against OpenAI, its CEO Sam Altman, and President Greg Brockman, alleging they betrayed the original nonprofit mission and turned the organization into a profit‑driven entity. Musk claims he funded OpenAI’s early development and now seeks to revert it to a nonprofit, remove Altman and Brockman from leadership, and divert damages to its charitable arm. OpenAI’s lawyers argue Musk pushed for a for‑profit structure to secure funding and talent, while Musk’s counsel says the company and investors, notably Microsoft, prioritized wealth over AI safety. The trial also addresses Musk’s social‑media conduct and may impact OpenAI’s IPO plans and public perception of AI governance
