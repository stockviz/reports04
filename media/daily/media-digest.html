    • Focus on upgrading legacy industrial corridors to incorporate robotics and improve cost‑competitiveness.

April–Dec Tax Growth At 8%, Higher Collections Needed To Meet FY26 Budgeted Tax Growth: Experts 2026-02-01 15:32

TV18BRDCST

The panel discussed India’s FY‑26 budget amid a hostile global backdrop. Sajid Chenoy (J P Morgan) highlighted the dilemma between fiscal conservatism—limited fiscal space after last year’s tax and GST cuts—and the need for adventurous policies to attract investment. He noted gross tax growth at ~8.5% (April‑Dec) and warned that higher tax targets in Jan‑Mar will be needed, while non‑tax revenues (PSU/RBI dividends) provide buffers. Chenoy expects the budget to stay conservative on numbers, aiming for the 4.4% deficit target and modest nominal GDP growth (≈10%).

Sameer Chakrabarti (CII) focused on two priorities: (1) better allocation of domestic savings—currently equity‑heavy—through policy tools, and (2) a push for manufacturing and export‑oriented growth. He called for customs‑duty rationalisation, simplification of FTAs, and reforms that boost FDI rather than short‑term tax concessions.

Both agreed that state finances are becoming a constraint: rising cash‑transfer schemes are inflating state deficits and crowding out CAPEX. Without state‑level fiscal consolidation, the central government’s ability to lower the overall debt‑to‑GDP ratio will be limited.

Key takeaways:

  • Fiscal math will be cautious; the budget will likely under‑promise and over‑deliver.
  • Tax growth shortfall will be offset by higher non‑tax receipts.
  • Manufacturing, export incentives, and customs reforms are expected to feature prominently.
  • Strengthening FDI flows and improving tax certainty are seen as longer‑term levers for rupee stability.
  • State‑level fiscal health will be critical to achieving the central government’s debt‑reduction targets.

Budget 2026: How Policies Will Shape Infra, Defence, Textiles & Key Sectors | Expert Analysis 2026-02-01 14:47

AMBER TRIDENT

Budget 2026 – Key Take‑aways

  • Fiscal Federalism: The government has accepted the 16th Finance Commission’s recommendations, retaining 41 % of vertical devolution and committing ₹1.47 lakh crore in assistance to states. Panelists view this as adequate to keep infrastructure pipelines flowing.

  • Infrastructure & Defence: Capital outlay for defence rises 22 % to ₹2.2 lakh crore (FY 26). Infra spending remains robust, with new pushes for data‑centre tax holidays, Red‑Earth corridor, and power‑finance restructuring.

  • Bond‑Market Reform: Introduction of Total‑Return Swaps (TRS) on corporate bonds is seen as a pivotal step to create a functional corporate bond market. However, success hinges on RBI’s stance, market‑maker support, and the inclusion of leverage. The market remains skeptical until detailed implementation rules emerge.

  • Equity vs. Fixed‑Income: The budget is “short on rhetoric, long on detail,” focusing on removing policy bottlenecks and easing business, rather than offering fresh equity‑side stimulus. Panels note a continued bias toward capex (PLI) over consumption‑driven measures.

  • PLI Expansion: Overall PLI allocation doubles to ₹40 lakh crore, reinforcing the self‑reliant agenda. Some schemes (e.g., mobile/IT hardware) see cuts, implying a re‑allocation rather than a net increase.

  • Tax & Market Frictions: GST revenue forecasts improve, but higher Securities Transaction Tax (STT) and short‑term LTCG tax are dampening retail participation. STT collections are lagging (₹50 k crore vs. ₹78 k crore target), adding short‑term strain.

  • Macro Outlook: Panelists praise India’s decade‑long macro‑stability, emphasizing the need to translate it into lower cost of capital. Transmission of RBI rate cuts into bond yields remains a key watch‑point; high gross borrowing (₹17.2 lakh crore) could pressure yields unless the composition leans toward T‑bills.

  • Sectoral Views:

    • Textiles: Trident Group’s Rajinder Gupta calls the budget “well‑articulated” but feels more could be done for export‑oriented firms and Indian‑branded cotton incentives.
    • Data Centres: Small tax holiday for cloud‑service exports noted; limited but positive impact.
    • Power/Electricity: Ongoing reforms outside the budget are essential given the sector’s investment needs.
    • Banking: High‑level committee on bank reforms announced; deeper reforms still awaited.

  • Market Sentiment: Short‑term irritants (STT, LTCG) may cause volatility, but fundamentals—EPS growth, FII inflows, rupee stability, and a US‑India trade deal—are seen as the longer‑term drivers of equity performance.

  • Conclusion: The budget is viewed as a continuation rather than a game‑changer, reinforcing capex and policy fixes while leaving equity stimulus minimal. Success will depend on execution of bond‑market tools, clarity on borrowing composition, and the pace of structural reforms in power, banking, and manufacturing.
