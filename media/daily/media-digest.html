From Zepto to Solving India’s Househelp Problem: How Aayush Agarwal Built Snabbit | The Disruptors 2026-01-17 12:01

URBANCO

Summary

  • Background: Aayush Agarwal grew up in Kolkata, completed an MBA at IMD Ghaziabad, worked briefly in a back‑office role at a Bangalore investment bank, then moved to startups. He spent nine months as Chief of Staff at Zepto, where he handled on‑ground operational problem‑solving and helped raise capital during a funding‑winter.

  • Founding Snabbit: Leveraging Zepto’s hyper‑local logistics playbook, Aayush launched Snabbit to bring a formal, tech‑enabled, hyper‑local model to India’s house‑help services (cleaning, cooking, etc.), a massive offline habit. “Snabbit” (Swedish for “quick”) signals speed and reliability.

  • Service Model:

    • Training & Vetting: Experts undergo a three‑day, ~25‑hour training program, background checks, and certification; only those who pass become service providers.
    • Gig Structure: Experts work flexible shifts (up to 8 hours/day, typically 5 hours of active jobs), earning ~₹23‑24 k per month. Snabbit guarantees a minimum income even on low‑demand days.
    • Network Economics: Dense micro‑market coverage reduces travel time, increasing utilization and allowing higher take‑home pay while keeping prices competitive for customers.

  • Key Metrics:

    • Current Volume: ~20,000 jobs per day across 40 pincodes (80 % of orders); operating for ~7 quarters.
    • Customer Acquisition Cost: ₹500‑600, with a payback horizon of 9‑10 months based on average 3‑3.5 transactions per month per user.
    • Job Ratings: >90 % of jobs receive 4‑5 stars; only 3 % receive 1 star.

  • Pricing & Subsidy: Early orders are heavily subsidized to drive adoption; discounts are short‑term and funded by Snappy, not passed to experts.

  • Profitability & Sustainability: Profitability hinges on improving network efficiency (higher utilization, reduced travel) rather than cutting expert pay. The model aims to maintain dignified treatment (using “experts” terminology) and competitive earnings.

  • Competition: Urban Company (formerly UrbanClap) is a publicly listed, well‑funded rival. Aayush views competition as a catalyst that validates the category and pushes Snappy to execute faster; he notes that Snappy entered the space earlier (2024) and differentiates by focusing on a non‑inventory, high‑frequency, nano‑market model.

  • Future Vision:

    • Market Size: India’s household services spend ~₹50 k per year per home; 98 % remains offline, representing a $40‑100 bn opportunity.
    • Growth Target: Scale from 20 k to 1 million jobs per day over the next few years, expanding into multiple service categories and deepening market penetration.

Indian IT Sector: Q3 Earnings Highlights | Editors’ Roundtable

INFY HCL-INSYS TCS

The Q3 earnings of India’s large‑cap IT firms showed modest but positive signs. All three companies—Infosys, TCS and HCL Tech—beat their low revenue‑growth expectations (Infosys +0.6%, TCS +0.8%). Guidance was nudged higher, with Infosys and HCL Tech upgrading their outlooks. AI revenue is expanding rapidly on a small base (≈4% of HCL’s and 6% of TCS’s total revenue, up ~20% QoQ), though concerns remain that AI could cannibalize traditional application‑maintenance work. Deal wins were strong, especially for Infosys (>$4 bn, 57% net‑new), yet overall growth forecasts stay in the low‑single digits for 2024 (≈3‑3.5%) and modestly higher (5‑8%) for 2025. Valuations are roughly in line, with Infosys trading at a slight premium (~22× earnings) to TCS. The sector appears to be emerging from its trough, showing incremental improvement and a tentative reset rather than a sharp rebound.

SC Passes Verdict On Tiger Global-Flipkart Case | CNBC TV18 2026-01-17 07:43

TIGERLOGS

The Supreme Court’s ruling in the Tiger Global‑Flipkart case signals that a Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) will no longer be treated as conclusive proof of treaty benefits, potentially affecting all transactions that rely on residency‑based tax treaties (e.g., Singapore, Mauritius, Netherlands, Cyprus). This creates uncertainty for past deals, especially those involving capital‑gain exits from FY 2019‑20 onward, as tax authorities may revisit assessments and apply General Anti‑Avoidance Rules (GAR) more aggressively, forcing investors to demonstrate real substance in offshore holding companies.

Key takeaways for founders and investors:

  • Jurisdiction choice must be driven by genuine commercial rationale, not just tax savings. Purely paper‑based offshore structures are now riskier.
  • Substance matters. Funds and startups need real operational presence or management activities in the chosen jurisdiction to withstand scrutiny.
  • Future deals and exits may face heightened tax scrutiny. Parties should be prepared to defend the economic substance of any offshore entity.
  • Founders need not fear a loss of global capital, but must align with investors who have robust, substance‑based structures.

Immediate advice: reassess current offshore holdings, ensure they serve a legitimate business purpose, document substance, and let business considerations dictate tax structuring rather than the reverse.
