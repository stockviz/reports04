Crude Oil Prices Surge Amid Middle East Tensions and Tight Inventories

TV18BRDCST

Overnight crude rallied to $97‑$98 per barrel, driven by reports of Iranian missile attacks on Kuwait and Bahrain and stalled US‑Iran diplomatic talks, which threaten the Strait of Hormuz. U.S. crude stocks fell for a sixth consecutive week, down 6.8 million barrels, leaving reserves at a multi‑year low of 357.1 million barrels. The IEA’s strategic release of 400 million barrels has only partially reached the market, and full normalization could take 6‑8 months if the strait reopens. Analysts warn that refined products like jet fuel and diesel will lag behind crude recovery. Global reserves are shrinking: IEA members hold 1.55 billion barrels (down from 1.8 billion), while China’s strategic stock sits at 1.4 billion barrels. Market participants predict crude could climb to $95‑$120 by summer as demand from China weakens—imports have fallen sharply due to higher prices and a shift toward electric vehicles—while supply from Russia, West Africa, and the Atlantic Basin eases pressure. The combination of geopolitical risk, inventory draws, and waning Chinese demand sets the stage for further price volatility

India’s IT firms pivot to on‑site “forward deployed engineers” as AI reshapes client demands

INFY HCLTECH

Indian IT services companies are reversing a trend toward remote staffing to meet growing client expectations for AI‑savvy engineers who combine technical, consulting and sales skills. New roles such as “frontier certified engineers” and “frontier business operators” at Cognizant, and similar “forward deployed engineers” at Infosys, are designed to redesign workflows, integrate AI, and manage hybrid human‑AI processes. While these high‑skill, on‑site professionals command higher salaries and strain short‑term margins, they promise higher‑value AI transformation work and outcome‑based pricing. Competitors like OpenAI’s DeployCo are also entering the market, intensifying competition for AI talent. Margins are mixed: Cognizant’s operating margin rose to 16.1% in calendar 2025, while Infosys and HCLTech saw declines in FY26. Experts expect demand for such engineers to keep growing, despite the current pressure on profitability

The case against listing Tata Sons, from one of Noel Tata’s closest advisers

TENNIND NSIL TATAMOTORS BEL TCIEXP KMSUGAR TATACAP

Title: Why Noel Tata and Senior Advisers Oppose Listing Tata Sons

Summary:
Noel Tata, backed by longtime confidant Farokh Subedar—who served 42 years at Tata Sons—argues that taking the holding company public would undermine the group’s unique, privately‑held structure. Subedar warns that a listing would subject strategic decisions—such as large‑scale investments, debt‑free asset acquisitions, and emergency support to group subsidiaries—to public‑shareholder approval, creating uncertainty and limiting the ability to act in the long‑term interests of the conglomerate. He cites past examples (the Tata Finance deposit‑run, the Air India takeover, the NTT Docomo commitment, and the Empress Mills gratuity) where the close‑knit ownership allowed swift, decisive action without minority‑shareholder constraints. While RBI regulations now classify Tata Sons as an “upper‑layer NBFC” and mandate a listing by 2025, Subedar contends there is no systemic risk in maintaining the current structure, especially given Tata Sons’ zero‑debt status and diversified portfolio. Nonetheless, pressure mounts from shareholders such as the Shapoorji Pallonji Group, proxy advisers, and two Tata Trusts vice‑chairmen who have shifted to support a listing, arguing it would improve governance and transparency. The debate pits the need for regulatory compliance and broader market discipline against preserving the Tata group’s historic, patient, and private governance model.

Policy Reforms Needed to Boost Foreign Investment in India

STANLEY

Rhythm Desai, Chief Equity Strategist at Morgan Stanley, highlighted that foreign investor sentiment toward India is currently very weak due to relative under‑performance in growth compared with other large markets, despite attractive valuations. He noted that while AI‑related capex is expected to stay robust through 2028, the near‑term growth outlook remains modest, limiting foreign inflows. Outflows are driven largely by passive fund rebalancing as India’s index weight falls, not by active disinvestment. Desai pointed to strong domestic retail buying and the potential for corporate issuances to create supply that could attract foreign buyers. He called for policy changes to make market access easier for overseas investors—streamlining registration, raising foreign ownership limits, and simplifying tax administration—while cautioning that major tax reforms (e.g., cutting capital‑gains tax) would have to be prospective to avoid market disruption
