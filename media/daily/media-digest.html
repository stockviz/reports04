Pankaj Tebriwal on Inflation‑Driven Market Shifts, a New Chemical Cycle, and West Bengal’s Growth Prospects

HDFCAMC DELTACORP

In a CNBC‑TV18 interview, IKIGAI Asset Manager’s founder‑CIO Pankaj Tebriwal explains that the Indian market is now split into a pre‑Feb 28 world and a post‑Feb 28 world, with inflation set to dominate the macro backdrop. High energy and raw‑material costs are eroding margins, but they also favour large, well‑capitalised firms that can pass on costs and capture market share from fragmented, unorganised players across sectors such as home‑improvement, tiles, plywood, PVC pipes, and especially chemicals. He notes that recent headwinds for Indian chemical makers—appreciated yuan, doubled logistics costs, and China’s removal of export rebates—have created a “new cycle” with expectations of double‑digit top‑line growth and operating leverage despite margin pressure. Recent NSE‑500 earnings show strong gains (large caps ≈ 8.5%, mid‑caps ≈ 50%, small‑caps ≈ 27‑28%), though the next quarter may be slower as raw‑material cost pass‑through lags. Tebriwal’s “dark‑horse” bets for the next 12‑18 months are energy, chemicals and power‑related themes. He also praises the recent West Bengal election outcome, arguing that a shift in governance could unlock the state’s latent assets (cheap labor, power, ports, fertile land) and add a meaningful boost to India’s GDP, mirroring recent industrial turnarounds in states like UP and Bihar. His investment focus remains bottom‑up, targeting sector leaders that can grow earnings and gain share in this high‑inflation environment

Instant‑Help Services Struggle with Workforce Shortage Amid Summer Demand Surge

URBANCO TEAMLEASE 5PAISA HALDER HDFCAMC

On‑demand home‑help platforms such as Urban Company’s Insta Help, Snabbit and Pronto face a severe shortage of gig workers in major Indian cities during the peak summer period, leading to limited slot availability, delayed or cancelled bookings, and user frustration. Seasonal migration of workers to villages for holidays and harvest, amplified by elections in West Bengal and Tamil Nadu, has reduced supply just as demand for domestic services—driven by dual‑income households and convenience—continues to grow. Platforms report rapid booking growth (e.g., Pronto’s daily bookings rose from ~1,000 to >18,000) but struggle to scale onboarding, training, and retention of domestic workers, who require higher trust and verification than other gig jobs. Companies are hiring more workers, raising funds (Snabbit’s $56 M Series D, Pronto’s $25 M round), and pledging to restore service, yet structural and cyclical challenges keep supply lagging behind demand in India’s fast‑expanding online home‑services market projected to reach ₹85‑88 billion by FY30

Crude Oil Prices Surge Over 85% in 2026 Amid US‑Iran Tensions

Crude oil jumped roughly 85% in 2026, driven by heightened volatility from escalating US‑Iran conflict in the Strait of Hormuz, where US forces have engaged Iranian vessels and drones. Analysts note tightening physical storage, low global oil inventories (≈101 days of demand) and bullish forecasts (Barclays sees $110 by May’s end). Yet supply is easing as US rig counts rise, Venezuela’s exports rebound, and OPEC+ adds 188,000 bpd from June, while pipeline capacity from Saudi Arabia to the UAE adds ~7 million bpd. QuestCorp Q4 Earnings Call Highlights Diversification and Margin Outlook

QUESS

QuestCorp reported muted Q4 growth, with general staffing revenues declining due to domestic election‑driven labor supply constraints, while overseas and professional staffing segments showed momentum. CEO Loheed Bhatia clarified that the cited NBFC insourcing loss occurred in the prior fiscal year and noted a 4% increase in headcount and 6% revenue growth year‑on‑year, ending the year with 479,000 workers. QuestCorp’s strategy focuses on Global Capability Centers (GCCs), which now account for ~70% of professional services revenue, while IT services to enterprises contribute ~10‑12%. The company sees significant upside from mid‑size GCCs (revenues $5‑15 bn) in the next 4‑5 years, underpinning its long‑term growth narrative

India’s April Auto Market Roars: Trucks, Tractors Surge as Rural Demand Fuels Growth

TATAMOTORS M&M MARUTI

Commercial vehicle retail sales jumped 15% YoY and tractor sales surged 23% in April, driven by strong freight activity and robust rural demand. Tata Motors maintained a 37% CV market share, Mahindra held ~24% in trucks, while VE Commercial gained ground; Ashok Leyland lost share despite double‑digit growth. Rural passenger vehicle sales rose 20% YoY versus 7% in urban areas, and CV demand grew 20% in villages versus 10% in cities. Overall auto retail sales rose 13% to 2.61 million units, with five of six segments hitting record monthly sales. Tractor sales hit 75,109 units, with Mahindra at ~42% share; CNH Industrial grew >50% YoY, while John Deere lagged. Inventory levels for passenger cars rose to 2,830 days, hinting at a seasonal slowdown, and April sales fell ~3% sequentially. SpiceJet’s Shrinking Fleet Threatens International Ops

INDIGO SPICEJET TENNIND

SpiceJet’s operational fleet has fallen from 33 aircraft at year‑end to about 21 in early May, nearing the DGCA’s minimum of 20 aircraft required for international flights. The decline results from lease returns, maintenance delays, spare‑part shortages, and financial strain, leaving the airline with 18 Boeing jets and three turboprops, many of which are wet or damp‑leased. Though the carrier cites 53‑54 aircraft on its Air Operator Permit, only 21 are active, prompting regulatory risk of suspension of its overseas routes unless a special dispensation is granted. The airline’s domestic market share slipped to 3.9%, ranking fourth, while rivals like Akasa Air and IndiGo dominate. SpiceJet is pursuing fund‑raising, extended credit‑linked guarantees, and efforts to return grounded planes to service, but its ability to meet the 20‑aircraft threshold and sustain international operations remains uncertain
