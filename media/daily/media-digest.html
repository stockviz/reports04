Is Your Property Making You Rich or Poor

DAMCAPITAL

In this NDTV Profit interview, Alex Mathew and Vishal Dhawan discuss how to identify and address poor real‑estate investments. Vishal explains that real‑estate returns are hard to gauge due to hidden costs (registration, GST, maintenance, loan interest, property taxes) and uncertain market valuations. He stresses calculating the true post‑cost return and comparing it to alternative assets (equities, fixed deposits) while accounting for taxes, leverage, and illiquidity premiums. Key red flags for a bad property include: (1) failing to meet a target return over 5‑7 years, (2) liquidity needs that outpace the asset’s sale timeline, and (3) excessive loan burden—EMIs should stay below 40‑45 % of net household income. If these triggers appear, Vishal advises reassessing the investment, possibly selling, and avoiding over‑leveraging to protect broader financial goals

Keeping Cash on the Sidelines? Here’s Where to Park It

NUVAMA

In a conversation with Varun Fatehpuria, CEO of Dalit Wealth Management, the importance of maintaining “dry powder” (cash reserves) amid a sideways market and modest earnings outlook is affirmed. Varun recommends keeping roughly 8‑10% of a fully invested equity portfolio as liquid cash. For short‑term needs (under 3 months), a savings account or liquid debt fund (≈5.7‑6% annualized) works; for 3‑6 months, ultra‑short duration or money‑market funds (≈6‑6.5%) are better. Longer horizons (>12 months) can consider arbitrage or income‑plus‑arbitrage funds, which offer equity‑like tax treatment (12.5% after a year) and returns near the repo rate (≈5.75‑6%). He cautions against holding cash in low‑yield bank accounts for extended periods due to opportunity cost. The overall guidance: retain modest cash reserves for flexibility, park them in high‑liquidity, higher‑yield debt instruments, and use longer‑term arbitrage strategies only when the investment horizon permits
