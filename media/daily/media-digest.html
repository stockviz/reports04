BIRLACORPN NSIL FEDERALBNK PNB SBIN CAPTRUST BANKBARODA PFC COALINDIA HDFCBANK

The Reserve Bank of India kept the policy repo rate unchanged at 5.25% and maintained a neutral stance, while raising its FY27 inflation outlook to 5.1% and trimming growth forecast to 6.6% amid supply‑chain risks. Despite this cautious tone, India’s Q4 FY26 real GDP surged to 7.8% (7.7% annualised), outpacing the 7.3% consensus, with nominal GDP up 8.9% and sectoral growth led by services (9.9%), secondary (8.8%) and tertiary (9.3%) activities. The RBI announced expanded, registration‑free access for NRIs, OCIs and PROIs to Indian equities and raised the NRI investment caps (overall from 10% to 24%). These measures, together with anticipated foreign‑currency and external commercial borrowing inflows, are expected to bolster the rupee and lower funding costs for banks, as evidenced by falling CD and corporate bond yields.

In parallel, the Tata Sons listing debate hinges on the RBI’s upcoming “upper‑layer NBFC” list; Tata remains classified as such and would be required to list by September 2025 unless re‑classified.

The RBI also signalled that a shift to plastic banknotes is under review, though no decision has been made.

Market reaction: the rupee appreciated ~1% on the day, equities showed mixed performance (banks up, IT down), and investors await Monday’s open with optimism after the blockbuster GDP print

Top Mutual Fund Categories and Allocation Strategies for the Next Decade

JPASSOCIAT

In a discussion with Himanshu Kohli, co‑founder of Client Associates, three mutual‑fund categories are highlighted as the most promising over the next ten years: (1) mid‑cap and small‑cap equity funds (especially via systematic investment plans to mitigate volatility), (2) diversified flexi‑cap (or all‑season) funds that allow managers to shift between large‑, mid‑ and small‑caps, and (3) multi‑asset or balanced funds for lower‑volatility, lump‑sum investors. For risk‑averse investors, a suggested allocation is roughly 40‑50% to multi‑asset funds, 30‑35% to flexi‑cap, and the remainder to mid/small‑cap funds; systematic investors might reverse this, favoring mid/small‑caps. Large‑cap funds are deemed more suitable for shorter (3‑5 year) horizons. International exposure is recommended for diversification, though tax and availability considerations apply. Under‑owned segments include sovereign‑wealth‑fund‑type (SIF) products and overseas equity funds. Thematic or sector funds (e.g., banking, financial services, energy, defence) may be considered for a 2‑3 year tactical window, but not for a long‑term buy‑and‑hold strategy. Gold exposure (5‑10% of the portfolio) can be achieved via ETFs, multi‑asset funds, or emerging SIF structures to provide a hedge against equity downturns
