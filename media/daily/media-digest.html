Oil Market Outlook Amid US‑Iran Tensions and Chinese Demand Concerns

TV18BRDCST

The show opens with a market recap: US indices slipped after non‑farm payrolls, Asian markets weakened on fresh US‑Iran trade tensions, and crude oil hovered around $100‑bbl despite a recent 6% weekly decline. Guest Mukher Saadev explains that the Hormuz bottleneck, once a 20 million‑bbl‑per‑day issue, has eased to roughly 8‑12 m bpd due to alternative pipelines and bypass routes, but the reduction still tightens supply. Global stock‑drawdown measures have provided a buffer, yet demand‑destruction talks (≈4‑5 m bpd) are modest compared to the supply shock. He notes India’s emerging austerity hints and the need for policy‑driven price hikes to curb consumption. The Saudi pipeline eases some pressure but cannot move all grades, and it shifts traffic to the Red Sea, competing with Russian routes. Saadev sees the epicenter shifting from Hormuz to China: Chinese crude imports have fallen from 13 m to 7 m bpd, product imports halved, and LNG purchases cut in half, leaving China vulnerable. The upcoming US‑China summit may influence pricing, but with no clear resolution, oil is likely to stay near $100/bbl in the short term, driven by supply constraints, geopolitical uncertainty, and limited demand‑side relief
