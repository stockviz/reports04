Low Cost Carriers Can Start Raising Fares To Make Up For Rev Loss From Seat Selection: Mayur Milak

TENNIND INDIGO

Title: Impact of Seat‑Selection Fee Reinstatement and Passenger‑Rights Rules on Indian Low‑Cost Carriers

Summary: Mayur Milak explains that reinstating seat‑selection charges—raising the free‑seat share from 20% to 60%—will have minimal effect on LCC revenues because the majority of seats remain uncharged and utilization is already high. The 40% of chargeable seats can generate extra income, but recent fare hikes (e.g., a 425 % increase on futures) already provide ample margin, so airlines are unlikely to raise fees further. The government’s move to rebundle fares and enforce stricter passenger‑rights rules may level the playing field between LCCs and full‑service carriers like Air India, but the financial impact is limited. Delays and cancellations are often outside airline control, so penalties may not significantly affect profitability. While rising jet‑fuel prices, rupee depreciation, and geopolitical tensions pose cost pressures, domestic demand during peak seasons (holidays, school vacations) should sustain earnings. Overall, Milak maintains a bullish outlook on Indian airlines, noting that recent stock corrections have already priced in much of the risk.

Point One Initiative Calls Companies to Donate 0.1% of Revenue for Global Renewable Funding

NPST DIAMONDYD

Point One, launched with 30 initial firms, asks companies to contribute 0.1% of revenue—potentially $200 million by 2030—to seed renewable projects and attract up to $3 billion from institutional investors. Backed by figures like Ryan Kohn and Dan Stern, the scheme leverages the collective economic weight of SMEs (over 90% of firms, 50% of global GDP) to generate a “first‑loss” contribution that could multiply private capital by up to 15×, aiming to finance a full transition to clean power by 2040. The initiative partners with the Global Energy Alliance for People and Planet to tap untapped corporate philanthropy as development aid wanes

Meta challenges CCPA’s Rs 10 lakh penalty over walkie‑talkie listings on Facebook Marketplace

MEESHO TANLA

Meta filed a petition in the Delhi High Court contesting a Central Consumer Protection Authority order that fined it Rs 10 lakh for allegedly allowing unauthorised walkie‑talkie sales on Facebook Marketplace. Meta’s counsel argued that Facebook is merely a notice‑board, not an e‑commerce platform, and that it does not facilitate transactions or charge commissions, thus outside CCPA’s jurisdiction. The court scheduled a hearing for March 25, asking Meta to explain the jurisdictional claim and why the National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission cannot hear the matter. The petition contends the CCPA exceeded its powers, misapplied e‑commerce rules, and violated natural‑justice principles, warning that such an interpretation could burden ordinary individuals selling personal items. The CCPA’s earlier order had identified over 16,970 non‑compliant walkie‑talkie listings across multiple platforms, directed Meta to block such listings, and mandated periodic self‑audits and public disclosure of compliance certificates
