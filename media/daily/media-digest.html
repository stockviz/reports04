Nikesh Arora on Agentic AI, Job Futures, and Palo Alto Networks’ Security Strategy

AGCNET

In a lively interview at the India AI Impact Summit, Palo Alto Networks CEO Nikesh Arora downplays fears of widespread job loss from “agentic AI,” likening the rollout of autonomous agents to the gradual development of self‑driving cars. He stresses that AI will replace repetitive tasks, creating new roles in model training, deployment, and security. Arora outlines Palo Alto’s proactive acquisitions—Chronosphere for observability, CyberArc for identity, and COI for coding‑agent control—to build a platform that secures the emerging AI‑driven ecosystem. He argues that while AI adoption is accelerating, precision and safety requirements will slow full deployment, especially in critical enterprise settings. The CEO predicts massive compute demand, driven by consumer‑level AI services, and warns that electricity and green‑energy concerns will become limiting factors. Overall, Arora is optimistic: the AI wave will expand bandwidth and compute needs, spur new employment, and fuel Palo Alto’s growth as it safeguards the expanding attack surface of agents and large language models

The demo highlights Siro, a robot powered by an object‑intelligence platform that can grasp entirely novel items—transparent, reflective, or oddly shaped—without prior training or explicit programming. By mimicking early human visual processing, Siro extracts geometry, texture, and motion cues to determine how to grasp objects, enabling “curiosity‑driven” pick‑and‑place actions. The technology addresses a major AI bottleneck: handling unseen objects, opening pathways for automating complex assembly tasks in industries such as automotive, consumer electronics, and semiconductor equipment. Siro has moved beyond prototype to commercial pilots, with deployments planned for flexible, software‑defined production lines that can switch tasks without costly hardware changes
