Adani Group Seeks Dismissal of SEC Fraud Suit on Grounds of No U.S. Jurisdiction and Extraterritorial Overreach

ADANIGREEN BCG

Billionaire Gautam Adani and his nephew Sagar have asked the Eastern District of New York to throw out the SEC’s securities‑fraud lawsuit over a 2021 $750 million bond sale by Adani Green Energy. Their pre‑motion letter argues the court lacks personal jurisdiction because the bonds were issued abroad under Rule 144A and Regulation S, the issuer is Indian, and any alleged misconduct occurred in India. The filing contends the SEC’s claims are impermissibly extraterritorial, lack a “domestic transaction,” and do not show investor losses or any specific misleading statements tied to the defendants. It also characterizes the alleged ESG and anti‑corruption statements as non‑actionable “puffery” and asserts the SEC failed to allege intent or knowledge. The Adanis seek dismissal under Rule 12(b)(2) and are prepared for a pre‑motion conference

Super Micro Initiates Independent Probe into Export‑Control Allegations

APOLLO

Super Micro announced an independent investigation following the indictment of co‑founder Yih‑Shyan Liaw, sales manager Ruei‑Tsang Chang and contractor Ting‑Wei Sun on export‑control violations involving the routing of U.S.‑made servers through Taiwan to Southeast Asia and then smuggling them into China. The U.S. Justice Department claims the trio moved at least $2.5 billion in AI technology, including shipments to Chinese universities linked to the PLA. Although Super Micro is not named as a defendant, it placed Liaw and Chang on leave, terminated Sun, and saw Liaw resign from the board. The company also launched an internal review of its global trade compliance program. The probe is led by independent board members Scott Angel and Tally Liu, with law firm Munger, Tolles & Olson and consulting firm AlixPartners providing investigative and forensic accounting support. No timeline for the investigation’s completion has been set

UBS Fails to Secure Clarification of 1999 Holocaust Settlement

BCG MANCREDIT

A U.S. judge declined UBS’s request to have the 1999 $1.25 billion settlement with Holocaust victims’ heirs clarified, leaving the bank without a court‑issued shield against future claims. UBS argued the accord should bar any new lawsuits over Nazi‑linked accounts, but Judge Edward Korman said he could not issue an advisory opinion absent a pending case. The dispute, brought by the Simon Wiesenthal Center, remains open, and UBS maintains the settlement was meant to end all controversy while acknowledging the right of Jewish groups to pursue truth
