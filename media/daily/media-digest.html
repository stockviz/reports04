India’s Triple Import Shock and Market Outlook

BIOCON

India is grappling with a “triple whammy” of soaring import bills—gold, semiconductors and oil—each adding roughly $40‑50 billion to the current‑account deficit, putting pressure on the rupee. While the RBI steps in to defend the currency and officials urge modest consumption‑saving measures, the speaker argues that these challenges are structural and cannot be fixed overnight. The overall view remains cautiously optimistic about India’s economic fundamentals

Minister Orders Probe into Online Ticket Cancellation Fees

The Consumer Affairs Ministry, led by Minister Pralhad Joshi, has directed the Department of Consumer Affairs and the Central Consumer Protection Authority to investigate whether online ticketing platforms impose excessive cancellation charges compared to airlines. The inquiry aims to assess transparency, potential violations of the Consumer Protection Act, 2019, and unfair trade practices, with the CCPA prepared to take actions including class actions. The move follows allegations by BJYM’s Tajinder Bagga that platforms like Agoda charge higher fees than airlines such as Akasa Air. The investigation will cover all online ticketing platforms’ cancellation policies

AI-Driven Shifts in 2025 Tech Layoffs

In 2025, tech layoffs are being reshaped by artificial intelligence rather than the pandemic‑driven over‑hiring of earlier years. Companies are targeting middle‑management roles for delayering, using AI to handle scheduling, reporting and coordination, and moving toward leaner, engineering‑heavy structures. Budgets are being redirected from headcount growth to AI infrastructure and talent, while firms adopt “AI‑first” hiring policies that favor automation over new hires. Customer‑support functions are increasingly automated, and industry research links a quarter of 2025 tech layoffs directly to AI and automation initiatives, signaling a new era where AI drives organizational redesign and workforce reductions

Legal Challenge May Stall $7.25 B Roundup Settlement

BAYERCROP

A proposed $7.25 billion class‑action settlement to resolve thousands of Roundup cancer lawsuits faces procedural hurdles. An attorney has moved to shift the case from Missouri state court to federal court, potentially disrupting the June 4 opt‑out deadline and the July 9 hearing. The dispute coincides with a U.S. Supreme Court case on whether Bayer can block state lawsuits alleging glyphosate causes non‑Hodgkin lymphoma. Bayer argues it complied with federal labeling, while plaintiffs cite a WHO classification that deems glyphosate probably carcinogenic. If many claimants opt out, Bayer may cancel the settlement. The settlement would fund annual payments for up to 21 years, with compensation ranging from $10,000 to $165,000 depending on exposure and age at diagnosis
