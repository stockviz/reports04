Investing in Uncertain Times: Why Optimism Wins and When to Exit

ICICIAMC HDFC

The conversation explores how investors should “save like a pessimist and invest like an optimist,” using historical Indian market data that shows long periods of flat or negative returns followed by substantial long‑term gains. Vijay Mantri highlights past low‑return phases (1992‑2003, 2000, 2008‑2014) and how SIP investors actually benefit during market downtrends because they acquire more units at lower NAVs. Valuation cues (Nifty P/E < 20, P/B < 3.1) suggest it’s a good time to accumulate, and in volatile markets the frequency of systematic transfer plans (STP) should be shortened (weekly or daily) to capture price swings. The key takeaway: stay disciplined, use rupee‑cost averaging, and avoid reacting to short‑term headlines; the market’s self‑correcting nature typically rewards long‑term optimism

OMCs Impose Discounted Refinery Transfer Prices to Cushion Fuel Price Freeze

BPCL

Indian state‑run oil marketing companies (OMCs) have set discounted refinery transfer prices (RTP) for petrol, diesel, ATF and kerosene, cutting the price paid to refiners by up to ₹60 per litre to mitigate losses from a self‑imposed retail price freeze. The discounts, effective from mid‑March, lower RTPs for diesel, ATF and kerosene by roughly 22–60 % of import‑parity cost. Integrated OMCs (IOC, BPCL, HPCL) can absorb some impact across refining and marketing, but standalone refiners such as MRPL, CPCL and HMEL, which sell primarily to OMCs, face a sharp margin squeeze. The move could also affect private refiners like Nayara Energy and Reliance if similar discounts apply. The policy shifts the burden of rising global oil prices from OMCs to the broader refining ecosystem, risking disproportionate strain on independent and private refiners lacking downstream retail exposure
