Curbs On Chinese Cos To Ease? Sectors With Security Concerns Will Likely Be Excluded: PL Capital 2026-01-09 10:23

NTPC SIEMENS ABB RPOWER ORIENTELEC

Key Points

  • Potential Restrictions: India may tighten rules on Chinese firms supplying equipment for power‑generation and transmission, focusing on “strategic” or security‑sensitive segments (e.g., BTG boilers, critical grid components).

  • Sector Impact

    • Power Generation (BTG/Boilers): Biggest exposure. Chinese firms like Shanghai Electric have historically won private‑sector orders; a curtailment would hurt them and benefit domestic players such as BHEL, L&T, and Reliance Power’s in‑house capabilities.
    • Transmission & Grid Equipment: Companies such as ABB and Siemens could see margin pressure if Chinese alternatives are limited; domestic manufacturers of transformers, switchgear, and control systems could gain market share.
    • Renewables & EPC: Less likely to be targeted; Chinese involvement is lower, so impact is muted. Domestic EPC firms may see modest upside.
    • Utilities & State‑Owned Buyers (e.g., NTPC): Already favor non‑Chinese suppliers; restrictions would reinforce existing procurement patterns with minimal disruption.
    • Telecom & Defense‑adjacent Infrastructure: Mentioned as other strategic areas where China‑origin equipment is being phased out; benefits accrue to Indian OEMs and allied foreign suppliers.

  • Beneficiaries

    • Domestic OEMs: BHEL, L&T, ABB‑India, Siemens‑India, and other local manufacturers stand to capture displaced Chinese business.
    • Suppliers of Indigenous Technology: Firms with “Make‑in‑India” credentials and government‑approved certifications.
    • Investors in Domestic Capex: Companies poised to benefit from the projected 40‑50 % capacity addition in power over the next two years.

  • Negatively Affected Parties

    • Chinese Vendors: Shanghai Electric, other Chinese BTG and turbine makers, and related component suppliers.
    • Indian firms heavily reliant on Chinese imports for cost‑competitiveness: May face higher input costs and delayed project timelines.
    • Stocks Sensitive to Chinese Exposure: BHEL (BHL) could see short‑term volatility; its order book is strong but execution margins may tighten if Chinese competition disappears abruptly.

  • Market Outlook for BHEL (BHL)

    • Strong order book from the past 24 months; execution capability and balance‑sheet health are now the focus.
    • Potential upside if Chinese BTG imports are curtailed, but near‑term risk of order‑flow moderation as the market adjusts.
    • Stock already reacted (≈ 10 % drop) on speculation; further moves will depend on the final scope of any curbs.

  • The Indian finance ministry is considering scrapping a 2020 rule that barred Chinese firms from bidding on government contracts in sensitive sectors such as power, railways, and metro signaling.
  • The news triggered a sell‑off in related stocks.
  • Brokerage outlooks:
    • Bernstein: Likely a segment‑by‑segment relaxation with prior approvals; could hurt domestic players like L&T and benefit asset owners such as Power Grid and NTPC.
    • Jeffries: Biggest winners would be BHEL, L&T, ABB and CG Power; defence firms and Cummins see the least impact; also potential effects on T&D firms, Siemens Energy and Hitachi Energy.
    • IIFL: Chinese vendor TVEA may be allowed to supply Power Grid standards on a case‑by‑case basis; the lift will be product‑specific, not blanket.
    • Philip Capital: Chinese firms have largely stayed out of grid, railway and metro‑signal tenders; the policy uncertainty could pressure sector valuations.
    • Macquarie: Easing could ease supply‑chain constraints, accelerate project execution and gradually lift import curbs.
    • ICI Securities: The relaxation could enable Chinese companies to set up Indian factories and compete locally.
  • Overall, analysts are divided on timing and extent, but consensus is that the policy shift would reshape competition and valuation dynamics in India’s capital‑goods sector.

India is poised to lift a five‑year ban on Chinese firms bidding for government infrastructure projects, aiming to restore commercial ties with China and address equipment shortages in transmission and heavy‑infrastructure sectors. The policy shift has already triggered a 4‑9% sell‑off in capital‑goods stocks, with BHEL falling ~9% and other names such as Cummins, ABB, L&T, and Hitachi Energy also slipping.
