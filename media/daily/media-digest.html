All About Kia Seltos | Features Explained | Overdrive | CNBC TV18

TATAMOTORS HYUNDAI

The 2026 Kia Seltos has been upgraded to compete as a standalone midsize SUV, shedding its former “shadow” of the Hyundai Creta. It features a sharper, more mature design with squared‑off DRLs, a larger footprint, a lower drag coefficient (0.31), and revised under‑body work. Interior improvements include a panoramic sunroof, larger rear doors with proximity‑activated handles, better seat cushioning, a memory‑function driver seat, and a refined climate‑control layout that retains physical buttons. The infotainment system is faster and cleaner, offering three‑year free connected services and OTA updates for up to 40 ECUs, plus comprehensive vehicle diagnostics.

Driving dynamics are enhanced: steering is balanced, all‑disc brakes with green calipers provide predictable stopping, and the suspension is softer yet taut, delivering comfort on rough roads without sacrificing handling. ADAS Level 2 is standard (front radar, rear radars, windshield camera) with future Level 2 Plus planned. Powertrains remain the same—a 1.5 L NA petrol, 1.5 L turbo petrol (paired with 7‑speed DCT or 6‑speed IMT), and a turbo diesel. Overall noise has been reduced by about 2 dB, and the new K3 platform improves refinement, NVH, and chassis balance. The Seltos now positions itself as a confident, well‑rounded contender in the Indian SUV market.

Market Recap – The Indian market snapped a two‑week rally, with the Nifty down ~2.5% (large‑cap drops up to 6%). BSE‑listed firms lost >₹15 lakh crore in market cap. The pull‑back is not driven by earnings misses but by deteriorating sentiment, amplified by geopolitical noise and continued foreign‑institutional (FI) selling, especially in large‑cap names (HDFC Bank, Reliance, Maruti).

Sentiment vs. Fundamentals – Despite an improving economic, profit and credit cycle (lower rates, higher liquidity), sentiment remains weak. Investors are trimming valuations amid uncertainty, and the anticipated reversal of FI outflows after three years of selling has not materialised.

Trade‑Deal Outlook – A possible India‑US trade agreement could revive sentiment, but its impact on GDP is modest (0.3‑0.5%). The larger boost may come from an upcoming EU‑India deal. Until such headlines materialise, sentiment‑driven buying is unlikely.

Sector & Earnings Outlook (Q3 FY26)
- Financials: Lenders expect a quiet quarter; credit‑card stress to ease. NBFCs likely to post strong AUM growth (4‑5%).
- Consumer Staples & Retail: GST‑driven demand could lift staples ~7% YoY; retail remains subdued except for jewellery and cable.
- Auto: Volume growth strong but squeezed by lower gross margins.
- IT: Flat expectations; investors watching AI‑revenue guidance from TCS, ITC Tech and mid‑caps (Co‑Forge, Persistent). Large‑cap IT undervalued with attractive dividend yields, but growth visibility is key.
- Pharma: Domestic sales solid; US business flat; generic players may lag.
- Cement & Metals: Mixed; ultra‑tech & JK Cement hold up, others face contraction.
- Energy: Mixed OMC outlook; Reliance could benefit from higher GRMs.
- Industrial: EBITDA growth ~13% YoY – the sector’s bright spot.
- Internet/Platform: Recent declines, but potential capex‑driven recovery.

Analysts (Kotak, Citi) project FY26 EPS ~1,072 and FY27 ~1,267; earnings guidance will be closely watched.

IT Positioning – Portfolio managers are underweight IT, especially large caps, preferring mid‑caps with proven growth. Valuations look attractive (3‑3.5% dividend yields) but growth visibility remains the decisive factor.

Nifty‑to‑Gold Ratio – The ratio has fallen to ~1.9 (Nifty ≈ 26,000; gold ≈ 13,900), near historic lows where equities have historically outperformed gold over the subsequent 6‑12 months (13‑20% returns). However, gold’s short‑term upside is supported by heightened geopolitical tension and central‑bank buying, while the longer‑term view still favours equities as Indian earnings compound ~13‑14% annually.

Key Take‑aways
1. Sentiment, not fundamentals, is the primary drag.
2. FI selling persists; a trade‑deal breakthrough could lift mood.
3. Q3 earnings are expected to be muted across most sectors; guidance will matter more than numbers.
4. IT growth (especially AI revenue) and mid‑cap performance will be closely monitored.
5. The low Nifty‑to‑Gold ratio suggests a potential equity bounce, but short‑term gold remains a safe‑haven play amid geopolitical risks.

Next week’s focus: Q3 earnings across banks, insurers, IT and the broader market reaction.
