Bullish On BFSI After Several Years Of Underperformance: Devang Mehta Of Spark Private Wealth

TITAN ANANDRATHI

The interview with Devang Mehta of Spark Private Wealth covered four main themes:

  1. Metals – The recent rally in gold, silver and other metals is driven by geopolitical tension, but the upside is limited. Existing holders may keep positions for a few more months, while new entrants face an unfavorable risk‑reward profile, especially in ferrous and non‑ferrous metals.

  2. Banking & Financial Services (BFSI) – After years of underperformance, BFSI is seen as a strong long‑term play. Mehta recommends focusing on capital‑market‑related firms—exchanges, depositories, asset‑finance companies, and wealth‑management outfits—rather than banks alone. These entities could deliver 18‑22 % annual growth over a 3‑5‑year horizon, compared with 10‑14 % for banks.

  3. Consumption – With fiscal measures (tax cuts, GST reductions) and lower RBI rates, consumer demand is reviving. Mehta favors a mix of discretionary and non‑discretionary players, ranging from rural‑focused retailers to premium brands like Titan, as well as auto manufacturers shifting toward higher‑end SUVs and the travel/hospitality sector.

  4. Cap‑Ex Driven Sectors – Engineering, power‑equipment and other capital‑intensive companies that have corrected recently are attractive for their growth potential, complementing the consumption and financialization themes.

Overall, Mehta suggests a portfolio built around three pillars—credit‑linked consumption, cap‑ex‑heavy firms, and accelerated financial services—to capture upside over the next three to five years.

MAHABANK

The MD highlighted that Bank of Maharashtra’s gold‑loan portfolio has surged from about ₹7,500‑8,000 crore 18‑20 months ago to roughly ₹22,000 crore today, with branch‑originated gold loans at ₹17‑18 crore and a 45‑50 % YoY growth rate. What Quick Commerce Isn’t Telling You | Expert Breaks Down The Major Market Shift 2026-01-14 10:13

SGMART

The speaker explains that while D‑Mart can eventually scale, its growth will be slow, and competition comes from both platform players and local “quick‑commerce” services that promise sub‑hour deliveries. Traditional “started‑levy” grocery models (e.g., Amazon Fresh, BigBasket) never gained traction, whereas quick commerce (Q‑Com) – initially focused on food & groceries – has expanded to include small white goods, accessories, apparel, beauty, stationery, toys, etc. The core competitive edge remains ultra‑fast delivery (10‑20 minutes); extending to 30‑60 minutes puts Q‑Com firms on par with larger retailers like Amazon, Flipkart, and Myntra. Regulations do not restrict ultra‑fast delivery, so maintaining sub‑30‑minute times is crucial. Ultimately, success hinges on improving customer experience while achieving profitability and avoiding cash burn; firms that preserve speed will outpace complacent rivals.
