India’s ATF Stabilisation Scheme Boosts Regional Aviation Amid Fuel Price Hike

The Indian government’s new Aviation Turbine Fuel (ATF) stabilisation fund caps fuel price rises at 25 % and cushions airlines from a 10 % jet‑fuel hike, offering crucial relief especially for regional carriers. Mano Chaku of Fly91 notes that while jet‑engine airlines face fuel‑to‑revenue ratios of 38‑40 %, Fly91’s turboprop fleet keeps its ratio around 22 %, rising only to 28‑30 % after the hike, demonstrating the resilience of turboprop operators. ATR’s Alexei Vidal stresses that turboprop aircraft burn up to 50 % less fuel than comparable jets, making them ideal for India’s under‑served short‑haul routes; the manufacturer currently has about 70 ATRs in India and expects demand for over 200 more. With 163 functional airports but only 70‑80 cities well connected, and 97 % of inter‑city trips still on ground transport, there’s a huge growth opportunity for regional aviation. Fly91 aims to expand from 6 to 60‑80 aircraft within 5‑6 years, covering 50‑60 cities and 280 weekly flights, while leveraging the ATF fund and ATR’s fuel‑efficient aircraft to manage costs and tap new market potential

India’s Tax Policy Turns Plug‑In Hybrids into Luxury Cars

ABMINTLLTD EICHERMOT HYUNDAI

India’s GST structure heavily favors pure battery‑electric vehicles (5 % GST) while imposing a 40 % GST on most plug‑in hybrids, creating a tax gap of about 12 lakh rupees on a 35‑lakh‑rupee car. This pushes PHEVs into the premium‑SUV segment, limiting them to affluent buyers despite their potential as a transition technology. A narrow exemption allows small‑car PHEVs (under 4 m and ≤1,200 cc) to pay 18 % GST, but major manufacturers focus on larger models. Consequently, companies like BYD, Mercedes‑Benz, JSW‑Chery and MG are targeting high‑end markets, and the PHEV market in India remains a niche, urban‑luxury offering rather than a mass‑adoption bridge to full electrification
