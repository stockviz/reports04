Supreme Court’s Tiger Global Ruling: Impact On Foreign Investors | SC Blow For Tiger Global

IDEA TIGERLOGS

The Supreme Court held that Tiger Global’s $1.6 bn sale of its Flipkart stake to Walmart is taxable in India, rejecting the claim of capital‑gains exemption under the India‑Mauritius tax treaty. The court deemed the transaction “treaty shopping” and said treaty benefits cannot be granted solely on a residency certificate; the 2016 amendment requiring genuine commercial substance applies.

Key implications discussed:
- Uncertainty for existing investors: The judgment undermines the “grandfather” protection for pre‑2017 investments routed through Mauritius (or other offshore vehicles) and could expose them to tax scrutiny.
- Substance over form: Investors must demonstrate real commercial substance and intent; merely using a conduit entity may no longer shield gains.
- Broader impact: PE, VC and other foreign funds with similar structures face heightened risk of reassessment and litigation, potentially dampening fresh capital inflows into India.
- Treaty sovereignty debate: Panelists argued that the court’s approach appears to override the bilateral treaty’s intent, raising questions about treaty stability and India’s tax‑nationalist stance.
- Future legal strategy: Some view the two‑judge decision as an outlier that does not overturn earlier Vodafone and Azadi‑Hochaw rulings; a larger bench may be sought for clarification.

Overall, the ruling signals a shift toward stricter enforcement of anti‑avoidance provisions, prompting foreign investors to rethink structuring and possibly curbing appetite for Indian startup exits until greater certainty is restored.

By applying the OECD Principal‑Purpose Test, the Court allowed tax authorities to look beyond form and examine commercial substance, creating significant uncertainty for legacy FPI/PE structures.

  • The decision is unexpected and disrupts established treaty jurisprudence, raising litigation risk for foreign investors with pre‑April‑2017 Indian investments.
  • Taxpayers can seek relief through a review petition, a curative petition, or by urging a larger bench to rehear the matter, arguing public importance and the need for certainty.
  • The government may mitigate the fallout by amending rules or issuing new assurances to restore confidence.
  • Immediate legal recourse for Tiger Global includes domestic review; international arbitration is not available under the current treaty framework.

Overall, the ruling underscores the need for clearer guidance on the grandfathering clause and the application of the Principal‑Purpose Test to prevent further disputes and preserve foreign investment flow into India.

Supreme Court Verdict on Tiger Global (Flipkart) – Key Takeaways

  • Core Ruling: A Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) or tax‑resiliency certificate issued by a foreign country is not conclusive proof of treaty‑benefit eligibility. The Indian tax authority can still examine whether the claimant has real substance and genuine residency in that jurisdiction.

  • Impact on the Tiger Global case: The Court held that Tiger Global lacked substantive presence in Mauritius; therefore, the 0 % capital‑gains rate under the India‑Mauritius treaty does not apply.

  • Effect on existing and future foreign investment:

    • Pre‑April 2017 investments (grandfathered under the old Mauritius treaty) remain unaffected for now, but any exit related to those legacy deals may face heightened scrutiny.
    • Investments made after April 2017 are already taxable in India, so the ruling does not change their tax treatment.
    • Current FDI/FII flows are not expected to be deterred; the decision primarily targets past transactions that relied solely on a TRC.

  • Broader precedent:

    • Any past litigation that treated a TRC (or equivalent certificate of residency, e.g., CUR) as a definitive proof of treaty benefits—across jurisdictions such as Singapore, the Netherlands, the U.S., etc.—could be revisited.
    • This includes other Flipkart exits (e‑Bay, etc.) and unrelated deals where TRCs were used to claim reduced dividend or capital‑gains tax rates.

  • What investors should do:

    • Ensure substantial economic presence in the treaty‑partner country (office, staff, board, real activities).
      – Be prepared for case‑by‑case examination by the Indian tax department, providing documentary and factual evidence of residency beyond the certificate.
