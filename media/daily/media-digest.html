Middle East LNG Disruption Tightens Global Supply and Extends Procurement Timelines

GLOBAL SPAL

The conflict in the Middle East has knocked out roughly 20 % of global LNG supply, with two major LNG trains offline removing 12‑13 Mtpa (≈3 % of world supply) and likely remaining out of service for three to five years. The United States, the main flexible source, is already operating at or above name‑plate capacity, limiting any short‑term surge in volumes. Replacement cargoes must come from the Atlantic basin, leading to longer voyages, higher freight costs and structurally higher delivered prices. Spot LNG prices sit around $20/MMBtu, forward prices $18‑19/MMBtu, and are projected to stay in the low‑teens for the next three to four years, with little chance of falling below $10/MMBtu. Asian markets, which import over 80 % of their LNG from the Gulf, face tighter procurement timelines and are responding with price‑led rationing, demand curtailment, and fuel switching back to coal, fuel oil, or other alternatives. India, Pakistan and Bangladesh are especially exposed due to limited gas storage and high reliance on LNG for power, heat and fertilizer production, making the region vulnerable to deeper economic impacts if disruptions persist
