The Science Behind an A380’s Thrust and Lift

TV18BRDCST

The video explains how the Airbus A380’s four turbofan engines generate thrust: air is drawn in, compressed, mixed with fuel, ignited, and expelled at high speed, producing forward force. Most thrust comes from the large front fan moving massive volumes of air. This thrust accelerates the aircraft down the runway, allowing air to flow over and under the wings, creating lift that overcomes the plane’s weight. Together, thrust and lift enable the 570‑ton A380 to take off and fly

KPMG Review Confirms IDFC First Bank Chandigarh Fraud Is Isolated

IDFCFIRSTB

A forensic review by KPMG concluded that the ₹646 crore fraud uncovered at IDFC First Bank’s Chandigarh branch was limited to that branch and involved collusion between branch staff, certain customer employees and external parties. The scheme used altered authorization letters, cheques, emails, forged fixed‑deposit advices and edited statements. The bank has repaid the amount with interest, recorded it in its accounts, asserts its core banking records are accurate, and is cooperating with investigators. The fraud was first flagged when a Haryana government department’s account balance discrepancy was discovered

SpaceX Secures Massive Cloud Compute Deal with Google

Google will pay SpaceX $920 million per month for access to roughly 110,000 NVIDIA GPUs and related compute resources from October 2026 through June 2029, a $11 billion annual revenue stream that serves as bridge capacity for Google’s Gemini Enterprise AI platform. The agreement, mirroring a larger $1.25 billion‑per‑month contract with Anthropic, lets SpaceX monetize unused compute while retaining flexibility to reallocate resources. These deals fund SpaceX’s AI‑infrastructure push ahead of its IPO, but the high GPU acquisition costs are driving losses and prompting considerations of in‑house GPU production, raising competitive tensions with Google Cloud and positioning SpaceX as a new player in the trillion‑dollar AI compute market

LIC’s Early Warnings on Rajesh Exports Ignored Until SEBI Probe

BFINVEST LICI RAJESHEXPO ARIHANTCAP MCX

Over the past five years, India’s largest institutional investor, LIC, repeatedly flagged serious accounting and governance concerns at Rajesh Exports—citing lack of transparency, misclassification of interest income, and non‑disclosure of subsidiary finances—by voting against most shareholder resolutions. Other major investors such as Norway’s sovereign wealth fund, Schwab’s emerging‑markets ETF, and Legal & General also opposed key proposals, especially re‑electing Chairman Rajesh Mehta and the auditor. Despite these red flags, the company’s resolutions passed, aided by Mehta’s 54.5% family stake. SEBI’s interim order on 3 June 2026 confirmed revenue inflation of up to ₹1.5 trillion through mis‑classified subsidiary transactions, validating LIC’s earlier concerns. Retail investors, whose holdings grew from 1.6% to 14.1% of the share capital, suffered heavy losses as the stock fell over 80% in five years. Three Remaining 60 Minutes Correspondents Choose to Stay Amid CBS Shake‑up

PARACABLES

In a memo obtained by the AP, Lesley Stahl, Jon Wertheim, and Bill Whitaker said they will remain at “60 Minutes” because they don’t want to see the iconic program die. Their decision follows a series of firings and departures ordered by new CBS News editor‑in‑chief Bari Weiss and executive producer Nick Bilton, including the ousting of longtime staff like Tanya Simon, Sharyn Alfonsi, Cecilia Vega, and the recent dismissal of Scott Pelley after a tense confrontation. The three correspondents expressed regret over how those journalists were treated, but said they are trying to rebuild trust with Bilton and will stay as long as they can continue the show’s tradition of independent, fearless journalism, leaving open the possibility of leaving later. Their stay is seen as a key step for Bilton in stabilizing the show ahead of its September season launch

Trump Weighs IPO for Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac Amid Uncertainty

MCCHRLS-B

President Donald Trump indicated that his administration is still considering an initial public offering of shares in mortgage giants Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, despite recent leadership changes at the Federal Housing Finance Agency and the intelligence community. The proposal, which could value the firms at around $500 billion and raise roughly $30 billion by selling 5‑15% of stock, has faced fluctuating market reactions, with shares falling over 30% this year amid concerns that a sale might raise mortgage rates and affect housing affordability. The administration has not yet made a final decision
