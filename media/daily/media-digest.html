ChatGPT Health: Can You Really Trust AI With Your Medical Advice? | The Big Question 2026-01-08 21:22

Summary

OpenAI has launched “ChatGPT Health,” a dedicated AI service that can read medical records, sync with wellness apps, and give personalized health information while claiming extra privacy safeguards and that the data won’t be used to train its core models.

The panel—comprising a critical‑care physician, a holistic‑health guru, a clinical psychologist, a cyber‑security expert, and a health‑tech investor—debated its promise and pitfalls:

  • Clinical perspective (Dr. Ashutosh Devari) – AI can help narrow down possible diagnoses and explain lab results, but it cannot replace a doctor’s judgment or prescribe treatment. Relying on it alone can be dangerous, as illustrated by a recent case where the AI gave a generic antiplatelet recommendation that conflicted with a patient’s bleeding risk.

  • Holistic‑health view (Miki Mehta) – Health is highly individualized, involving personality, lifestyle, and emotional context. AI’s statistical approach can miss these nuances; face‑to‑face interaction remains essential for true healing and post‑procedure recovery.

  • Cyber‑security angle (Ritesh Bhatia) – Past OpenAI breaches and the inherent value of personal health data raise doubts about the promised “secure universe.” Risks include data hallucination, bias toward Western norms, and potential misuse by insurers or pharma companies despite encryption claims.

  • Psychological angle (Mezzaveen Doherty) – AI lacks ethical duty of care, legal liability, and the ability to read non‑verbal cues. It may be useful for information gathering or low‑level support, but diagnosis, treatment planning, and therapeutic responsibility must stay with qualified professionals.

  • Business outlook (Arpita Garwal) – Smaller health‑tech firms must differentiate themselves by layering domain‑specific, culturally relevant data on top of the generic AI model. The AI wave will force a redesign of product strategies, but it won’t outright replace niche players; accuracy and hallucination remain major hurdles.

  • Regulatory & accountability concerns – AI tools are not regulated as medical devices and have no clear liability chain. Until a feedback loop and accountability framework are established, they should be positioned only as adjuncts, especially in underserved rural areas where doctor scarcity is acute.

Overall consensus: ChatGPT Health can democratize access to medical information, aid preparation for doctor visits, and provide quick insights, but it cannot substitute clinical judgment, empathy, or legal accountability. Users should treat it as an informational aid, verify its output with qualified professionals, and remain vigilant about privacy and data‑security implications.

Direct‑tax side (Pranav Saita)

- Clarifications sought on: fast‑track de‑mergers’ tax neutrality, treatment of share buy‑backs, ESOP‑tax deferral, and the anti‑abuse provision on below‑fair‑value share acquisitions.

Indirect‑tax side (Sorabh Agarwal)

TDS rationalisation
- Consolidate the current 37 TDS provisions into 3‑4 broad categories (salary, punitive high‑rate, and a single general rate) to cut compliance and litigation.

Customs tariff rationalisation
- Align duty structures with the “Bharat‑self‑reliant” manufacturing push: lower duties on raw materials, medium duties on intermediates, higher duties on finished goods.
- Use sector‑specific incentive schemes (≈ ₹25,000 cr) to gradually raise duties on imports that hinder domestic production (e.g., mobile phones) and lower them once local capacity is built, creating a level playing field for Indian and foreign firms.

Overall, EY urges the Finance Minister to embed these certainty‑driven, simplification‑focused measures in the upcoming Union Budget to spur private investment, job creation and a more competitive customs regime.
