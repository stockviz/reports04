Feb WPI Near Forecast, March Set to Test Inflation and Growth

CUB

In February, India’s Wholesale Price Index (WPI) rose 2.13% year‑on‑year, matching the 2% poll expectation, with core inflation at 3.3% and a modest 0.5% month‑on‑month rise. Analysts note that the February figure is likely the last “benign” reading; March’s WPI is projected to accelerate to around 2.6%‑3%, driven by higher commodity costs (petroleum, chemicals, fertilizers, aluminium, metals, plastics). The rise will lift nominal GDP and tax‑to‑GDP ratios but compress corporate margins, GVA and EBITDA, and could eventually feed into CPI inflation. Recent metal price corrections eased February’s pressure, but supply disruptions—particularly in gas, oil, and key inputs—pose a downward bias for Q4 output, potentially revising real GDP growth from the forecast 7% to nearer 6.5% while nominal growth could appear near 11% due to the WPI surge. The consensus is to watch March closely as it may signal a shift from subdued inflation to higher price pressures across the economy

Honda Begins Pan‑India Road Testing of 0 Alpha EV SUV Ahead of 2027 Launch

HYUNDAI M&M MARUTI TATACAP LANDMARK

Production Disruption Risks in Auto Sector Amid Gas Supply Issues

M&M MARUTI

Analyst Jay Kale warns that ongoing gas supply constraints, heightened by the Iran conflict, could cause production disruptions across the entire automotive supply chain, affecting all OEMs. The impact will hinge on diesel and petrol price changes, consumer cost‑of‑ownership concerns, and rising commodity costs that pressure margins. While no single company can be singled out as the most vulnerable, the weakest link—any small tier‑1 supplier lacking gas—could choke the whole chain. Historically, similar shocks (e.g., the Russia‑Ukraine war) have led to stock corrections but also recovery opportunities. Kale remains optimistic about firms with strong market‑share gains and pricing power, especially in two‑wheelers (e.g., Hero) and passenger‑vehicle leaders like Mahindra and Maruti, which are better positioned to pass raw‑material costs onto consumers

EV Motor Localisation Deadline Extended to 2026

EICHERMOT TATAMOTORS

The Indian government has pushed the domestic‑manufacturing deadline for traction motors in electric buses and N2‑category trucks to September 1 2026 under the PM E‑Drive scheme, with a partial requirement from September 2025 for motor‑transmission assemblies. The move, announced via a Gazette notification on March 13 2026, aims to ease supply‑chain pressures—especially rare‑earth magnet shortages—by allowing OEMs to continue importing sub‑assemblies while building local capacity. Major commercial‑vehicle makers such as Tata Motors, Ashok Leyland, Eicher Motors and EKA Mobility, as well as bus specialists like JBM Auto and PMI Electro Mobility, stand to benefit, avoiding production disruptions and supporting broader EV deployment. Domestic suppliers like Sona BLW Precision Forgings and Pricol also gain time to expand production of motor components, controllers, inverters and related systems
