Higher Difference Between LTCG On Equity, Debt In India Vs Many Large Developed Markets: Experts

PRUDMOULI LICHSGFIN AXISBANK LGBBROSLTD HYUNDAI WHIRLPOOL CUB

Summary

  • Context: Indian equity markets are down ~5% from all‑time highs and ~4% in early 2026, prompting calls to lower long‑term capital‑gains tax (LTCG) on equities. The current tax structure creates a large disparity between the 12.5 % LTCG rate on equities and the 30‑+ % marginal tax on interest income from debt, deposits, and bonds.

  • Key Points from the Experts:

    1. G Mahalingam (ex‑RBI/SEBI):
      • The high equity LTCG benefit encourages risk‑seeking behavior, pushing retail and corporate savers into equities despite higher volatility.
      • The differential (equity LTCG ≈ 12.5 % vs. interest tax ≈ 33‑42 %) is effectively a “wealth tax” on fixed‑income returns, discouraging investment in debt.
      • Over‑taxation of debt may distort asset allocation and raise financing costs for the economy.
    2. Anantranarayan (former banker/SEBI):
      • Tax considerations dominate asset allocation; retirees are forced to take more equity risk than their appetite permits.
      • Interest income taxed at marginal rates without indexation acts like a wealth tax, pushing investors toward equities.
      • A uniform, low LTCG rate across asset classes would let investors allocate based on risk, not tax.
    3. Srinivasan Varadarajan (ex‑Axis/Union Bank):
      • From a policy angle, the government seeks more equity capital to fuel growth; hence the preferential equity tax.
      • However, 80 % of debt capital is institutional; retail‑driven equity incentives have limited macro impact.
      • The current incentive has led to a surge in equity inflows (≈ 8.8 lakh crore) but also inflated valuations and misallocation of resources.
      • High equity tax advantage has inadvertently facilitated foreign direct investment (FDI) outflows, as multinational firms find Indian share prices cheap and repatriate capital.
      • The tilt risks depriving the debt market of needed funding, especially for lower‑rated borrowers and MSMEs that rely on banks.

  • Implications:

    • For Debt Markets: Reduced attractiveness of debt could tighten funding for corporates, especially MSMEs, leading to higher financing costs and potential slowdown in productive investment.
    • For Banks: Deposit growth is slowing; banks may face funding gaps while still bearing the burden of financing weaker borrowers.
    • For Capital Allocation: Tax‑driven skew toward equities may cause asset‑class misallocation, inflated equity valuations, and volatility.
    • For Macro Stability: Persistent current‑account deficits (~2.5 %) combined with weakened debt capital flows could strain external financing and amplify macro‑economic risks.

  • Suggested Policy Direction:

    • Re‑evaluate the LTCG rate to reduce the gap between equity and debt taxation.
    • Consider a uniform, low tax on long‑term gains across asset classes, possibly indexed for inflation.
    • Align tax policy with the goal of balanced financial deepening—encouraging both equity risk capital and robust debt markets.
    • Use the upcoming budget(s) to address the tax distortion, support bond‑market development, and safeguard funding for the MSME sector.
