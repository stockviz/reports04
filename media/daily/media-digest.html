Bill Gates On India: Cutting Child Deaths, AI & Digital Innovation | Davos 2026

Bill Gates, speaking at Davos 2026, acknowledges current geopolitical turbulence but remains optimistic that cooperation and innovation will prevail. He highlights the dramatic reduction in child mortality since the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation began, crediting partnerships with India—especially the digital health platform ADHR and DigiLocker‑type identity systems—as models that can be replicated worldwide via open‑source projects like MOSIP. Gates sees AI as a catalyst for progress in education (personalized tutoring), agriculture (precision advice for smallholders), and health (virtual doctors, language‑specific support), while warning of risks: weaponisation, deepfakes, cyber‑attacks, job displacement, and the need for regulatory standards and social safety nets. He cites upcoming AI‑driven pilots in India and the Horizon 1000 program in Africa (starting in Rwanda) that aim to streamline clinic workflows and expand access to care. Despite acknowledging threats such as nuclear weapons and fragmented global cooperation, Gates concludes that strong U.S.–India ties, continued philanthropic effort, and the transformative power of technology keep him fundamentally optimistic.

What Are The Key Stocks & Sectors In Focus Today? | Market Cues With Dipan Mehta Of Elixir Equities

WIPRO SKYGOLD DRREDDY KALYANKJIL ICICIBANK RELIANCE

Deepan Mehta, Director at Elixir Equities, says the Indian market is being dragged down by geopolitical tensions, persistent foreign‑institutional investor outflows and a weak rupee, which are all interlinked. He remains hopeful that the current earnings season could spark a reversal, despite recent large‑cap disappointments (e.g., Reliance, Wipro).

Regarding the upcoming budget, Mehta expects a focus on export‑oriented incentives and import‑substitution schemes (e.g., PLI extensions), rather than solely on defense spending, which he views as a known but limited catalyst. He suggests investors watch for policies that boost exporters and substitute imports across sectors such as electronics.

“US AGI Is Overhyped”: IBM CEO & India’s IT Minister Talk India’s AI Sovereignty At Davos 2026

TATACAP

Summary of the Davos 2026 conversation with IBM CEO Arvind Krishna and India’s IT Minister Ashwini Vaishnava

  • AI sovereignty & ecosystem – Krishna stresses that AI is a decades‑long strategic asset; India must develop its own “sovereign” models rather than rely on US or Chinese providers. Small, domain‑specific models (≈50‑120 B parameters) can handle >95 % of enterprise workloads, making them cost‑effective and easier to adapt to Indian data (languages, health, defense, legal).

  • Economic & risk factors – India’s lag in deep‑learning breakthroughs stems from limited capital and a risk‑averse culture (90 % failure rate is discouraged). A larger GDP would free up a trillion‑dollar‑scale AI budget, enabling more experimentation.

  • Infrastructure roadmap

    • Semiconductors – India is building a fab ecosystem (10 plants under construction, pilot production beginning now, full commercial output by 2026). The plan follows a “walk‑before‑run” path: start with 28‑90 nm nodes, then progress to 7 nm by 2030 and 3 nm thereafter.
    • Compute & energy – $70 bn slated for AI infrastructure; private‑sector nuclear energy (Shanti Act) will support power‑intensive workloads.
    • Models – IBM is supporting 12 Indian‑focused LLM projects, with 3‑4 slated for release soon; these are tailored for sector‑specific use‑cases and can be infused with sovereign data.

  • Application layer & ROI – The highest value will come from AI‑driven enterprise applications, not from maintaining legacy systems. Indian IT services firms are already pivoting, with AI hiring up 33 % and a focus on rapid, cost‑efficient deployments.

  • Global AI dynamics –

    • US view – Some chase AGI by scaling compute, but Krishna argues true progress will require integrating knowledge into models, not just larger parameters.
    • China view – Emphasis on embedding AI across all processes; India should combine this application focus with its own sovereign model stack.

  • India’s competitive positioning – Although China has a two‑decade head start, AI is a multi‑decade evolution. By leveraging low‑cost, high‑efficiency small models and building a full stack (chips → compute → models → applications), India can become a third pillar alongside the US and China.

  • Advice to the next generation –

    • Pursue deep, critical thinking across subjects (physics, math, humanities).
    • Use AI as an assistive tool, not a replacement; retain ownership of final outputs.
    • Leverage India’s growing startup ecosystem; risk‑taking is now more supported than in the past.
    • Recognize that volume‑driven businesses can fund deeper‑tech ventures (e.g., chip design startups now receiving VC backing).

  • Future initiatives –

    • AI Impact Summit (February) will showcase how AI can boost productivity, position India as a model for the Global South (akin to the UPI stack), and embed safety guardrails in the AI stack.
    • Ongoing collaboration with IBM to accelerate semiconductor manufacturing and model development, aiming for India to rank among the top‑four/five semiconductor nations by 2030.
