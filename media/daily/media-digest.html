Centre’s Draft Social Security Rules To Redefine Benefits And Ensure Fair Treatment For Gig Workers?

ZOMATO ETERNAL DHANI TEAMGTY

Unit‑economics:
- Avg. order ₹600 → platform take‑rate 15 % (₹90) + brand commission 3 % (₹18) + delivery‑charge (≈₹12) ≈ ₹120 revenue per order.
- Costs: dark‑store rent/operations ≈ ₹42, rider payout ≈ ₹50, shrinkage/other ≈ ₹40 → total ≈ ₹132.
- Result: –2 % contribution margin at ₹600 AOV; breakeven only around ₹750‑₹800, improving with higher AOV or higher take‑rate. Rider payout is the biggest cost lever; a ₹10 rise pushes breakeven AOV up by ≈₹100.

Gig‑worker earnings:
- Quick‑commerce rider gets ₹40‑₹50 per order (≈₹90‑₹130 gross/hr, net ₹70‑₹120 after expenses).
- Food‑delivery riders earn slightly more per order but travel farther; earnings per hour are the key metric.

Labor dispute (Dec 2025‑Jan 2026):
- Gig unions issued 15 demands, notably a ₹40,000 minimum monthly wage and a ban on 10‑minute deliveries.
- Nationwide strike claimed >2 lakh participants; platforms reported record volumes the next day, suggesting limited impact.
- Politicians criticised the 10‑minute model; platforms defended it.

Social‑security & employment model:
- ~10‑15 % of riders work full‑time (≈8‑10 hrs/day); ~80‑90 % are pure gig workers across multiple platforms.
- Courts recognise the need for social security but have not defined mechanisms for multi‑platform workers.
- Platforms are gradually converting some logistics roles to full‑time contracts, but FT wages stay similar to gig earnings because PF/ESIC contributions reduce take‑home pay.
- Any increase in rider payout or mandated benefits will likely be passed to consumers, further squeezing already thin margins.

Government role:
- Debate over market‑driven vs. regulatory intervention.
- Proposed wage code and social‑security mandates could raise platform costs; without clear employer attribution for multi‑platform workers, implementation is complex.

Growth outlook (Team Lee/quick‑commerce staffing):
- Quick‑commerce now accounts for ~6 % of total headcount; contributes 20‑25 % of annual hiring growth.
- Monthly hiring for quick‑commerce roles ≈ 3,000‑4,000 positions, expanding from metros into Tier‑2/3 cities.

Bottom line: Quick‑commerce profitability hinges on higher average order values or lower rider payouts; rising labor demands and potential regulatory social‑security obligations threaten margins and may be transferred to end‑users, while the sector continues to be a major hiring engine despite these challenges.

TATASTEEL SWIGGY JINDALSTEL GMBREW TBZ MCX ZOMATO NATCOPHARM PRECAM TITAN NAUKRI 5PAISA JSWSTEEL

Manishy Ray Chaudhary, CEO of Emory Capital Partners, warned that strained government finances will likely push authorities to raise taxes on sin goods (tobacco, liquor) and possibly other consumables, while also seeking non‑tax revenue through privatizations and monetisation.

He highlighted that geopolitical tensions will remain a “new normal” in 2026, driving higher defence spending in India and Korea and underpinning a strong commodity cycle—especially in gold, base metals and copper/aluminium, where Emory favours cheaper Chinese miners over Indian peers.

Foreign‑institutional inflows into India remain low because earnings growth, currency stability, and interest‑rate outlook are not yet favourable; a stable US‑India trade deal and revived earnings could revive FIIs.

Emory holds no positions in quick‑commerce firms (Zomato, Swiggy, Zepto). While labour‑union disputes may cause short‑term pain, Chaudhary sees a longer growth runway and would consider buying after a 20% price correction.

He concluded that broader Indian markets are currently range‑bound, noting modest gains in stocks such as MCX, Precision Wires, Natco, MV, and GM Breweries, despite occasional volatility.

60 Years Of Thermax | Last Few Months Have Seen Some Green Shoots In Private Capex, Says Company

THERMAX

Thermax, marking its 60‑year anniversary, is pivoting from a traditional boilermaking business to a “new‑energy solutions” provider. CEO Ashish Bhindari says the shift is driven by the global energy‑transition, India’s “Vixit Bharat” ambition, and emerging high‑growth sectors.

Capex outlook
- Thermax is primarily a private‑capex business; public‑capex plays a minor role.
- Post‑election slowdown and the slowdown in ethanol after India moved to E20 dented orders, but the last few months have seen a resurgence.
- New private‑capex pipelines are coming from data‑centers, electronics manufacturing, semiconductors, solar‑module production and related infrastructure, which Thermax is now targeting.

Data‑center wins
- Two recent large contracts: 23 absorption chillers for a marquee US data‑center (first phase) and a hybrid closed‑loop cooling‑tower system for a major Indian data‑center.
- These projects mark Thermax’s entry into the data‑center cooling market.

FGD (flue‑gas desulphurisation)
- The company has stopped expecting new FGD orders for the next three years and is completing its existing backlog, which should be cleared by FY 27.
- No further central‑government mandates are anticipated to revive the segment.

Portfolio reshaping
- Low‑margin legacy businesses are being wound down, with remaining projects (including FGD) slated for completion by FY 27.
- Bio‑CNG remains a strategic focus; despite technical challenges, it is viewed as a potential “next‑generation ethanol” for India.

Growth pockets
1. New industrial segments (data‑centers, electronics, semiconductors, solar).
2. Clean‑water solutions.
3. International markets.

Export trajectory
- Exports currently account for about 14 % of revenue; Thermax aims to double that share within the next three years.
- Fastest‑growing regions are the Middle East, sub‑Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia and the United States, where recent data‑center and other projects are driving demand.
