US Job Cuts In January At The Highest Level Since 2009 Recession | CNBC TV18

U.S. employers announced over 1.8 million job cuts in January—double the previous year and the highest monthly total since the 2009 recession, with a 200 % jump from December. Hiring intentions fell to just 5,300 workers, the lowest January figure recorded since tracking began in 2009. The largest layoffs came from Amazon (16,000 corporate jobs), UPS (30,000 shifts toward high‑margin health‑care logistics), and Dow (4,500, 13 % of its workforce, to enable AI‑driven automation). AI‑related cuts accounted for only about 7 % of the total (7,624 jobs); most layoffs were driven by contract losses, economic uncertainty, restructuring, cost control, over‑hiring, and bureaucratic flattening. The trend is expected to continue, as evidenced by a February restructuring at Amazon’s newsroom that eliminated 300 of 800 journalists, signaling broader de‑risking amid weak hiring confidence.

It’s all about hours clocked—Urban Company data punctures work value talk

ZOMATO URBANCO

Urban Company’s data (April‑Dec 2025) shows its top 5% of professionals work ~167 hours/month—about twice the average – and earn roughly double the monthly income (₹51,673 vs ₹28,322), but their higher pay comes from longer hours and more orders, not higher hourly rates (₹310‑₹313 for all). CEOs of aggregators claim gig jobs match entry‑level IT salaries and promote upskilling, but critics say the model forces longer hours for shrinking take‑home pay without real career progression.

RBI Post Monetary Policy Presser | VRRs & OMOs Are Liquidity Tools, Growth Is Looking Better: Guv

CONTROLPR SIL TIMESGTY TVVISION SABEVENTS

Summary of RBI Post‑Monetary‑Policy Press Conference

  • Liquidity Management: The RBI emphasized its duty to provide ample, sufficient liquidity to meet the economy’s productive needs and to ensure monetary‑policy transmission across all markets (overnight, money, government‑security, corporate credit). It will continue to use its liquidity tools—Open Market Operations (OMOs), short‑ and long‑term Variable Rate Repo (VRR) facilities, etc.—to keep overnight rates anchored at the policy repo rate. Liquidity operations are ongoing and will be supplied “proactively and pre‑emptively” as needed, even outside formal policy statements.

  • Government Borrowing & Yield Curve: The government’s borrowing program is being viewed in net terms rather than gross. Net borrowing for the current fiscal year is about ₹11.5 lakh crore, with the next year projected at ₹11.7 lakh crore, a modest increase despite a larger budget. Additional liquidity will come from Treasury‑bill issuances and planned “switches” of roughly ₹2.5 lakh crore; buybacks are not currently factored in.

  • Market Liquidity & Credit‑Deposit Ratio: The RBI sees no immediate need to change the liquidity‑management framework (call‑money corridor, STF limits, etc.). The credit‑deposit (CD) ratio is cyclical; a high CD ratio is expected when credit growth outpaces deposits. The regulator stresses that liquidity adequacy is monitored through LCR and NSFR, which remain at comfortable levels.

  • External Sector Outlook: Macro fundamentals are strong—current‑account surplus, robust reserves, and multiple bilateral/multilateral deals (EU, EFTA, Oman, UK, etc.) are improving the external position. Capital‑account liberalisation (e.g., insurance‑sector FDI, data‑centre tax holidays, safe‑harbor rules) is expected to attract further investment.

  • Inflation & Growth: Inflation is still within the “sweet spot.” Core inflation, excluding volatile food and precious‑metal prices, remains benign, while growth prospects have improved, supporting the RBI’s confidence in meeting its dual objectives.

  • Customer‑Centric Measures: The RBI highlighted new guidelines on mis‑selling, a compensation framework for small‑value frauds, and broader efforts to enhance customer experience across banks and NBFCs. OTP‑related frauds will be covered under the compensation scheme.

  • Overall Stance: The RBI will maintain its current policy stance—anchoring overnight rates at the repo rate, providing liquidity as required, monitoring government borrowing, and ensuring transmission of policy rates—while remaining confident about the external sector and inflation outlook.
