Key Takeaways from Storyboard18’s Marketing Round‑Table: Diamond Legacy, Gen Z, and India’s Evolving Ad Landscape

3MINDIA CIPLA VISACHROME

The episode opened with a snapshot of India’s ad market – WPP projects an 8.8 % CAGR to Rs 2 lakh crore by 2026, digital now exceeds $8.3 bn while TV falls 6.8 %; growth is driven by micro‑drama, retail media and AI. New compliance rules (RBI on influencer‑led financial promos, MIB’s broadcast rulebook) and the resolution of the trade‑desk vs ad‑tech fee dispute set the regulatory backdrop. Uber’s multi‑touchpoint ad platform, creator‑economy rates (Summer Renner earning ~₹76 lakh per reel), Instagram’s global rollout of post‑view reel ads, and Levi’s creative FIFA stadium work illustrate shifting media tactics. Leadership moves at Cipla, Big Basket, and Schneider Electric were noted.

In the first interview, Toranj Mehta (DBEARS) explained that the biggest challenge for natural diamonds is consumer confusion with lab‑grown stones; natural diamonds retain rarity, value and “preciousness” that lab‑grown pieces lack (prices have fallen ~90 % in three years). Indian buying is increasingly emotional and self‑driven – 64 % of purchases are made by women themselves, with Gen Z accounting for 51 % of buyers. DBEARS leverages India’s heritage (origin of diamonds, Maharaja legacy) while offering contemporary, personality‑driven designs.

Forevermark’s evolution since its 2011 launch was outlined: a shift from dealer partnerships to exclusive retail stores, introduction of Indian‑specific services (gold‑exchange, upgrade schemes, savings plans) and localized designs (mangalsutra, nose‑pins). The “Love From Dad” campaign was born from observing the ritual of a second ear‑piercing, using the father‑daughter bond to showcase diamond gifting beyond the traditional husband‑gift model.

The second segment tackled cultural relevance. Speakers distinguished “borrowing” cultural symbols (celebrity, song, costume) from true cultural connection, which requires understanding underlying societal motivations. Gen Z’s media habits—reading comments instead of columns—mean brands now react to emotional audience feedback, offering both opportunities and distortions.

Nikhil Sharma (Perfetti) defended long‑form TV for mass‑appeal products, while Visa’s Gaurav Ramdev described building an “invisible” brand through reliability, security and rewards. Parle’s discussion highlighted the high last‑mile cost that limits D2C, the need for regional, emotionally resonant messaging, and the balance between mindful consumption and product availability.

Overall, the show underscored India’s booming ad spend, tightening compliance, the premiumization wave, and the strategic pivots brands must make to engage a financially empowered, culturally savvy Gen Z audience

AI Disruption in Indian Tech: Demand, Hiring Trends, and Future Opportunities

TCS PAISALO JARO INFY

The panelists, Raghunath Anant Malshikar and Neeti Sharma, discussed the current state of demand in India’s tech industry, emphasizing that despite short‑term weakness—partly reflected in Accenture’s earnings—the sector remains strong due to India’s large pool of AI talent (16% of global AI experts). Sharma outlined four industry shifts: moving from volume to value, headcount to productivity, generic to specialized skills, and a transition to a “diamond‑shaped” workforce. While traditional IT services hiring has dipped 30‑40%, demand for AI‑related roles is rising, with hiring expected to rebound in 6‑12 months across both tech and non‑tech sectors (banking, healthcare, logistics, e‑commerce). Malshikar stressed that AI changes tasks, not jobs, and that entry‑level roles will evolve from routine coding to solution‑engineering, requiring new skill sets. Both agreed that India’s biggest AI advantage lies in a combination of exporting talent, services, and eventually AI‑enabled products, leveraging the nation’s “affordable excellence” to create and distribute high‑value, low‑cost solutions such as AI‑driven healthcare diagnostics. The consensus is that overall hiring will stay positive, but the nature of work and required competencies will transform

Fortune 500 2026: Record Profits, Falling Workforce

BCG AHL SHBAJRG GALAXYSURF

The 2026 Fortune 500 posted a record $21 trillion in revenue and $2.1 trillion in profit, while total employment dropped 301,049 jobs to 30.5 million. The decline stems mainly from large firms like Walgreens Boots Alliance and Nordstrom leaving the list after go‑private deals, removing over 650,000 jobs, while the 22 newcomers added only 317,000 positions. Established companies barely hired—overall a 0.1 % increase—with Amazon adding 20,000 workers and Walmart holding flat, while UnitedHealth cut 10,000. Retail remains the biggest employer but shed 0.9 % of staff; technology fell 1 % and financials grew 0.9 %. Revenue per employee hit $687,094 and profit per employee $68,743, the highest ever, reflecting AI‑driven productivity gains that are not translating into broader hiring. Harvard economist Lawrence Katz warns this “low‑hire, low‑fire” pattern may widen as AI reshapes business models

Tata Consumer’s Push for Higher Margins via Distribution Overhaul

ADFFOODS FINEORG HINDUNILVR TATACONSUM NESTLEIND

Tata Consumer Products aims to raise its EBITDA margin to 17% within three years by revamping its distribution model and deepening market penetration. After a 15% revenue jump to ₹20,290 cr in FY‑26, the firm’s EBITDA margin sat at 13.9%, lagging peers like HUL and Nestlé. To bridge the gap, it added 189 new distributors, shifted 356 to a new go‑to‑market framework, and restructured general trade, splitting markets by product focus (salt vs. non‑salt) and growth potential. The company is expanding quick‑commerce (now ~20% of sales) and e‑commerce channels, which grew 62% in FY‑26, and targeting white‑space segments such as food‑service and pharmacy via recent acquisitions (Capital Foods, Organic India). These moves, alongside strategic pricing, trade‑margin management, promotion optimization, and price architecture, are designed to drive 70–100 bps annual margin improvement and support faster‑growing product lines that crossed ₹4 trn in FY‑26. Despite a 6% share dip in 2026, the strategy seeks to outpace the broader FMCG index decline
