India’s Solar Export Boom Crumbles as US Tariffs Bite

VIKRAMSOLR WAAREEENER ADANIENPP1 NDTV PREMIERENE

US anti‑dumping and countervailing duties, now over 230%, have slashed Indian solar module exports, wiping out a $2 billion market that once offered 50‑100% higher margins. Most top Indian manufacturers reported near‑zero exports in Q4 FY26; Waaree Energies, the sole exporter, saw shipments fall due to shipping delays and tariff pressures. Companies are shifting focus to the domestic market and seeking alternative supply chains—e.g., sourcing cells from Ethiopia—to bypass US origin rules, while some explore new markets (Europe, Middle East, Australia) but face stiff Chinese competition. Plans for US‑based production are being reconsidered as regulatory hurdles and talent shortages persist, and the broader export slump worsens India’s current‑account deficit

Flipkart’s Gamified, AI‑Driven Push to Convert Bharat’s New Internet Users into Shoppers

MEESHO SHBAJRG 3MINDIA

Flipkart is revamping its hyper‑value platform Shopsy into a feed‑led, game‑and‑reward‑centric app that uses AI to personalize recommendations, aiming to familiarize tier‑2/3/4 consumers with digital experiences before urging purchases. Executives say the goal is to build comfort with digitization rather than immediate sales, betting that higher engagement will eventually translate into commerce. The move counters competition from Meesho, Amazon Bazaar, and offline retailers, and leverages AI tools for sellers while operating a no‑commission model across 1,300 categories. Critics warn that gamification may not guarantee conversions, but Flipkart views Shopsy as a long‑term onboarding layer for India’s next wave of online shoppers

India’s EV Cell Industry Pushes to Close China Price Gap

RELIANCE OLAELEC ARE&M RAJESHEXPO EXIDEIND STYRENIX

Indian lithium‑ion cell makers are ramping up domestic production and seeking government incentives to narrow a 20‑30% cost gap with Chinese rivals. Upcoming Chinese VAT hikes could raise imported cell prices by about 9%, while India’s lithium‑ion imports surged 56% to $4.6 bn in FY‑26, with two‑thirds coming from China. Companies such as Exide, Amara Raja, Agratas and Ola Electric plan new production lines, but face challenges including the lack of local chemistry expertise (NMC, LFP), limited PLI fund disbursements, and dependence on Chinese equipment. Industry calls for incentives on raw‑material inputs, a dedicated Battery Component and Materials Mission, and workforce upskilling to build a robust supplier ecosystem and achieve price competitiveness

PayMate’s Payroll Crisis Tied to Pending DigiAsia Deal

BMWVENTLTD 3MINDIA BCONCEPTS DELTACORP VISACHROME PAYTM

Visa‑backed B2B payments firm PayMate India Ltd has been unable to pay employee salaries for up to a year, linking the clearance of dues to the closing of a restructured, cash‑free stock‑swap acquisition of DigiAsia Corp and subsequent funding. The deal, originally announced as a $400 million transaction with at least $25 million cash, is now a pure stock swap, and the company says payroll will be settled by 30 June once the deal and a new capital raise are completed. PayMate has faced senior‑leadership exits, legal notices from staff over unpaid wages, and operational cutbacks, including the shutdown of its West Asian unit. The liquidity strain intensified after RBI’s 2024 crackdown on intermediary‑routed card transactions. The firm, which previously withdrew a ₹1,500 crore IPO plan, still aims to pursue a public listing after the DigiAsia transaction closes

AI Costs Outpace Expectations, Prompting Corporate Pullbacks

Major firms like Microsoft and Uber are scaling back AI tool usage after discovering that token‑based pricing makes large‑scale deployment far more expensive than hiring human workers. Microsoft halted most Claude Code licences, shifting to GitHub Copilot CLI, while Uber burned its entire 2026 AI budget in four months despite incentivizing usage. Token consumption rises with usage and agentic AI, and even as per‑token prices fall, total spend climbs, outpacing anticipated cost reductions. Executives—including Nvidia’s VP of applied deep learning—acknowledge compute costs now exceed employee wages, casting doubt on ambitious AI‑agent visions without clearer economic solutions

Top Indian IT firms see 40% drop in H‑1B visas, TCS hit hardest, Infosys bucks the trend

TECHM KISSHT TCS INFY HCLTECH WIPRO

H‑1B approvals for India’s six largest IT services companies fell to 11,041 by March 2026—a 40% decline from the previous year—due to tighter U.S. visa rules and higher costs. Infosys secured the most visas (3,195) and even grew, while TCS experienced the steepest drop, losing 3,242 approvals to just 2,885. Firms are shifting to offshore work and local hiring to reduce visa dependence. Recent U.S. policy changes also raise H‑1B wage requirements, impose a $100,000 annual fee per foreign worker, and tighten green‑card rules, prompting industry and lawmakers to criticize the moves and consider legal challenges
