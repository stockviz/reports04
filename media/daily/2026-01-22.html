Bank of India Stuns With 16 Bps Surge In Margins; Believe Margin & Growth Are Sustainable, Says Co

ICICIBANK BANKINDIA

Bank of India posted a strong Q3, with net‑interest margin (NIM) rising 16 bps QoQ to 2.57 % despite a December repo‑rate cut. Advances grew 13.6 % YoY while deposits rose 11.6 % YoY; in absolute terms deposit growth (≈92 bn ₹) outpaced advances (≈81 bn ₹).

Key drivers
- Yield on advances held steady at 7.81 % by shifting a portion of low‑yield AAA‑rated PSU loans to higher‑margin AA‑rated advances.
- Cost of deposits fell to 4.77 % (down 8 bps), helped by cheaper bulk deposits versus retail term deposits.

Liquidity & asset quality
- CD ratio around 72 % with LCR comfortably above the 100 % regulatory minimum.
- Slippage ratio rose 2 bps to 0.57 % (SMA‑2) after one road‑asset turned NPA; overall SMA‑2 exposure remains low at 0.75 % of the loan book.
- Recovery in Q3 was ≈₹1,500 cr (cash + unrealised interest), and the bank expects ≈₹2,000 cr in Q4.

Growth focus
- Emphasis on RAM (Retail‑Agriculture‑MSME) portfolio, now 58 % of advances, targeting 65 % long‑term; RAM segments are growing 15‑20 % YoY.
- Anticipates margin bottom‑out in Q4 with potential improvement thereafter, provided no further repo cuts.

Guidance & outlook
- Expect NIM to hold in Q4, with margin improvement thereafter.
- ROA at 0.96 % (projected ≈1 % in Q4); FY26 annualised ROA targeted at 0.91‑0.92 %, moving toward 1 % in FY27.
- No consolidation plans disclosed.

The market rewarded the results with a ≈5 % share price gain.

Valuations & Earnings Are Looking Up For India: HSBC | Where Is Indian Market Headed In 2026?

CANHLIFE TRENT HDFC

Summary

  • Market outlook: India’s equity market has been “tough” but showed a brief rebound in late‑2023 Q4. Momentum has since shifted to Taiwan, South Korea and China because of the AI‑driven data‑center build‑out, pulling foreign investors (FIs) away from India.

  • Current FI sentiment: FIs remain underweight India, citing three main risks:

    1. Currency risk – a weakening rupee erodes returns; investors can mitigate by focusing on export‑oriented firms.
    2. Earnings sustainability – recent earnings upgrades are modest (≈8‑10% YoY); analysts expect 11‑13% growth next year, but the durability of this recovery is uncertain.
    3. Long‑term structural risk – rapid infrastructure investment (as seen in Indonesia) could intensify competition, compress margins and slow earnings growth.

  • Fiscal stance: India’s fiscal discipline is strong (deficit ~2.8‑2.9% of GDP), but some argue there is room for modest expansion to boost growth. Excessive tightening could pressure the rupee; a balanced mix of fiscal spending and rate cuts is needed.

  • Comparative earnings & valuations:

    • Taiwan & Korea: Companies like TSMC, Samsung and Hynix are posting 30‑50% earnings upgrades driven by AI/data‑center demand, trading at relatively rich multiples but with strong forward‑looking EPS growth.
    • China: Domestic investors dominate; foreign inflows remain limited. Massive household cash piles may eventually flow into equities or gold, but the immediate impact on FI allocations is muted.

  • Gold: Indian households hold ~35 000 t of gold (≈ $5 trn), now exceeding the total equity market value. This creates a wealth effect and a safe‑haven demand that could rise further amid geopolitical and dollar‑access concerns.

  • Key investment themes for India (2026):

    1. Domestic consumption: hospitals, auto manufacturers, jewellery and retail (e.g., Trent).
    2. Financials: banks with improving credit growth and reasonable valuations.
    3. Export‑oriented / IT exposure: for FI currency hedging.

  • Outlook for FI re‑entry: A return hinges on (a) rupee stabilization, (b) credible, sustainable earnings growth, and (c) a shift in global momentum away from Taiwan/Korea. If those conditions materialize, valuations could re‑rate and attract fresh foreign capital by 2026.

Summary of the Davos 2026 conversation with IBM CEO Arvind Krishna and India’s IT Minister Ashwini Vaishnava

  • AI sovereignty & ecosystem – Krishna stresses that AI is a decades‑long strategic asset; India must develop its own “sovereign” models rather than rely on US or Chinese providers. Small, domain‑specific models (≈50‑120 B parameters) can handle >95 % of enterprise workloads, making them cost‑effective and easier to adapt to Indian data (languages, health, defense, legal).

  • Economic & risk factors – India’s lag in deep‑learning breakthroughs stems from limited capital and a risk‑averse culture (90 % failure rate is discouraged). A larger GDP would free up a trillion‑dollar‑scale AI budget, enabling more experimentation.

  • Infrastructure roadmap

    • Semiconductors – India is building a fab ecosystem (10 plants under construction, pilot production beginning now, full commercial output by 2026). The plan follows a “walk‑before‑run” path: start with 28‑90 nm nodes, then progress to 7 nm by 2030 and 3 nm thereafter.
    • Compute & energy – $70 bn slated for AI infrastructure; private‑sector nuclear energy (Shanti Act) will support power‑intensive workloads.
    • Models – IBM is supporting 12 Indian‑focused LLM projects, with 3‑4 slated for release soon; these are tailored for sector‑specific use‑cases and can be infused with sovereign data.

  • Application layer & ROI – The highest value will come from AI‑driven enterprise applications, not from maintaining legacy systems. Indian IT services firms are already pivoting, with AI hiring up 33 % and a focus on rapid, cost‑efficient deployments.

  • Global AI dynamics –

    • US view – Some chase AGI by scaling compute, but Krishna argues true progress will require integrating knowledge into models, not just larger parameters.
    • China view – Emphasis on embedding AI across all processes; India should combine this application focus with its own sovereign model stack.

  • India’s competitive positioning – Although China has a two‑decade head start, AI is a multi‑decade evolution. By leveraging low‑cost, high‑efficiency small models and building a full stack (chips → compute → models → applications), India can become a third pillar alongside the US and China.

  • Advice to the next generation –

    • Pursue deep, critical thinking across subjects (physics, math, humanities).
    • Use AI as an assistive tool, not a replacement; retain ownership of final outputs.
    • Leverage India’s growing startup ecosystem; risk‑taking is now more supported than in the past.
    • Recognize that volume‑driven businesses can fund deeper‑tech ventures (e.g., chip design startups now receiving VC backing).

  • Future initiatives –

    • AI Impact Summit (February) will showcase how AI can boost productivity, position India as a model for the Global South (akin to the UPI stack), and embed safety guardrails in the AI stack.
    • Ongoing collaboration with IBM to accelerate semiconductor manufacturing and model development, aiming for India to rank among the top‑four/five semiconductor nations by 2030.
