Feel The Government Has Been Excessively Conservative With Revenue Collection Targets: Axis Bank 2026-02-02 10:38

AXISBANK OFSS 5PAISA

Summary

  • Predictable Budget: Nilkan Mishra praised the budget’s continuity and lack of tax shocks, saying predictability helps businesses plan and keeps spending efficient.

  • Revenue‑Collection Conservatism: He noted the government’s revenue targets are overly cautious. While small‑savings inflows rose 32 % in the first nine months of FY 26, the full‑year projection is down 13 % versus FY 25, implying a 1–1.5 lakh‑crore shortfall. This conservatism limits cash available to banks, pushing up government cash balances to ₹2.5‑3 lakh crore and reducing deposits for lending.

  • Financing Pressures: The conservative outlook raises borrowing needs, pushing 10‑year bond yields to 6.76 % (above the desired ~6 %). Higher yields increase banks’ borrowing costs (≈7 % for one‑month funds) and could dampen growth if financing remains expensive.

  • Fiscal Discipline vs. Yield Impact: Despite fiscal consolidation, the elevated yields stem from operational issues between the RBI and the government rather than fiscal laxity. Lower yields would support cheaper financing and help close the output gap.

  • Growth Outlook: Core inflation is low, indicating slack in the economy. With a trend growth target of ~7 % and a sizable output gap, closing it will require cheaper fiscal financing and supportive monetary policy.

  • Data‑Center Opportunity: Commitments from Microsoft, Amazon and Google amount to $70‑75 bn over 4‑5 years. Mishra sees tax incentives more as an enabling factor than a primary driver; the sector’s growth will depend on competitive energy, water, land, and infrastructure costs rather than solely on tax policy.

While Market Is Fixated On STT Hike, Everything Else In Budget 2026 Was Fantastic: S Naren

ICICIBANK PRUDMOULI

S Narayan, executive director and CIO of ISSA Prudential AMC, said the market’s focus on the Securities Transaction Tax (STT) increase is masking a largely positive Budget 2026. He praised the fiscal deficit’s containment, the data‑centre tax incentive aimed at attracting AI investment, and overall macro stability, noting that the central government’s finances are among the strongest in recent years.

He explained that the STT hike mainly hurts high‑frequency traders, while retail investors, who have historically lost money in derivatives, are unlikely to benefit. In fixed‑income, Narayan warned that the budget’s modest small‑savings‑scheme borrowing target may underestimate actual collections, implying net borrowing could be lower than forecast despite higher gross yields.

Regarding the reopening of ICICI’s small‑cap fund after a 22‑month suspension, he emphasized there is no current “euphoria” in the small‑cap space; valuations remain high across asset classes, so the fund’s relaunch is a precautionary move rather than a bullish signal.

On precious metals, Narayan called the recent rally “euphoric” and too volatile for standalone exposure. He advises investors to keep metal allocations within multi‑asset portfolios and cautions against silver ETFs, citing the difficulty of pricing commodities.

He noted foreign‑institutional investors remain interested in India because of its strong structural story, but high global valuations limit aggressive equity allocations. Asset allocation, not sector bets, remains the prudent approach.

Finally, Narayan said AI stocks, while expensive, have not entered a bubble like precious metals; a correction is possible but not imminent. A broader global equity correction could occur within the next two years, which would likely benefit Indian equities.

STT Hike| Bid & Ask Spreads Will Widen As High Frequency Trading Activity Declines: Crosseas Capital 2026-02-02 10:07

IIFLCAPS 5PAISA

The interview with Rajesh Bahiti, Managing Director of Trossees Capital Services, focuses on the recent ST T increase and its market repercussions. Bahiti argues that the government’s move is a signaling tool rather than a revenue‑raising measure, aiming to curb speculation in futures and options—sectors he likens to “palm‑smoking, tobacco, alcohol, casinos.” By raising ST T, especially on delivery‑based trades, the policy is expected to widen bid‑ask spreads and make high‑frequency trading (HFT) and arbitrage less profitable, thereby reducing the liquidity those participants provide.

Key points:

  • Liquidity impact: HFT firms, which supply most market depth, will face thinner margins, potentially withdrawing, which could strain liquidity for both futures and options.
  • Market behavior: Traders may shift from futures to options, but the overall volume could become unviable for professional participants, leading to broader market illiquidity.
  • Government intent: The tax hike is portrayed as a deterrent to speculative activity, not primarily a revenue source, though the government simultaneously raises taxes across capital‑market instruments (e.g., LTCG, AIF surcharge), indicating a broader trend of higher taxation on market participants.
  • Future outlook: Bahiti suggests that continued tax pressure may make the market less attractive for both retail and institutional traders, possibly prompting a slowdown in activity unless growth rates alone sustain market health.
  • Personal exposure: He avoids investing in capital‑market stocks to prevent conflicts of interest, limiting his exposure to a disclosed NSE holding.

STT Hikes Stump D-St | No ‘Offset’ From Capital Gains Tax & STT Hike Soured The Mood: Samir Arora 2026-02-02 09:01

M&M 5PAISA

Samir Arora, founder and fund manager at Helios Capital, joined the post‑budget discussion to explain why the recent rise in securities transaction tax (STT) is hurting market sentiment and liquidity. He argues that the equity market should be treated as an independent sector, not merely a by‑product of sector‑specific incentives, and that the government needs to acknowledge and offset the added cost—by cutting capital‑gains tax, reducing cash‑transaction STT, or offering other relief—especially when the market is already underperforming and investors are losing in foreign‑currency terms.

Arora stresses that high STT and other taxes increase the equity risk premium, discouraging foreign institutional investors (FIIs) and making Indian equities less attractive compared to global markets. He calls for verbal support and, if needed, concrete policy actions (e.g., temporary loan facilities, duty reductions) to sustain liquidity and confidence. While he remains long‑only and has continued buying selectively, he cautions that without supportive measures the market may stay weak, and he expects FIIs to stay cautious or sell.

In short, Arora wants the budget to pair the STT hike with compensatory tax relief or other incentives, to protect market liquidity, reduce the equity risk premium, and signal that the Indian stock market is a priority for economic growth.
