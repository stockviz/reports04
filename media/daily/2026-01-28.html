Valuation In India High On Absolute As Well As On A Relative Basis: Kotak Institutional Equities 2026-01-28 11:42

EMBDL ITC

  • Market outlook: Sanjeev Prasad says the Indian market has moved from a very negative stance in 2024‑25 to a more neutral one. Earnings are stabilising, with FY‑26 earnings growth expected around 8% and FY‑27 around 15%, driven by a modest recovery in several sectors.

  • Valuation concerns: Despite improving earnings, multiples remain high relative to the rest of Asia, especially in consumer stocks. This high valuation is the main risk and could trigger further corrections over the next 12‑18 months.

  • Key headwinds:

    • Elevated valuation levels and lack of globally‑recognised tech names limit foreign‑investor interest.
    • Domestic political uncertainty (upcoming elections) and slower adoption of AI in India compared with other Asian markets.
    • Potential permanent reset in multiples for many sectors, meaning earnings may rise while price multiples fall, leading to muted total‑return prospects over a 3‑5 year horizon.

  • Sector‑specific views:

    • ITC: Recent price correction after the tobacco‑tax hike makes the stock attractive; core tobacco earnings appear cheap (~15‑times earnings) and the business remains a strong cash generator.
    • Max Healthcare: High‑growth business with solid fundamentals; despite trading at ~40 times earnings, the company’s execution and pipeline justify the valuation.
    • Embassy Pharma & Lupin: Both have reached the end of their rate‑cutting cycles and face earnings‑growth constraints (pricing pressure, delayed product launches), prompting a reduction in exposure.

  • Overall expectation: The market is likely to experience a gradual correction in multiples while earnings improve modestly. Investors should be cautious, focusing on high‑quality names with resilient cash flows, and be prepared for a period of subdued returns despite a positive earnings backdrop.

India & EU Sign Landmark FTA | European Cars Can Become Cheaper By 7-8% Due To FTA: BNP Paribas

ASHOKLEY M&M MARUTI

  • India and the EU have formalised a free‑trade agreement; it will be signed later this year after legal vetting and ratification.
  • Under the deal, cars made in India will get duty‑free access to the EU, while EU‑built cars (CBU) imported into India will see tariffs cut from the current 70‑110 % to about 10 % over 5‑10 years – a potential price drop of ~20 % in the long run, with an interim reduction to 35‑40 % of the tariff.
  • Most European models sold in India are assembled locally as CKD units; for these the tariff cut translates to an estimated 7 % price reduction.
  • The analyst expects only partial pass‑through of the tariff savings to consumers because of currency volatility and profitability concerns; the main benefit will be higher margins and viability for European OEMs, not a major competitive shock to Indian manufacturers.
  • The premium‑segment market may expand with more European models, but mass‑market Indian OEMs (Maruti, Mahindra & Mahindra, etc.) are unlikely to face material headwinds.
  • For auto parts, India already enjoys low‑cost production and modest EU import duties, so the FTA adds little incremental advantage; any export‑revenue uplift could be offset by the lower valuation multiples attached to EU exposure.
  • The analyst remains cautious on the broader auto index: commodity price spikes could shave ~100 bps off margins in the near term, though commercial‑vehicle players can pass through cost pressures more easily.
  • Among stocks, Ashok Leyland stands out with the best risk‑reward profile, benefiting from an up‑cycle in commercial vehicles, margin expansion, and strong free cash flow; other auto stocks show limited upside (e.g., M&M’s target price implies modest upside).
