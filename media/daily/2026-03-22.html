India Lifts Domestic Airfare Cap Amid New Seat‑Selection Rules

INDIGO

The Indian Ministry of Civil Aviation has withdrawn the temporary domestic fare caps imposed since December, citing stabilized market conditions, while urging airlines to maintain reasonable, transparent pricing and reserving the right to re‑impose controls if needed. Concurrently, a new directive mandates that at least 60 % of seats on any flight be free of extra‑charge seat‑selection fees, aiming to protect consumers. Airlines, represented by the Federation of Indian Airlines, oppose the rule, arguing it will erode ancillary revenue and force higher ticket prices to cover costs. Additional government initiatives include seating passengers on the same PNR together, pet‑friendly policies, and simplified transport of musical and sports equipment. The government balances consumer protection with industry flexibility, monitoring fare trends to prevent exorbitant hikes

Weight‑Loss Drugs: Benefits, Risks, and the Need for Careful Use

RCOM

Vedanta challenges Adani’s JAL takeover at NCLAT

ADANIENPP1 JPASSOCIAT VEDL

Vedanta Ltd has appealed to the National Company Law Appellate Tribunal against the NCLT’s approval of Adani Enterprises’ ₹14,543 cr bid for bankrupt Jaiprakash Associates. Vedanta argues the lenders violated IBC principles by favoring a lower‑value plan, citing its higher ₹12,505.85 cr NPV offer and a revised bid with more upfront cash. The Committee of Creditors defended the decision, saying the process complied with IBC rules, considered factors beyond headline value, and rejected Vedanta’s late revised offer. The NCLT upheld the lenders’ choice, noting no procedural breach and that the CoC’s commercial wisdom is final. The NCLAT will now review whether any clear legal violations occurred, with the outcome likely hinging on procedural compliance rather than bid value. The resolution gives Adani a large land bank and real‑estate assets in the NCR, while Vedanta seeks to overturn what it calls a commercial conspiracy

Rupee Slides to Record Lows Amid Oil Surge and Capital Outflows

UNIONBANK NAM-INDIA GANGESSECU

The Indian rupee hit a new record low of 93.7 per dollar, driven by soaring oil prices, a strong US dollar, and massive foreign fund outflows exceeding $8 billion in March. The RBI has intervened heavily, selling over $15 billion in March and holding a $100 billion net short forward position, while balancing liquidity concerns. Analysts attribute the rapid depreciation to a “perfect storm” of global oil supply shocks, West Asia tensions, and domestic pressures, warning that continued oil price highs could push the rupee toward 94–95. They debate RBI’s tools—FX swaps, FCNR/NRI deposits, and forward market actions—and whether a managed depreciation might benefit exporters versus relentless defense. Overall, the consensus is that unless oil prices fall and capital flows normalize, the rupee’s decline is likely to persist, though a rebound could follow a resolution of the oil crisis and improved sentiment

Adobe’s AI‑Era Crossroads: From Subscription Success to Valuation Turbulence

COFFEEDAY

Shantanu Narayen transformed Adobe from a boxed‑software seller into a subscription giant, growing revenue from $3 bn (2007) to $24 bn (2025) and lifting market value from $24 bn to a peak of $322 bn. The shift to Creative Cloud and a series of acquisitions (Omniture, Marketo, Magento) built a broad Digital Experience platform and expanded staff to ~31 k. However, from late‑2023 to early‑2026 the stock fell from $272 bn to $101 bn after regulators blocked the $20‑30 bn Figma deal and investors worried generative AI could erode demand. Adobe now charges higher‑tier subscriptions for AI features, faces potential seat‑usage decline, and relies on Azure/AWS for AI compute while keeping capital spend low (0.8% of revenue vs. rivals’ higher rates). The next CEO must safeguard the subscription moat, navigate AI competition, and decide whether to deepen infrastructure ownership

State Street and Voya Shift to Mortgage Bonds Amid Rising Energy Prices and Corporate Credit Risks

GSPL BFINVEST

As oil prices surge and inflation worries grow, major managers like State Street and Voya are favoring mortgage‑backed securities (MBS) and other securitized debt over high‑grade corporate bonds. The move is backed by recent Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac purchases, tighter MBS spreads, and a perception that MBS are cheaper relative to corporate credit, which has widened due to higher borrowing costs and profit pressures. While the strategy offers diversification and lower credit risk, analysts warn that a de‑escalation of geopolitical tensions or policy changes could quickly narrow spreads, and that both MBS and corporates could be impacted by persistent energy‑driven inflation
