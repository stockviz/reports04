Skoda Octavia RS 4th‑Gen Review – Sharp Styling, More Power, Practical Performance

BMW F450GS Trophy Review – Lightweight Adventure Bike Overview

BMWVENTLTD TVSELECT

The BMW F450GS Trophy, built in collaboration with TVS in India, is positioned as an accessible, adventure‑oriented motorcycle featuring a 420 cc parallel‑twin engine (≈47 PS, 43 Nm), modern GS styling, and a host of electronics (cornering ABS, traction control, ride modes, IMU, TFT display). Three trims are offered: a base model (~₹4.7 L) with standard KYB suspension and Brembo brakes, a mid‑spec adding enduro accessories, and the top “Trophy” variant (~₹5.3 L) with adjustable KYB suspension, aluminum engine guard, tinted windshield, and a first‑in‑segment ERC (automatic centrifugal clutch) system that eases low‑speed riding while retaining manual gear shifts. Ergonomics are upright with 845 mm seat height, decent ground clearance, and a narrow seat; the 6.5‑inch TFT display is praised, though pillion comfort is limited. The bike’s chassis feels balanced, suspension responsive, and overall dynamics agile for both on‑road and off‑road use, though power delivery can be abrupt in ERC mode and the ride may lack buttery smoothness for some. At 178 kg, it remains lightweight for a GS, but ownership costs (service, parts) are high. Overall, the F450GS Trophy offers a capable, feature‑rich entry into the GS family at a relatively lower price point, with the caveat of premium maintenance expenses

India’s Growth Holds Up, but Inflation and Current‑Account Pressures Mount

HINDUNILVR NESTLEIND

Despite the Iran‑War‑driven surge in crude to $85‑$95 a barrel, India’s economy showed resilience in Q1 2024‑25, with GDP around 7.4% YoY and surprisingly strong FMCG (≈6.3% YoY) and auto sales (30%+ growth). Rural demand remains robust, while urban FMCG growth slowed to ~2%. Leading indicators—diesel sales, non‑oil exports, and GSD collections—are already weakening, suggesting a potential slowdown. Economists project FY27 growth to fall to 6.5‑6.8%, with inflation likely edging above the 5% target (adding 30‑40 bp for each $10 rise in crude) and CPI‑impact of ~5 bp per rupee of petrol price hike. The current‑account deficit is expected at ~2% of GDP (≈₹80‑90 bn), translating to a BOP gap of $25‑$35 bn, putting continued downward pressure on the rupee. Risks include higher fuel prices, El Niño‑related crop stress, tighter financial conditions, and limited capital inflows until geopolitical tensions ease
