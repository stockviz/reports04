Hum Do, Humare Do: Indian Couples Shift to Paired Low‑Speed E‑Cycles Amid Fuel‑Price Surge

TVSELECT HEROMOTOCO HONDAPOWER BAJAJ-AUTO OLAELEC

Rising petrol prices are prompting urban Indian households, especially working couples, to replace a single electric scooter with two low‑speed e‑cycles for daily commutes, metro links and short trips. These 250 W, sub‑25 km/h cycles cost ₹25‑45 k, require no registration, licence or insurance, and deliver operating costs of about ₹0.07 /km versus ₹2.50 /km for petrol bikes. The “Hum Do, Humare Do” model serves as a pragmatic second‑mobility asset, reducing fuel spend, easing congestion, and appealing to gig workers and delivery fleets. Manufacturers are now adding IoT tracking, smart locks, and connected features to address theft and parking concerns, signaling the next phase of growth beyond affordability

Petrol & Diesel Price Hikes in India: OMC Losses, Global Oil Shock & Policy Impact

IOC BPCL

In just 11 days, India’s fuel prices rose by a cumulative ₹7.5 per litre across four hikes (₹3 on May 15, ₹0.99 on May 19, ₹0.90 on May 23, and ₹2.70 on May 29). The hikes aim to offset massive losses faced by the state‑run Oil Marketing Companies (OMCs), which saw daily deficits drop from about ₹1,000 crore to ₹670 crore after the increases. Each ₹0.5 rise per litre adds roughly 7‑11 % to OMC margins (IOCL 7 %, BPCL 8 %, HPCL 11 %). With India consuming ~463 million litres of petrol and diesel daily—85 % imported—the price hikes are a response to soaring global oil prices (up $68‑$213 since the West Asia crisis) and a weakening rupee, aiming to curb outflows while the OMCs retain ~90 % market share. The cumulative hikes have recovered about 44 % of the earlier losses, leaving OMCs still losing roughly half of their previous daily deficit

The Conbini Revolution: How Ignoring Critics Built Japan’s Convenience Store Empire

DELTACORP TCNSBRANDS EFCIL

The article highlights Toshifumi Suzuki, the “father of Japan’s konbini” (convenience stores), who revolutionized the 7-Eleven model in Japan despite widespread skepticism. Critics initially dismissed his idea as incompatible with Japan’s small-scale retail culture, but Suzuki’s focus on quality, accessibility, and innovation—like introducing onigiri (rice balls) and financial services—turned konbini into a cultural staple. His success defied conventional wisdom, proving that ignoring opposition can lead to groundbreaking change.

Suzuki’s ousting in 2016 by activist investor Dan Loeb, framed as a corporate governance victory, now seems flawed: Seven & I Holdings has struggled since, with stagnant shares despite market growth. The article argues that short-term financial pressures often overlook long-term value, questioning whether dismantling visionary leadership always benefits companies. Suzuki’s legacy—prioritizing customer needs over consensus—suggests that true innovation requires defying the status quo, even when critics call it reckless. The konbini phenomenon remains a testament to his unconventional approach
