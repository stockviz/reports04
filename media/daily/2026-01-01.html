Why Retirement Planning Remains India’s Biggest Financial Blind Spot | Your Money Matters 2026-01-01 18:05

NAM-INDIA

Retirement has become the top financial priority for Indian households in 2025, yet only 37% have an actual retirement plan, down from previous years. This highlights a growing gap between intent and execution. Key reasons include rising lifestyle and education expenses, difficulty in identifying with one’s future self, behavioral biases, and the influence of short-term market trends and social media. There’s a shift away from viewing insurance as an investment tool toward recognizing the need for dedicated wealth and retirement planning. Challenges also include low financial literacy, limited access to suitable products, and a lack of trust in long-term advisory relationships. Traditional savings instruments like fixed deposits and gold remain popular, hindering more effective retirement planning. While early retirement trends (FIRE movement) were popular, many now expect to work longer due to financial realities. Generating alternative income through skills or side businesses is gaining awareness and importance, not just for financial security but also mental well-being in retirement. However, despite increasing intent, actual adoption has slightly declined, indicating a need for better financial education, product accessibility, and advisor engagement.

Tobacco & Cigarette Stocks Tumble As New Taxation Structure Comes Into Effect From February 1 2026-01-01 13:27

ITC GODFRYPHLP

Tobacco stocks, including ITC and Godfrey Phillips, plunged sharply—with ITC down nearly 8% and Godfrey Phillips over 11%—following a government notification introducing a new taxation structure effective February 1, 2026. The new structure imposes a 40% GST on cigarettes (up from 28%) and adds a fresh excise duty of ₹2.10 to ₹8.50 per stick (₹2,100 to ₹8,500 per thousand sticks), alongside existing taxes like the National Calamity Contingent Duty (NCCD). Contrary to earlier assumptions that the 40% GST would subsume other levies, the new excise duties are in addition to it, significantly increasing the tax burden.

Previously, total taxes accounted for 50–55% of MRP; now that could rise to 65–80%. This may force cigarette companies to raise prices by 18–35%, impacting volumes due to potential shifts to illegal or cheaper alternatives. If companies avoid price hikes, EBIT could fall by over 40% given the high-margin nature of the business. Historically, tax increases led to partial price pass-through and volume declines, but the current scale is unprecedented.

The market is reacting negatively, pricing in worst-case scenarios. ITC hit its lowest level since March 2023, trading around ₹370, with heavy volumes—over 10 crore shares traded early. Godfrey Phillips faced even sharper losses due to its premium international brands like Marlboro being more vulnerable to smuggling. Investor concerns center on lack of clarity on final tax incidence, margin pressure, volume risks, and potential growth in the illegal cigarette trade, all affecting profitability and government revenue long-term.
