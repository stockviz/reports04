Vedanta Shifts to Flexible Dividend Policy Amid Demerger

HINDZINC VEDL

Vedanta Ltd is moving from a fixed commitment of distributing at least 30% of profits as dividends to a discretionary, board‑driven framework, allowing payouts of 30% or any other amount deemed appropriate. The change coincides with the company’s split into five listed entities, each of which will adopt its own policy. While the move offers greater flexibility for capital allocation and the ability to pass on dividends from subsidiary Hindustan Zinc, analysts warn it introduces uncertainty for investors who relied on predictable yields. The company argues the shift does not signal lower payouts, citing strong total shareholder returns, but the loss of a guaranteed minimum dividend may alter its appeal as a yield‑focused stock

AI vs Outsourcing: India’s IT Services at a Crossroads

TCS FRACTAL WIPRO 5PAISA RUBICON

The CNBC‑TV18 townhall examined the pivotal role of India’s IT/BPM sector—now generating $250 bn in revenue and employing millions—against the backdrop of generative AI’s potential to automate up to 40 % of software work. Analysts Phil and Dan argued that, unlike past tech cycles, AI represents a structural shock that compresses delivery timelines and challenges the traditional labor‑arbitrage model. They highlighted the need for Indian firms to pivot from selling effort to selling outcomes, deepen C‑suite relationships beyond CIOs, and become cloud‑first, AI‑enabled consultants. While the disruption is profound, both see a net opportunity: the addressable market could expand as services evolve into “services as software,” with firms that invest in domain expertise, AI platforms, and transformational consulting positioned to thrive. They rated the impact around 7–9 out of 10, viewing it as a major but navigable shift

RBI’s Shadow‑Bank Rule Change Could Force Tata Sons to List

RUBICON BCG NSIL BBL

The Reserve Bank of India’s revised definition of shadow lenders now treats entities that receive funds from group affiliates as having indirect public‑fund access, meaning Tata Sons may have to comply with mandatory public‑listing requirements. The change, effective July 1, targets non‑bank lenders with both direct and indirect public‑fund exposure. Analysts note that several Tata Group companies hold a combined 12 % stake in Tata Sons, potentially triggering the listing rule, which the group missed for a September 2025 deadline. Tata Sons had earlier sought to surrender its core investment company registration to stay private, preserving control for the Tata Trusts and avoiding disclosure obligations. Minority shareholder Shapoorji Pallonji Mistry has called for a listing, framing it as value‑unlocking rather than merely regulatory compliance. The RBI is tightening oversight across the ecosystem, suggesting further regulatory clarity ahead
